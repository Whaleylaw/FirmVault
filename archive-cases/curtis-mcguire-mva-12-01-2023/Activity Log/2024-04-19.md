Kentucky State Police Public Records
A document has been released for record request #24-2151 along with the
following message:

Following a diligent search, the Kentucky State Police ("KSP") has
determined you are seeking records pertaining to Case No.16-23-0480.
Because KSP is actively investigating this incident, and the Commonwealth’s
Attorney has not declined to prosecute nor concluded prosecution regarding
it, KSP is partially denying your, request pursuant to KRS 17.150(2),
incorporated into the Open Records Act by KRS 61.878(1)(l), and
61.878(1)(h).

Pursuant to KRS 17.150(2), “[i]ntelligence and investigative reports
maintained by criminal justice agencies are subject to public inspection if
prosecution is complete or a determination not to prosecute has been made.”
KRS 61.878(1)(l) authorizes public agencies to withhold “public records or
information the disclosure of which is prohibited or restricted or
otherwise made confidential by enactment of the General Assembly.” Thus,
KRS 17.150 is incorporated into the Open Records Act by KRS 61.878(1)(l).
In 20-ORD-090, the Attorney General’s Office ruled that “the completion of
a prosecution or a decision not to prosecute is a condition precedent to
public inspection” of records within the scope of KRS 17.150(2). Further,
the Attorney General’s Office has analyzed the underlying purpose of KRS
17.150(2) and its “companion statute,” KRS 61.878(1)(h), observing that
“[i]nvestigative reports are nearly always withheld from public inspection
to protect sources of information and techniques of investigations and also
to prevent premature disclosure of the contents to the targets of
investigations, which could thwart law enforcement efforts.” 19-ORD-166
(citing OAG 83-123). Finally, the term “investigative report” is “broad
enough to extend to laboratory, forensic, and other reports generated in
the course of an investigation.” *Id*. (citing 05-ORD-246). For example,
audio and video recordings, including “dash-cam video recordings,” fall
within the parameters of KRS 17.150(2). *See *07-ORD-095 (quoting
04-ORD-234); 20-ORD-104.



The Attorney General has also evaluated KRS 17.150 in relation to
investigative materials associated with an ongoing matter and held this
provision “merely requires the agency to provide a specific reason for
withholding the records. KRS 17.150, therefore, makes the records at issue
exempt from disclosure until there is no prospective law enforcement
action, so long as the agency specifies what that action is or could be.”
19-ORD-166 (citing 14-ORD-154); *see also City of Ft. Thomas v. Cincinnati
Enquirer*, 506 S.W.3d 842 (Ky. 2013)); 20-ORD-105; 14-ORD-223 (holding that
“current status of a criminal prosecution could be a factor affecting the
threshold for a showing of harm to the agency under KRS 61.878(1)(h) as
interpreted in *City of Fort Thomas*” and noting the enforcement action was
“at a very early stage and the effect of public release of the relevant
jail records upon the fairness of a future trial is” unpredictable);
16-ORD-162. Based upon the foregoing, KSP has justified its partial denial
of your     request, pursuant to KRS 17.150(2), incorporated into the Open
Records Act by KRS 61.878(1)(l).



However, KSP is partially complying with your request by attaching hereto a
copy of the initial Kentucky Incident-Based Reporting System (“KYIBRS”)
Report concerning this investigation, excluding the narrative portion (you
may receive fewer pages than the number indicated on the report). This
portion of the initial KYIBRS Report is subject to disclosure regardless of
the status of the investigation, pursuant to 09-ORD-205. KSP has redacted
certain personal information from the attached records pursuant to KRS
61.878(1)(a), which exempts “information of a personal nature where the
public disclosure thereof would constitute a clearly unwarranted invasion
of personal privacy.” *Id.* Here, such personal information consists of
social security numbers, home addresses, personal telephone numbers,
driver’s license numbers, dates of birth, etc., the disclosure of which may
leave persons at risk for identity theft and which does not serve the
purpose of the Open Records Act. *See Zink v. Commonwealth*, 902 S.W.2d 825
(Ky. App. 1994); *Kentucky New Era v. City of Hopkinsville,* 415 S.W.3d 76
(Ky. 2013) (affirming the “categorical redaction” of such information
relating to private individuals).

21-ORD-034 reaffirms the Open Records Act does not require public agencies
to answer questions or provide information regarding your specific request.
If you wish to appeal this decision, you may do so by filing a complaint
with the Office of the Attorney General, Open Records/Open Meetings
Division, The Capitol, 700 Capital Avenue, Suite 118, Frankfort, KY 40601,
pursuant to KRS 61.880(2), or by filing an original action in Franklin
Circuit Court under KRS 61.882. If you first appeal to the Attorney General
but are dissatisfied with the Attorney General’s decision, you may further
appeal to Circuit Court pursuant to KRS 61.880(5).
