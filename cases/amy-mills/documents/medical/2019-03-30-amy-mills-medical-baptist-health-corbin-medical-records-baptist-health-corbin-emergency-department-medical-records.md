Occipital Neuralgia

Occipital neuralgia is a type of headache that causes episodes of very bad pain in the back of your head. Pain from occipital neuralgia may spread (radiate) to other parts of your head. The pain is usually brief and often goes away after you rest and relax.

These headaches may be caused by irritation of the nerves that leave your spinal cord high up in your neck, just below the base of your skull (occipital nerves). Your occipital nerves transmit sensations from the back of your head, the top of your head, and the areas behind your ears.

What are the causes?

Occipital neuralgia can occur without any known cause (primary headache syndrome). In other cases, occipital neuralgia is caused by pressure on or irritation of one of the two occipital nerves. Causes of occipital nerve compression or irritation include:

Wear and tear of the vertebrae in the neck (osteoarthritis). Neck injury.

Disease of the disks that separate the vertebrae.

Tumors.

Gout.

Infections.

Diabetes.

Swollen blood vessels that put pressure on the occipital nerves. Muscle spasm in the neck.

What are the signs or symptoms?

Pain is the main symptom of occipital neuralgia. It usually starts in the back of the head but may also be felt in other areas supplied by the occipital nerves. Pain is usually on one side but may be on both sides. You may have:

¢ Brief episodes of very bad pain that is burning, stabbing, shocking, or shooting. e Pain behind the eye.

Pain triggered by neck movement or hair brushing.

Scalp tenderness.

e Aching in the back of the head between episodes of very bad pain.

How is this diagnosed?

Your health care provider may diagnose occipital neuralgia based on your symptoms and a physical exam. During the exam, the health care provider may push on areas supplied by the occipital nerves to see if they are painful. Some tests may also be done to help in making the diagnosis. These may include:

* Imaging studies of the upper spinal cord, such as an MRI or CT scan. These may show compression or spinal cord abnormalities.
