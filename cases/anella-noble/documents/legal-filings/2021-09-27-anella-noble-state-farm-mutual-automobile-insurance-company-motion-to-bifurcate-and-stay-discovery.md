I. INTRODUCTION 
This action arises out of a motor vehicle accident which occurred on January 3, 2021 in 
which Plaintiff filed suit against Virginia Sewell and an Unknown Driver for injuries alleged to 
have been sustained in the accident. Plaintiff also contends that Defendant, State Farm Mutual 
Automobile Insurance Company (“State Farm”), acted in bad faith by failing to engage in good 
faith settlement negotiations to resolve the claim. 
State Farm requests an Order from the Court bifurcating trial and staying discovery on the 
bad faith claim until the underlying claim is resolved by verdict or otherwise. 
II. ARGUMENT 
A. Kentucky Law Requires Bifurcation of Trial. 
The Court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will 
be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any claim. CR 42.02. 
Kentucky courts have uniformly held that insurance companies have an absolute right to

bifurcation. Although the current Complaint contains allegations of common law bad faith and 
violations of Kentucky’s Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act, the basis of the allegations is 
irrelevant when analyzing whether the claim should be bifurcated. Smith v. Allstate Ins. Co., 403 
F.3d 401, 407 (6th Cir. 2005). 
In Wittmer v. Jones, 864 S.W.2d 885, 891 (Ky. 1993), the Kentucky Supreme Court 
recognized and ordered that bad faith claims be tried separately from underlying tort actions. The 
Court’s decision to bifurcate in Wittmer was expressly adopted from Justice Leibson’s dissent in 
Federal Kemper Ins. Co. v. Hornback, 711 S.W.2d 844, 846-49 (Ky. 1986), a first-party breach of 
contract case. Specifically, the Court in Wittmer held: 
Turning once more to the Dissenting Opinion in Federal Kemper
that was incorporated by reference into the Majority Opinion in 
Curry [v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co., 784 S.W.2d 176, 178 (Ky. 
1989)]: “A bifurcated procedure was the proper way to try the 
present case.” 
Id. at 891 (citations omitted). 
Justice Leibson’s dissent in Federal Kemper is the foundation of bad faith law in Kentucky. 
711 S.W.2d at 846-49. In Federal Kemper, the insureds sought recovery for fire damage to their 
home under a fire insurance policy issued by Federal Kemper Insurance Company. Id. at 844. 
The insureds also alleged that the insurer engaged in bad faith in the handling of their claim. Id. 
at 844-45. Justice Leibson recognized that the underlying contract claim should be bifurcated from 
the bad faith claim: 
A bifurcated procedure was the proper way to try the present case. 
This procedure better protected the rights of the insurance 
company/movant because it kept out of the contract phase evidence 
which was relevant to the issue of bad faith but unnecessary and 
possibly prejudicial to the insurance company in the trial of the 
preliminary question of liability under the insurance contract.

Id. at 849; see also Motorist Mutual Ins. Co. v. Glass, 996 S.W.2d 437 (Ky. 1999) (insured’s firstparty claim for UIM benefits should have been bifurcated from bad faith claim); Mike Breen, Bad 
Faith in Kentucky: A Primer at p. 77 (“If the bad faith case has been filed in conjunction with the 
underlying claim, counsel should immediately move for a bifurcation of proceedings. The 
company has an absolute right to do so under Federal Kemper and Wittmer. Both cases say that 
bifurcation protects the carrier, an exercise in understatement.”). 
The rationale for bifurcation set forth in these decisions applies equally to the instant case. 
Plaintiff’s action for bad faith against State Farm will involve the presentation of evidence and 
issues which are unnecessary to the determination of Plaintiff’s personal injury claim. For 
example, to prevail on a claim of bad faith, Plaintiff must prove that State Farm “knew there was 
no reasonable basis for denying the claim or acted with reckless disregard for whether such a basis 
existed.” Wittmer, 864 S.W.2d at 890. This means that Plaintiff must present “sufficient evidence 
of intentional misconduct or reckless disregard of the rights of an insured . . . to warrant submitting 
the right to award punitive damages to the jury.” Id. Evidence to establish these issues bear no 
relevance to the personal injury claim and would only serve to prejudice Virginia Sewell and the 
Unknown Driver in the defense of the personal injury claim. For these reasons, and as more fully 
stated above, Plaintiff’s personal injury claim must be bifurcated for purposes of trial from the bad 
faith claim. 
B. Discovery on the Bad Faith Claim Must be Stayed until the Underlying Action 
is Resolved. 
For good cause shown, a party may move the court for a protective order that discovery 
may be had only on specified terms and conditions. CR 26.03(1). Although the Wittmer Court 
did not specifically address the issue of discovery, its opinion supports postponement of discovery 
as does other Kentucky case law.

The addition of a bad faith claim to a liability action should not expand the reasonable 
bounds of discovery. For example, here Plaintiff may request the production of State Farm’s claim 
file, which would address the defenses being provided to Virginia Sewell and is typically 
considered work product. Production of such information would be prejudicial to Ms. Sewell as 
counsel for Plaintiff would now have information relative to the underlying action including any 
communications provided by counsel for Ms. Sewell. 
In Morrow v. Brown, Todd & Heyburn, the Kentucky Supreme Court recognized that, in 
an action against an insurer for bad faith, “courts have permitted discovery of opinion work product 
where the mental impressions are at issue in the case and the need for the material is compelling.” 
957 S.W.2d 722, 725 (Ky. 1997). As recognized by this decision, however, the discoverability of 
work product in bad faith cases is limited to those actions where the underlying liability suit has 
been resolved: 
We are of the view that the opinion work product sought to be 
discovered must be directed to the pivotal issue in the subsequent 
litigation and the need for the material must be compelling. 
. . . 
While an attorney’s private thoughts are most certainly deserving 
of special protection, concern for privacy must give way when the 
activities of counsel are directly at issue in subsequent litigation. 
Id. at 726 (emphasis added). 
This sound principle was further recognized in Terrell v. Western Cas. & Surety Co., 427 
S.W.2d 825 (Ky. 1968). In Terrell, a bad faith failure-to-settle case, the Court allowed discovery 
of all documents and materials relating to any negotiations or offers of settlement after the 
underlying suit had been resolved: 
These matters could not be considered as having been produced in 
preparation or in anticipation of any litigation that is now or may

hereafter be pending. They concerned another suit or suits 
which have been terminated. 
Id. at 828 (emphasis added). 
Moreover, the great weight of authority in other jurisdictions holds that the proper 
procedure is to bifurcate and stay discovery on the bad faith claim until such time as the underlying 
action is resolved. See, e.g., In re Bergeson, 112 F.R.D. 692 (D. Mont. 1986); State ex. rel. State 
Farm Fire & Casualty Co. v. Madden, 451 S.E.2d 721 (W. Va. 1994); Bartlett v. John Hancock 
Mut. Life Ins. Co., 538 A.2d 997 (R.I. 1988); Maryland American General Ins. Co. v. Blackmon, 
639 S.W.2d 455 (Tex. 1982). 
There are a number of reasons for postponing discovery on the bad faith claim. One of the 
most important reasons is the prejudice to State Farm and its insured in defending the personal 
injury action if the insurer is required to produce its claim file or work product. As the West 
Virginia Supreme Court recognized in Madden, permitting a plaintiff access to the insurer’s claim 
file can have a highly prejudicial effect on the insured’s ability to defend the tort claim for personal 
injuries. In granting bifurcation, the Madden court stated: 
Allowing the additional claims against State Farm to proceed would 
necessarily entail discovery of State Farm’s files that would 
undoubtedly prejudice [the insured’s] ability to defend itself against 
[the plaintiff’s] original negligence claim. 
451 S.E.2d at 726. The risk of privileged material being disclosed which might jeopardize the 
defense of the underlying claim is simply too significant to ignore: 
For the claims file to be discoverable, the underlying claim must first 
be resolved, either by settlement or litigation. Otherwise, privileged 
material may be disclosed which would jeopardize the insurance 
company’s defense. 
In re Bergeson, 112 F.R.D at 697. State Farm should not suffer the potential prejudicial effects of 
disclosure of privileged material simply because Plaintiff has asserted a bad faith claim.

In Riggs v. Schroering, the Kentucky Supreme Court clarified that discovery in a bad faith 
case is a two-way street. 822 S.W.2d 414 (Ky. 1991) Not only is the insurer required to produce 
items such as its claim file relevant to the case, but the insurer is permitted to depose Plaintiff’s 
counsel and review materials within Plaintiff’s attorney’s file relevant to negotiations and 
settlement of the underlying claim. Thus, Plaintiff could also be prejudiced by allowing discovery 
to proceed before trial of the personal injury negligence claim. 
Other reasons for postponing discovery include judicial economy and preservation of party 
resources. For example, in Kentucky, to proceed with a bad faith claim, a plaintiff must be able 
to prove a prima facie case. The first element in a prima facie case is proof that the insurer had a 
duty to pay the value of the claim as alleged by the plaintiff. Wittmer, 864 S.W.2d at 890. Insurers 
have not acted in bad faith if the value of the claim is fairly “debatable on the law or the facts.” 
Id.; Curry, 784 S.W.2d at 178. Accordingly, if Virginial Sewell and her insurance carrier prevail 
at trial, the resolution of Plaintiff’s underlying case may eliminate the necessity of trying the bad 
faith claim. To require discovery on the bad faith allegations under these circumstances results in 
needless litigation expenses and use of valuable judicial resources. 
Finally, as recognized in Bartlett, allowing discovery to proceed on a bad faith claim could 
result in the filing of bad faith claims with every tort or breach of contract claim for the sole 
purpose of acquiring access to the claim file. 538 A.2d at 1001-02; see also Mike Breen, Bad Faith 
in Kentucky: A Primer, at p. 77 (“filing a bad faith case may often be an unimaginative plaintiff 
tactic to get a copy of the claims file . . . .”). The mere allegation of bad faith in claims handling 
should not be sufficient to permit plaintiff discovery that she would otherwise be unable to obtain. 
Travelers Indem. Co. v. Allied Signal, Inc., 124 F.R.D. 101 (D. Md. 1989). For this reason, and 
those outlined above, State Farm respectfully requests that its Motion be sustained.
