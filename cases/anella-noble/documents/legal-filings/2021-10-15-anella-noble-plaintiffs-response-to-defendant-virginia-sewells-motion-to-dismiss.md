PLAINTIFF’S RESPONSE TO DEFENDANT VIRGINIA SEWELL’S
MOTION TO DISMISS
Comes the Plaintiff Anella Noble, by counsel, and files this response to Defendant 
Virginia Sewell’s motion to dismiss. The motion should be denied as moot given the motion for 
leave to amend the complaint filed contemporaneously with this response.
BACKGROUND
This case arises from an automobile wreck on January 3, 2021. On that date, Ms. Noble 
was a rear-seat passenger in a vehicle that was hit by another vehicle, a Dodge Journey. The 
negligent driver of the Dodge Journey immediately fled on foot. The investigating LMPD officer 
attempted to locate the negligent driver by contacting the owner of the vehicle, Virginia Sewell, 
but Ms. Sewell provided conflicting and false information to the officer. As noted in the police 
report:
Unit 1 operator [unknown negligent driver] was not on seen when police arrived. 
Officers located a photo of suspected male on social media of a male described as 
the subject that fled. The subject was identified … as an individual known to Unit 
1 vehicle owner [Virginia Sewell]. Officers found out that [Sewell] then made a 
stolen vehicle report for her vehicle while officers were on scene working the 
accident. Officers called [Sewell] and she stated she spoke with her boyfriend 
prior to filing the report for the stolen vehicle.

2
There was no damage to the vehicle’s ignition and it being a push start would not 
allow the vehicle to start without a key. Officers spoke with [Sewell] and she 
stated she only has one set of keys. Then she changed her statement when 
confronted about the ignition. She then stated she had a spare but has not been 
able to locate that key for a while.1 Given the circumstances, Plaintiff filed suit less than eight months later, on August 30, 
2021. Plaintiff alleged “negligence” against Ms. Sewell and the unknown driver, a separate count 
of fraudulent misrepresentation against Ms. Sewell due to false representations and concealment, 
and an Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act (UCSPA) claim against the insurer of Ms. 
Sewell’s vehicle, State Farm.2 On September 23, 2021, Defendant Sewell sufficiently understood the allegations in the 
Complaint to file an Answer.3 Nevertheless, Defendant Sewell filed a “motion to dismiss” 
pursuant to CR 12.02(f), claiming Ms. Noble failed to allege fraud “with particularity” under CR 
9.02. The motion lacks merit, but the motion should also be denied as moot given Plaintiff’s 
motion for leave to file an amended complaint which is filed contemporaneously with this 
response.
LEGAL ARGUMENT
Although styled a “motion to dismiss,” Defendant Sewell’s motion is more accurately 
titled a motion for judgment on the pleadings, because the pleadings are closed. CR 12.03 
provides: 
After the pleadings are closed but within such time as not to delay the trial, any 
party may move for judgment on the pleadings. If, on such motion, matters 
outside the pleading are presented to and not excluded by the court, the motion 
shall be treated as one for summary judgment and disposed of as provided for in 
Rule 56, and all parties shall be given reasonable opportunity to present all 
materials made pertinent to such a motion by Rule 56.

 Police report, attached as Exhibit 1.
2
 Complaint, ¶¶ 1-18.
3
 Answer, attached as Exhibit 2.

3
 A court should not grant a motion for judgment on the pleadings “unless it appears the 
pleading party would not be entitled to relief under any set of facts which could be proved....” 
Pari–Mutuel Clerks’ Union of Kentucky, Local 541, SEIU, AFL–CIO v. Kentucky Jockey Club, 551 S.W.2d 801, 803 (Ky. 1977) (emphasis added). Accordingly, “the pleadings should be 
liberally construed in the light most favorable to the plaintiff, all allegations being taken as true.” 
Morgan v. Bird, 289 S.W.3d 222, 226 (Ky. App. 2009); see also Archer v. Citizens Fidelity Bank 
& Trust Co., 365 S.W.2d 727 (Ky. 1962). Moreover, under the standard for judgment on the 
pleadings, the Court must consider the allegations in the answer, as well as the allegations of the 
complaint. Bennett v. Bennett, 477 S.W.2d 799, 801 (Ky. 1972). When considering a motion for 
judgment on the pleadings, the moving party admits not just the allegations in the complaint, but 
also admits the untruth of the allegations in the answer. Archer, 365 S.W.2d at 729 (moving 
party admits “not only the truth of all of his adversary’s well-pleaded allegations of fact and fair 
inferences therefrom, but also the untruth of all of his own allegations”).
I. CR 9.02 does not warrant dismissal.
CR 9.02 states that “all averments of fraud” are to be “stated with particularity,” but CR 
9.02 does not require a recitation of “each minute detail.” Scott v. Farmers State Bank, 410 
S.W.2d 717, 722 (Ky. 1966). 
The rule only warrants a recitation of “the time, the place, the substance of the false 
representations, the facts misrepresented, and the identification of what was obtained by the 
fraud.” CR 9.02. Or, in other words, “[c]ompliance with the particularity required by CR 9.02 
merely commands that the claimant set forth facts with sufficient particularity to apprise the 
defendant fairly of the charges against him or her.” Denzik v. Denzik, 197 S.W.3d 108, 110 (Ky. 
2006). Indeed, the word “fraud” need not even used in the Complaint to satisfy CR 9.02. And as 

4
the predecessor to our Supreme Court stated in eloquent terms, even in an era when technical 
pleading standards were still in vogue: “[a] bear well painted and drawn to life is yet the picture 
of a bear, although the painter may omit to write over it, ‘This is the bear.’” Kentucky Electric 
Co. v. Barret, 132 Ky. 717, 116 S.W. 1186, 1189 (1909)(internal citations omitted). 
Moreover—and most pertinent to the present case and the pending motion—“[a] pleading 
is sufficient if it identifies the circumstances constituting fraud so that the defendant can 
prepare an adequate answer from the allegations.” Denzik, 197 S.W.3d at 110 (emphasis 
added). The statement in Denzik requires that Defendant Sewell’s motion be denied because, as 
the record reflects, Defendant Sewell was sufficiently aware of the allegations in the Complaint 
to file an Answer.
Finally, even if Defendant Sewell had filed a CR 12.02 motion in lieu of an Answer, then 
dismissal would still not be the appropriate remedy. As the Supreme Court has stated, a motion 
for a more definite statement under CR 12.05 is the proper way to raise a pleading particularity 
matter. See McCollum v. Garrett, 880 S.W.2d 530, 533 (Ky. 1994)(“Our examination of the 
record reveals no misleading of or prejudice to McCollum. We note that he timely and properly 
filed an answer to the complaint and did not move pursuant to CR 12.05 for a more definite 
statement, an approved method for clarifying vague or ambiguous pleadings”); see also 
Denzik, supra (refusing to dismiss pursuant to CR 9.02 where defendant failed to invoke CR 
12.05 before filing an answer). 
Again, Defendant Sewell waived the right to seek a more definite statement by filing an 
Answer. Alvey v. Kern, 354 S.W.2d 516, 517 (Ky. 1962)(“The purpose of the motion for a more 
definite statement is to enable a party to frame a responsive pleading.”). 
II. Defendant’s motion should be denied as moot.

5
Even if CR 9.02 applied here, Defendant Sewell’s motion is rendered moot by Plaintiff’s 
motion to amend the complaint filed contemporaneously with this motion. Leave to amend a 
complaint “shall be freely given when justice so requires.” CR 15.01. Here, the wreck occurred 
only months ago. The Plaintiff is well within the two-year statute of limitations under the Motor 
Vehicle Reparations Act (MVRA). Accordingly, the motion to amend the complaint should be 
allowed. In addition to the existing allegations in the complaint, the amended complaint also sets 
forth a common-law negligent entrustment claim against Defendant Sewell. Negligent 
entrustment has been recognized in Kentucky since at least 1931. See Brady v. B&B Ice Co., 242 
Ky. 138, 45 S.W.2d 1051 (1931). The common-law theory of negligent entrustment is actionable 
against a vehicle owner who entrusts her vehicle to another whom she knows to be 
inexperienced, careless, or reckless, and the vehicle owner is liable for the natural and probable 
consequences of the entrustment. McGrew v. Stone, 998 S.W.2d 5 (Ky. 1999).
CONCLUSION
For all of the above reasons, the Court should deny Defendant Sewell’s motion.
