Heat Therapy
Heat therapy can help ease sore, stiff, injured, and tight muscles and joints.
Heat relaxes your muscles. This may help ease your pain and muscle spasms.
What are the risks?
If you have any of the following conditions, do not use heat therapy unless your doctor says it is okay. These conditions include:
• New bruises.
• Open wounds.
• Any of these in the area being treated:
—46 Healing wounds.
—46 Infected skin.
—46 Scarred skin.
• Problems with how blood moves through your body (circulation).
• Loss of feeling (numbness) in the part of your body that is being treated.
• Unusual swelling of the part of the body that is being treated.
• Blood clots.
• Diabetes.

How to use heat therapy
There are different kinds of heat therapy. These include:
• Moist heat pack.
• Hot water bottle.
• Electric heating pad.
• Heated gel pack.
• Heated wrap.
• Warm water bath.
Your doctor will tell you how to use heat therapy. In general, you should:
1. Place a towel between your skin and the heat source.
2. Leave the heat on for 20–30 minutes. Your skin may turn pink.
3. Take off the heat if your skin turns bright red. This is very important. If you cannot feel pain, heat, or cold, you have a greater
risk of getting burned.
Your doctor may also tell you to take a warm water bath. To do this:
1. Put a non-slip pad in the bathtub to prevent a fall.
2. Fill the bathtub with warm water.
3. Check the water temperature.
4. Soak in the water for 15–20 minutes, or as told by your doctor.
5. Be careful when you stand up after the bath. You may feel dizzy.
6. Pat yourself dry after the bath. Do not rub your skin to dry it.
General recommendations for heat therapy
• Be careful not to burn your skin when using heat therapy. High heat or using heat for a long time can cause burns.
• Do not sleep while using heat therapy. Only use heat therapy while you are awake.
• Check your skin during heat therapy.
• Do not use heat therapy if you have a new injury, especially if you have swelling on the injured area.

Don't Wait! Stop a Heart Attack Before It Starts
What is a heart attack?
A heart attack is damage or death to a part of the heart from severely decreased or lack of blood flow to the heart. Over time, arteries
can become narrow from the buildup of fat and cholesterol, which is called plaque. The plaque can rupture causing a blood clot to form.
When the blood clot forms, the artery can become severely narrowed or completely blocked, causing a heart attack.
• Heart attack is the leading cause of death in the United States.
• 85% of muscle damage occurs within the first 2 hours.
• Delay in the recognition of heart attack symptoms increases the chances of death.
