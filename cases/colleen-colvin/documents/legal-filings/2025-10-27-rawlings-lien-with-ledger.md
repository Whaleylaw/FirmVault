The health plan covering Colleen Colvin is an ERISA self-funded health plan. As such the
contributions of the employer and the plan members pay plan benefits. If the members were relieved of
the obligation to reimburse the health plan for the medical benefits paid on their behalf, the cost of those
benefits would have to be defrayed by other plan members via higher contributions to the plan.
According to our legal counsel, Federal Courts, including the United States Supreme Court, have
reiterated that ERISA imposes a fiduciary duty on plan sponsors to ensure that assets of plans are
preserved in order to satisfy present and future claims. Any reduction of the health plan's lien will place
a burden on other plan members and beneficiaries to meet the plan's financial responsibilities with their
contributions. Such reductions limit the funds available to pay the group's other medical claims.
Attached is a case from the Eleventh Circuit Court of Appeals in which an injured party was
ordered to reimburse the health plan completely for the medical expenses paid. Zurich Am. Ins. Co. V.
O'Hara, 604 F.3d 1232 (11th Cir. 2010). Our legal counsel has highlighted the portion of the opinion
that acknowledges the injured party would be in a better position if he were not forced to reimburse the
health plan. However, the Court reinforced that the health plan fiduciaries must "take impartial account
of the interest of all beneficiaries." Id. at 1237; Varity Corp. V. Howe, 516 U.S. 489, 116 S.Ct. 1065
(1996). The Court went on to explain that maintaining the financial viability of self-funded health plan
would be unfeasible without the enforcement of reimbursement and subrogation provisions.
