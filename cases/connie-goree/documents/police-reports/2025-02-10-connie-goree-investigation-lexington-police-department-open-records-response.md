Dear Aries Paul Peñaflor, 
In response to your request received on February 3, 2025 where you requested a copy of the 
abovementioned documentation: our office searched our available records with the information 
you provided and located incident 2024-167613. 
Collision reports are governed by KRS 189.635(6)(9) where only the individuals/organizations 
listed below are entitled to receive copies of collision reports:
1. Parties to the collision;
2. The insurers of any party who is the subject of the report;
3. The attorneys of the parties
4. Any party to litigation who includes with their request a copy of the first page of a District 
or Circuit Court clerk-stamped complaint naming all parties
5. The Department of Workplace Standards;
6. News-gathering organizations, solely for the purpose of publishing or broadcasting the 
news; and
7. Produced pursuant to a properly executed subpoena or court order.
Those individuals or businesses listed in 1 through 7 above are entitled to copies of collision reports 
and corresponding collision related materials. Since you have provided documentation proving

representation of an involved party, your office falls into one of the above stated categories. As

The telephone numbers from where the calls were made have been redacted from the provided 
documentation. This information is exempt from public inspection pursuant to KRS 65.752(4) and 
KRS 61.878(1)(a) which state:
KRS 65.752(4) states in areas where enhanced 911 service has been implemented, an employee 
of a PSAP shall not retrieve or disclose ALI information except in response to a 911 call or for the 
purpose of maintaining the ALI database, unless ordered by a court of competent jurisdiction. This 
section is incorporated into the Open Records Act pursuant to KRS 61.878(1)(a) in which 
releasing public records containing information of a personal nature where the public disclosure 
thereof would constitute a clearly unwarranted invasion of personal privacy. Furthermore, in 
[Bowling v. Brandenburg] it was clearly stated that the releasing of 911 tapes seeking police 
assistance would have a chilling effect on those who might seek assistance because they may 
become subject to potential retaliation, harassment, or public ridicule. 
In reference to your request for 911 tapes: the retention schedule for recordings of all telephone 
and radio communications to and from the police department is 60 days. This includes 911 calls 
as the recording software used automatically deletes all communications greater than 60 days. 
Therefore, should it have existed, the 911 audio you are requesting would no longer exist as the 
date of occurrence is past the 60 day retention schedule.
In reference to your request for the body worn camera footage: our office must charge 
commercially $22.50 per hour for requests seeking body worn camera videos and must receive a 
notarized affidavit regarding the attorney’s responsibility for the care and custody of the video 
with each request. As our office did receive an Attorney Affidavit it failed to recognize the 
responsibility for the care and custody of the video footage requested. Therefore, as our office has 
neither received the correct affidavit nor permission to process the body worn camera video(s) 
with the commercial rate of $22.50 per hour, your request for body worn camera video(s) has been 
denied pursuant to KRS 61.168 and KRS 61.169.
For informational purposes, the retention schedule approved by the Kentucky Department for 
Libraries and Archives states that the Lexington Police Department is only required to retain “nonevidentiary” body worn camera video recordings for 60 days. Since incident 2024-167613
occurred over 60 days ago and no criminal charges were placed, responding officers marked their 
corresponding videos as “non-evidentiary”. As such, these videos no longer exist and cannot be 
provided. 
Lastly, our records indicate no photographs were taken in regard to this collision.
