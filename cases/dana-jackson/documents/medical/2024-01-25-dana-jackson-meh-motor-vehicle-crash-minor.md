Cervical Sprain
A cervical sprain is a stretch or tear in one or more of the ligaments in the neck. Ligaments are the tissues that connect bones. Cervical
sprains can range from mild to severe. Severe cervical sprains can cause the spinal bones (vertebrae) in the neck to be unstable. This
can result in spinal cord damage and in serious nervous system problems.
The time that it takes for a cervical sprain to heal depends on the cause and extent of the injury. Most cervical sprains heal in 4–6
weeks.
What are the causes?
Cervical sprains may be caused by trauma, such as an injury from a motor vehicle accident, a fall, or a sudden forward and backward
whipping movement of the head and neck (whiplash injury). Mild cervical sprains may be caused by wear and tear over time.
What increases the risk?
The following factors may make you more likely to develop this condition:
• Participating in activities that have a high risk of trauma to the neck. These include contact sports, auto racing, gymnastics,
and diving.
• Taking risks when driving or riding in a motor vehicle.
• Osteoarthritis of the spine.
• Poor strength and flexibility of the neck.
• A previous neck injury.
• Poor posture.
• Spending long periods in certain positions that put stress on the neck, such as sitting at a computer for a long time.
What are the signs or symptoms?
Symptoms of this condition include:
• Pain, soreness, stiffness, tenderness, swelling, or a burning sensation in the front, back, or sides of the neck, shoulders, or
upper back.
• Sudden tightening of neck muscles (spasms).
• Limited ability to move the neck.
• Headache.
• Dizziness.
• Nausea or vomiting.
• Weakness, numbness, or tingling in a hand or an arm.

Finding Treatment for Addiction
Addiction is a complex disease of the brain that causes an uncontrollable (compulsive) need for:
• A substance. This includes alcohol, drugs, or prescription medicines, such as painkillers.
• An activity or behavior, such as gambling or shopping.
What are the risks?
Addiction is a progressive disease. Without treatment, addiction can get worse. Living with addiction puts you at higher risk for injury,
poor health, loss of employment, loss of money, and even death.
Addiction changes the way your brain works. Because of this change:
• The need for the medicine, drug, or activity can become so strong that you think about it all the time.
• Getting more and more of your addiction becomes the most important thing to you.
• You may find yourself leaving other activities and relationships to pursue your addiction.
• You can become physically dependent on a substance.
• Your health, behavior, emotions, and relationships can change for the worse.
