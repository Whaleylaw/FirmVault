1. FACTS & LIABILITY
On September 24, 2021, Mr. Volk was the restrained driver of a vehicle traveling northbound 
on Interstate 65 in Johnson County, Indiana. At the same time, Mr. Pinkston was the driver of a 
vehicle also traveling northbound on Interstate 65 directly behind Mr. Volk’s vehicle.

Mr. Volk reduced his speed due to a construction worker on the shoulder of the highway

coverage for the damages Mr. Volk sustained on September 24, 2021.
As shown in the Indiana Officer’s Standard Crash Report,1 Officer Brent Alspach of the 
Indiana State Police investigated the collision and determined that Mr. Pinkston was the sole party 
responsible for the collision due to his following too closely.
The conditions for establishing liability, in this case, are clear. Mr. Pinkston owed a duty 
of care to the other drivers on the road. Pursuant to IN Code § 9-21-5-1(a), “[a] person may not drive 
a vehicle on a highway at a speed greater than is reasonable and prudent under the conditions, having 
regard to the actual and potential hazards then existing. Speed shall be restricted as necessary to avoid 
colliding with a person, vehicle, or other conveyance on, near, or entering a highway in compliance 
with legal requirements and with the duty of all persons to use due care.”

3.5. Past and Future Pain and Suffering
Under Indiana law, historically, the most highly compensable area of damages, in general, is 
in the area of physical pain and suffering.
There is no question that the September 24, 2021, incident caused Mr. Volk a great deal of 
physical and emotional pain and suffering. As a result of the incident, Mr. Volk suffered serious 
injuries that affected, and continue to affect, his quality of life. Mr. Volk experienced difficulty and 
pain when trying to complete simple daily activities that he used to be able to complete with ease 
prior to the September 24, 2021, incident. 
Mr. Volk experiences significant limitations in mobility, making simple tasks such as walking 
and performing household chores extremely challenging. Additionally, he suffers from frequent 
headaches and cognitive injuries, which have significantly impacted his ability to concentrate and 
engage in activities he previously enjoyed, such as sleeping peacefully. To this day, Mr. Volk 
continues to experience pain and discomfort as a result of the September 24, 2021, incident at issue. 
Mr. Volk was diagnosed with a meningioma, and although the collision did not cause the 
condition, Mr. Volk was asymptomatic before the collision. After the collision, Mr. Volk suffered 
persistent headaches and cognitive impairments including delayed mental processing, dizziness, and 
seizure activity. These symptoms necessitated aggressive treatment including a craniotomy.
As you are aware, pre-existing injuries generally do not compromise car collision injury 
claims when a victim suffers aggravation of a prior injury or makes a prior injury more serious. A 
well-established principle in personal injury law is that “a defendant must take a plaintiff as he finds 
him.” This legal doctrine is referred to as the “eggshell skull rule.” The notion is that a defendant may 
be held liable for the injuries he causes even if they are more severe than would have been anticipated.
Mr. Volk’s pain and suffering as a result of the September 24, 2021, incident should remain 
at the forefront of your consideration as you evaluate his significant noneconomic damages.
