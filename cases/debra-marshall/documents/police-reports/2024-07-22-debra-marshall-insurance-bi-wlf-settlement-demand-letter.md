# FACTS  LIABILITY

On December 3, 2022, Ms. Marshall was the operating her moped traveling southbound on Dixie Highway in Cave City, Barren County, Kentucky. At the same time, Ms. Reed was the driver of a vehicle traveling southbound on Dixie Highway, directly behind Ms. Marshall. Suddenly and without warning, Ms. Reed collided with the rear end of Ms. Marshall’s moped. Upon impact, Ms. Marshall landed on the hood of Ms. Reed’s vehicle and was slammed into the ground. The collision resulted in Ms. Marshall sustaining significant personal injuries and other damages.

[**NTD**: Please note the police report had conflicting narratives from Ms. Marshall and Ms. Reed. Ms. Reed claimed Ms. Marshall was turning out of the parking lot at the time of the collision, however, Ms. Marshall claimed she was already operating her moped on Dixie Highway when she was hit from behind. Please review and revise at your discretion.]

[**NTD**: Please note the EMS records indicated that Ms. Marshall was not wearing a helmet. Please review and revise at your discretion.]

As exposed in the Kentucky Uniform Police Traffic Collision Report, Officer Rafael Casas of the Cave City Police Department investigated the collision. While Officer Casas did not indicate that Ms. Reed was responsible for the collision, it is clear from the facts and circumstances of the collision that Ms. Reed was the sole contributor to the collision due to her failure to control her speed.

[**NTD**: Please note the police report indicated that Ms. Marshall was the sole contributor to the collision due to her failing to yield the right of way. We have used failure to control speed as Ms. Reed’s contributing factor, however, the police report and narrative do not indicate that she had any contributing factor. Please review and revise at your discretion.]

<u>**The conditions for establishing liability are clear**</u>. Ms. Reed owed a duty of care to the other drivers on the road. Pursuant to **Ky. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 189.390(2)**, “[a]n operator of a vehicle upon a highway shall not drive at a greater speed than is reasonable and prudent, having regard for the traffic and for the condition and use of the highway.” On the date of loss, Ms. Reed lacked attentiveness and all due care, operated her vehicle at an excessive rate of speed for traffic conditions, and failed to take reasonable evasive action so as to avoid the collision. As a result of Ms. Reed’s acts and/or omissions on December 3, 2022, Ms. Marshall suffered significant personal injuries and damages for which Ms. Reed is undoubtedly liable.

## Loss of Household Services

As a result of the December 3, 2022, incident, we estimate that Ms. Marshall has been, and will continue to be, unable to contribute **$13,387.75** in labor in the form of past and future household services. Our calculation rests on three variables:

- Before her incident, we assume that Ms. Marshall contributed an average of 2.71 hours of household chores per day to the household, in line with the average contribution of persons of similar age and gender in America.

- We assume that the monetary value of Ms. Marshall’s labor is consistent with the average housekeeping salary of $12.68 an hour in the State of Kentucky, as estimated from the May 2022 Report on Occupational Employment and Wages by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics.

- In our estimation, Ms. Marshall suffered a 100% impairment from contributing to household chores from December 3, 2022, to September 8, 2023, and a conservative 10% impairment for three years thereafter. 

Assuming no further impairment of household chores after September 8, 2026, solely for simplicity and convenience of early settlement, we calculate the value of Ms. Marshall’s loss of household services between December 3, 2022, and September 8, 2026, at **$13,387.75**.

The value of lost household services, which includes losses for tasks the injured claimant provided around the home, are cognizable damages in personal injury cases in Kentucky where proof of such loss is substantiated by a preponderance of the evidence. *Primal Vantage Co., Inc. v. O’Bryan*, No. 2018-CA-0000106-MR, 2019 WL 6044870, at *2 (Ky. Ct. App. Nov. 15, 2019), aff’d in part, rev’d in part and remanded, No. 2020-SC-0247-DG, 2022 WL 3641122 (Ky. Aug. 18, 2022). Recovery for the loss of household services should be limited to the period of recuperation, and, if there is a permanent impairment of a person’s ability to perform household tasks, that item of damage would be included within the claim for the permanent impairment of earning capacity. *Schulz v. Chadwell*, 558 S.W.2d 183, 189 (Ky. Ct. App. 1977).

## Past and Future Pain and Suffering

There is no question that the December 3, 2022, incident caused Ms. Marshall a great deal of physical and emotional pain and suffering. As a result of the incident, Ms. Marshall suffered serious injuries that affected, and continue to affect, her quality of life. Ms. Marshall experienced difficulty and pain when trying to complete simple daily activities that she used to be able to complete with ease prior to the December 3, 2022, incident. To this day, Ms. Marshall continues to experience pain and discomfort as a result of the December 3, 2022, incident at issue. 

[**NTD**: Please include specific details about the client’s emotional and physical damages, if available.]

Ms. Marshall’s pain and suffering as a result of the December 3, 2022, incident should remain at the forefront of your consideration as you evaluate her significant noneconomic damages.

The goal of compensatory damages is to make the injured party whole to the extent it is possible to measure the injury in terms of money. *Ky. Cent. Ins. Co. v. Schneider*, 15 S.W.3d 373, 374 (Ky. 2000). Damages should include fair compensation for any physical or mental suffering caused by the injury. *Ky. Cent. Ins. Co. v. Schneider*, 15 S.W.3d 373, 374 (Ky. 1995). There is no set rule for computing damages for pain and suffering. The general rule is that the damages must be reasonable, free from sentiment and free from punishment so as not to amount to punitive damages. *Noel v. Creary*, 385 S.W.2d 951, 953 (Ky. 1965). The probability of future pain and suffering may be considered when calculating damages if the evidence shows that future pain and suffering may stem from the injury. *Nussbaum v. Caskey*, 235 Ky. 640, 32 S.W.2d 18, 19 (Ky. App. 1930).

Liability is clear. Should this case go to a jury, the sole question to answer would be how much money Ms. Marshall is entitled to receive under Kentucky law.
