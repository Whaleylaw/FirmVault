In the case at bar, the Plaintiffs’ allegations assert that, at most, Allstate is the liability
insurer for the vehicle negligently driven by theUnknown Driver and owned by Defendant Nail.
Kentucky is not a “direct action” state for claims asserted against a third party’s liability insurer.
Pryor v. Colony Ins., 414 S.W.3d 424, 432 (Ky.App. 2013). Thus, no direct claim or action can
be made byaplaintiff against a third-party liability insurer until liability has been formally
adjudicated against that insurer’s insureds. Id.
Because there has been no formal adjudication offault and damages against that
motorist—much less against Defendant Nail for her alleged false claims—the Plaintiffs have no
standing to pursue any claim against third-party insurer Allstate. Consequently, their Complaint
fails to state any claim against Allstate for which relief can be granted for these three oftheir
four allegations against Allstate.
As for the fourth allegation, the Plaintiffs’ broad-brush vague assertion that all
“Defendants should be subject to significant punitive damages over and above its insurance
limits . . . for engaging in such behavior that was willful and reckless” does not state any valid
claim against Allstate. The only thing that Allstate is specifically alleged to have done is to not
pay or provide coverage to the Plaintiffs’ on their liability claims caused by the negligent
Unknown Driver.

Furthermore, the Plaintiffs have no standing to make a punitive damage claim against
Allstate, as a third-party insurer, unless it were done in the context of a third-party bad faith

State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Reeder, 763 S.W.2d 116, 117-18 (Ky. 1989) and Glass at 451-
52. As the Kentucky Supreme Court has explained, its case of Wittmer v. Jones, 864 S.W.2d
885 (Ky. 1993) “gathered all ofthe bad faith liability theories under one roof and established a
test applicable to all bad faith actions, whether brought bya first-party claimant or a third-party
claimant, and whether premised upon common law theory ora statutory violation.” Davidson v.
American Freightways, 25 §.W.3d 94, 100 (Ky. 2000).
In Wittmer, the Court set forth an overarching three-part test threshold for all bad faith
claims, requiring: (1) the “insurer must be obligated to pay the claim under the terms of the
policy,” (2) the “insurer must lack a reasonable basis in low or fact for denying the claim,” and
(3) “it must be shown that the insurer either knew there was no reasonable basis for denying the
claim or acted with reckless disregard for whether such a basis existed.” Wittmer at 890. In
other words, these three points constitute basic elements ofany bad faith claim, to include any
punitive damages claims against a third-party liability insurer.
