BACKGROUND
On July 14, 2020, Betty Prince, a resident of Trimble County, went to Norton Hospital in 
Louisville to fill out paperwork for a future procedure. Ms. Prince was elderly and needed 
assistance walking. When Ms. Prince was finished with the paperwork, Norton Hospital 
employees walked Ms. Prince to the exit of the waiting room and left her, at which time Ms. 
Prince fell and suffered severe injuries. 
Ms. Prince subsequently died on April 7, 2021. Robert Prince was appointed 
representative of Ms. Prince’s estate on June 23, 2021.

LEGAL ARGUMENT
Although styled a “motion to dismiss” pursuant to CR 12.02(f), Norton Hospitals’ motion 
is more accurately titled a motion for judgment on the pleadings, because the pleadings are 
closed. CR 12.03 provides: 
After the pleadings are closed but within such time as not to delay the trial, any 
party may move for judgment on the pleadings. If, on such motion, matters 
outside the pleading are presented to and not excluded by the court, the motion 
shall be treated as one for summary judgment and disposed of as provided for in 
Rule 56, and all parties shall be given reasonable opportunity to present all 
materials made pertinent to such a motion by Rule 56.

4
A court should not grant a motion for judgment on the pleadings “unless it appears the 
pleading party would not be entitled to relief under any set of facts which could be proved....” 
Pari–Mutuel Clerks’ Union of Kentucky, Local 541, SEIU, AFL–CIO v. Kentucky Jockey Club, 
551 S.W.2d 801, 803 (Ky. 1977) (emphasis added). Accordingly, “the pleadings should be 
liberally construed in the light most favorable to the plaintiff, all allegations being taken as true.” 
Morgan v. Bird, 289 S.W.3d 222, 226 (Ky. App. 2009); see also Archer v. Citizens Fidelity Bank 
& Trust Co., 365 S.W.2d 727 (Ky. 1962). Moreover, under the standard for judgment on the 
pleadings, the Court must consider the allegations in the answer, as well as the allegations of the 
complaint. Bennett v. Bennett, 477 S.W.2d 799, 801 (Ky. 1972). When considering a motion for 
judgment on the pleadings, the moving party admits not just the allegations in the complaint, but 
also admits the untruth of the allegations in the answer. Archer, 365 S.W.2d at 729 (moving 
party admits “not only the truth of all of his adversary’s well-pleaded allegations of fact and fair 
inferences therefrom, but also the untruth of all of his own allegations”). To the extent KRS 
411.167 provides any defense it is an affirmative one, on which the Defendant bears the burden 
of proof. Lynn Mining Co. v. Kelly, 394 S.W.2d 755, 759 (Ky. 1965); CR 8.03.
I. KRS 411.167 only applies to pro se plaintiff filers.
KRS 411.167, enacted by the Kentucky Legislature in 2019, is titled “Certificate of merit 
for medical malpractice actions.” Certificates of merit were originally proposed as an alternative 
to the Medical Review Panel Act, which the Kentucky Legislature passed in 2017.5 Shortly after

the claimant is not asserting any claims that require expert testimony. Only one certification is 
necessary, even if there are multiple defendants. KRS 411.167(3). And a claimant can opt not to

Importantly, KRS 411.167 only requires only that “[a] claimant commencing any action
identified in KRS 413.140(1)(e) or against a long-term-care facility as defined in KRS 216.510” 
file a certificate of merit, not claimant’s counsel. The statute does not define “claimant,” but it 
does expressly differentiate between a “claimant” and “his or her counsel” in another part of the 
statute. See KRS 411.167(2)(c)(identifying when the claimant may nonetheless commence an 
action, even though the claimant “or his or her counsel” retained to investigate could not obtain 
expert consultations). 
The “cardinal rule of statutory construction is that the intention of the legislature should 
be ascertained and given effect.” MPM Fin. Grp., Inc. v. Morton, 289 S.W.3d 193, 197 (Ky. 
2009). As both the plain statutory language and the statute’s legislative history reflect, the 
purpose of the statute is to “help stop frivolous lawsuits” by requiring “claimants”—meaning 
plaintiffs only—to certify that they have a “reasonable basis” for the lawsuit. KRS 
411.167(2)(a).10 There is no similar requirement for claimant’s counsel. 
The distinction between claimant filers who are subject to KRS 411.167 and attorney 
filers who are not makes sense for several reasons. 
First, the legislature has no power to supersede court rules governing attorney conduct 
and, more specifically, court rules mandating that an attorney’s signature alone certifies that a

Second, extending the requirements of KRS 411.167 to attorney filers defies logic. The 
judicial branch already has the power to, and in fact already does, sanction attorneys for filing 
frivolous complaints on behalf of clients. As mentioned, the “affidavit or declaration” mentioned 
in KRS 411.167(2) is already satisfied by the attorney’s signature on the complaint which 
certifies that the complaint is brought in good faith and well-grounded in law and fact. See CR 11
supra. The Rules of Professional Conduct likewise requires an attorney to provide competent 
representation and act with reasonable diligence. See SCR 3.130(1.1)(“A lawyer shall provide 
competent representation to a client. Competent representation requires the legal knowledge, 
skill, thoroughness and preparation reasonably necessary for the representation”); SCR 
3.130(1.3)(“A lawyer shall act with reasonable diligence and promptness in representing a 
client”). “Courts must construe statutes in a manner that saves their constitutionality whenever 
possible consistent with ‘reason and common sense.’” Commonwealth v. Kash, 967 S.W.2d 37, 
44 (Ky. App. 1997), citing Diemer v. Commonwealth, Transportation Cabinet, 786 S.W.2d 861, 
863 (Ky 1990). Assuming the plain language of KRS 411.167 limiting the scope of the statute to

“claimants” is ignored, it would require an unreasonable, nonsensical, and unconstitutional 
construction of KRS 411.167 to extend the statute to attorney filers where court rules already 
provide that an attorney’s signature provides the necessary certification. 
Third, attorneys have long been subject to damages in tort for wrongful use of civil 
proceedings or abuse of process by defendants if an attorney files suit against such defendants 
without a reasonable basis. Clark Equip. Co. v. Bowman, 762 S.W.2d 417, 420 (Ky. App. 1988)
(“Historically successful litigants have had a remedy for the filing of meritless lawsuits in the 
nature of a countersuit for malicious prosecution [wrongful use of civil proceedings] or abuse of 
process.”). Indeed, the Legislature, immediately preceding KRS 411.167 in KRS Chapter 411 
provides for the “liability of attorney for professional negligence.” See KRS 411.165(1). It makes 
sense, then, that the Legislature would add a certificate of merit requirement for “claimants” in 
KRS 411.167, thereby allowing a statutory right of recovery by medical providers as against the 
pro se litigants who file complaints without a good faith basis in law or fact. See KRS 
446.070(“A person injured by the violation of any statute may recover from the offender such 
damages as he sustained by reason of the violation, although a penalty or forfeiture is imposed 
for such violation.”). Before the enactment of KRS 411.167, a defendant medical provider 
already had the right to recover damages against an attorney filer for instituting frivolous 
litigation. See Raine v. Drasin, 621 S.W.2d 895 (Ky. 1981)(allowing wrongful use of 
proceedings claim against attorney after medical malpractice complaint was dismissed), 
abrogated on other grounds by Martin v. O’Daniel, 507 S.W.3d 1 (Ky. 2016). Likewise, before 
the enactment of KRS 411.167, a represented claimant already the right to recover damages in 
tort from his own attorney due to his or her attorney’s negligence in filing such a frivolous 
complaint pursuant to KRS 411.165. The Legislature is presumed to be aware of these existing 

9
laws when enacting a new statute. St. Clair v. Commonwealth, 140 S.W.3d 510, 570 (Ky. 2004).
And the Legislature is presumed not to enact meaningless or redundant legislation. See Spencer 
v. Estate of Spencer, 313 S.W.3d 534, 544 (Ky. 2010)(“This change, too, would be meaningless 
if securities accounts were already included in the Multiple Party Account provisions. We 
presume, of course, that the General Assembly did not intend for its 1998 acts to be meaningless, 
and so conclude that the Court of Appeals was correct when it held that the KRS 391.315 
presumption of right of survivorship did not apply to the Spencers’ joint Edward Jones 
account.”). KRS 411.167 would be both meaningless and redundant if held applicable to attorney 
filers because the requirements in the statute already exist in both court rules and caselaw, where 
they are accompanied by sanctions and recovery in tort from the attorney filer. 
Although the language in KRS 411.167 clearly does not apply to represented parties, 
even if the “meaning or intention” of “undefined words or terms in a statute” are “uncertain,” 
then courts must then “look to traditional rules of statutory construction.” MPM Fin. Grp., Inc. v. 
Morton, 289 S.W.3d 193, 198 (Ky. 2009). “It is a rule of statutory construction that a statute 
should be construed so that no part of it is meaningless or ineffectual.” Allen v. McClendon, 967 
S.W.2d 1, 3 (Ky. 1998). Here, if the term “claimant” necessarily includes “claimant’s counsel,” 
then the reference later in the statute to “the claimant or his or her counsel” would be redundant 
and the reference to “his or her counsel” is meaningless. See KRS 411.167(2)(c); see also 
Jefferson Cty. Bd. of Educ. v. Fell, 391 S.W.3d 713, 718-19 (Ky. 2012) (“We presume that the 
General Assembly intended for the statute to be construed as a whole, for all of its parts to have 
meaning, and for it to harmonize with related statutes.”). 
It is also a general rule of statutory construction that “omissions are intentional.” Fox v. 
Grayson, 317 S.W.3d 1, 11 (Ky. 2010). “This rule is based on logic and common sense,” and it

“expresses the concept that when people say one thing they do not mean something else.” Id. 
Had the Legislature intended to impose the certificate of merit requirement on claimants and
claimants’ attorneys, they could have said so, just as numerous other states have done with 
similar statutes.11
Tellingly, other statutes in KRS Chapter 411 with similar notice requirements specify that 
such notice must be provided at the commencement of the action by the plaintiff or counsel. For 
example, KRS 411.188(2) provides: “[a]t the commencement of an action seeking to recover 
damages, it shall be the duty of the plaintiff or his attorney to notify, by certified mail, those 
parties believed by him to hold subrogation rights to any award received by the plaintiff as a

Justice), and §§ 14, 54, and 241 (jural rights doctrine)).

redundant as court rules and caselaw already regulate attorney filers, penalize attorneys for filing

II. KRS 411.167 also does not apply to premises liability actions like this one.
KRS 411.167 is titled “Certificate of merit for medical malpractice actions.” Although 

12
the title of the statute plainly demonstrates that it does not apply to premises liability cases like 
the one here, so too does the language of the statute. KRS 411.167 only extends to negligence or 
malpractice claims against long-term-care facilities or “any action identified in KRS 
413.140(1)(e).” The latter statute, KRS 413.140(1)(e), is limited to an action “against a 
physician, surgeon, dentist, or hospital licensed pursuant to KRS Chapter 216, for negligence or 
malpractice.” 
Here, Count II of the Complaint12 does not allege direct negligence or malpractice by any 
“physician, surgeon, dentist, or hospital” under KRS 413.140(1)(e).13 Instead, Count II of the 
Complaint asserts vicarious liability on the part of the hospital due to the acts or omissions of its 
employee- nurses and staff that contributed to Ms. Prince’s fall. Respondeat superior liability 
that is imposed on a principal for the acts of an agent or employee is not the same as a negligence 
or malpractice claim asserted directly against the principal. See, e.g., MV Transp. Inc. v. Allgeier, 
433 S.W.3d 324, 337 (Ky. 2014) (“plaintiff may assert and pursue in the same action a claim 
against an employer based under respondeat superior upon the agent’s negligence, and a 
separate claim based upon the employer’s own direct negligence in hiring, retention, supervision, 
or training”). To read KRS 411.167 more broadly would require that a certificate of merit 
accompany any claim against any hospital employee for any reason. Thus, for example, 
certificates of merit would be required for claims arising if a hospital visitor tripped and fell 
because of a hazard a maintenance employee left in a hallway, or if a cafeteria worker failed to 
properly store meat leading to food poisoning, or if a gift store worker negligently dropped a 
heavy vase on a customer’s foot. These are just a few of the many possibilities, all of which are

III. Alternatively, Plaintiff’s Estate should be granted leave to file an Amended 
Complaint or, at a minimum, a dismissal without prejudice with leave to refile.
CR 15.01 provides that leave to amend “shall be freely given when justice so requires.” 
Further, as the Supreme Court recently confirmed, “Kentucky law ‘favors the right of litigants to 
have their rights disposed of on the merits rather than technicalities,’ and therefore courts have 
broad discretion in permitting amendments or other reasonable changes in pleadings.” Nami 
Resources Company, L.L.C. v. Asher Land and Mineral, Ltd., 554 S.W.3d 323, 343 (Ky. 2018), 
quoting Kentucky Home Mutual Life Insurance Co. v. Hardin, 277 Ky. 565, 126 S.W.2d 427, 
431 (1938). The Supreme Court previously held that “[l]ong ago, in 1953, we abandoned the old 
rules of common law pleadings and adopted modern Rules of Civil Procedure. We no longer 
approach pleadings searching for a flaw, a technicality upon which to strike down a claim or 
defense, as was formerly the case at common law.” Smith v. Isaacs, 777 S.W.2d 912, 915 (Ky. 
1989). Unfortunately, relying on “a technicality upon which to strike down a claim or defense” is 
precisely what Norton Hospitals seeks to do here via KRS 411.167—a statute which, as 
mentioned, provides no penalty for non-compliance.
Here, although the incident occurred on July 14, 2020, Mr. Prince was not appointed 
administrator of Ms. Prince’s estate until June 23, 2021. Under Kentucky law, the Plaintiff Estate 
has one (1) year from the date of that appointment—or until June 23, 2022—to assert any claims. 
See KRS 413.180(1)(allowing a personal injury action belonging to the decedent to be filed to be 
filed more than year from the date of the incident “if commenced within one (1) year after the

(Ky. 1992)(“The purpose of KRS 413.180 is to allow time for the appointment of a personal 
representative and then to give that personal representative time to evaluate claims and determine 
whether to pursue those claims …”). Accordingly, there would be no prejudice whatsoever to 
Norton Hospitals in granting the Plaintiff Estate leave to file an amended complaint accompanied 
by a certificate of merit. Therefore, Plaintiff requests leave to file same should the Court deem 
that KRS 411.167 is applicable here.
