Comes the Defendant, Norton Hospitals, Inc. (“Norton’’), by counsel, and for its Response
and Objection to Plaintiff's Motion to Compel, states:
This is a premises liability case concerningatrip and fall that occurred at Norton Hospital.
On July 14, 2020, Betty Prince visited Norton Hospital with her husband, Robert Prince, for a
medical appointment.' While in the registration area on the second floor of Norton’s downtown
hospital, Ms. Prince was invited by a registration clerk, Lucretia Vinson, to walk back to a private
office to complete her registration. Ms. Prince walked to Ms. Vinson’s office without issue and
completed her registration. However, upon returning to the waiting area, Ms. Prince tripped and
fell.
This is not a case where the Plaintiff is alleging some sort of hazard or condition on the
premises caused the Plaintiff to fall. Rather, the Plaintiff blames Norton for failing to appreciate
Ms. Prince asa fall risk and take adequate precautions. The Plaintiff has preemptively provided
an affidavit of Nurse Donna S. Jones, RN, which purports to contain expert opinions critical of

Interrogatory No. 2
Interrogatory No. 2 request the Defendant to “identify any and all falls resulting from [sic]
injury of any form which occurred in the second-floor outpatient registration area of Norton
Hospital downtown... 1n the five (5) years preceding the fall at issue.”” Norton objects on several
grounds: (1) the Interrogatory is overly broad, unduly burdensome, and irrelevant; and (2) the
identification of other patients at Norton Hospital is prohibited from disclosure under HIPAA. 45
C.F.R. § 164.502.
First, the identification of other hypothetical falls is simply not relevant to this case. This
is not a case where the Plaintiff is alleging some sort of hazard or dangerous condition on the
premises. Thus, this is not a case where the existence of prior falls is relevant (e.g., prior notice
of a hazard on the premises). Here, the Plaintiffs criticism is that the Defendant failed to recognize

Moreover, the letter from Plaintiff’s counsel that forms the basis of Plaintiff's Motion to
Compel simply asks that the Defendant identify “What section and/or portion of HIPAA would be
violated by disclosure of other instances of individuals falling...” Respectfully, the Defendant
already provided the citation to the relevant HIPAA statute in their discovery responses. 45 C.F.R.
§ 164.502 prohibits the disclosure of protected health information. “ProtectedHealth Information”
is defined under 45 C.F.R. § 160.103 and includes information that “relates to the past, present, or
future physical or mental health condition of an individual; the provision of health care to an
individual; or the past, present, or future payment for the provision of health care to an individual.”
The Defendant cannot fathom how it could meaningfully “identify” falls on the premises that
resulted in injury without identifying Norton patients, health care provided to those patients, and
their health conditions. Such would be an obvious violation of HIPAA. Accordingly, the
Plaintiff's Motion to Compel on this topic should be denied.
