Pursuant to CR 37.01, a party may move for an order compelling discovery when the other party fails to answer or otherwise respond to discovery requests. When considering a motion to compel, a discovery request is considered to be seeking relevant information if there is any possibility that the information sought may be relevant to a claim or defense raised. Transamerica Life Ins. Co. v. Moore, 274 F.R.D. 602, 608 (E.D. Ky. 2011).

Moreover, courts have consistently upheld a strong presumption in favor of openness in pretrial discovery, unless compelling reasons justify withholding information from the public. Meyer Goldberg, Inc. v. Fisher Foods, Inc., 823 F.2d 159 (6th Cir. 1987), see also Rudd Equip. Co. v. John Deer Constr. & Forestry Co., 834 F.3d 589 (6th Cir. 2016). A party can only overcome the presumption of openness by demonstrating that failing to protect information will result in “clearly defined and very serious injury.” Shane Group, Inc. v. Blue Cross Blue Shield of Michigan, 825 F.3d 299 (6th Cir. 2016).

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

Following the incident described above, Plaintiff filed their Complaint on July 1, 2021, alleging premises liability and medical negligence Specifically, the Complaint alleged that Defendant Norton failed to maintain its premises in a reasonably safe condition. Further, the

Complaint alleged that employees knew or should have known Mrs. Prince was at a high risk of




falling and did not adequately care for her. The negligence of Defendant Norton and its agents was alleged to be a substantial factor in causing the above-described fall, and Plaintiff's subsequent injuries.

Unfortunately, after the filing to this case, Mrs. Prince passed. Thus, the Estate of Betty Prince was created and an Amended Complaint was filed with Robert Prince as the Administrator for the Estate of Betty Prince. Plaintiff subsequently served written discovery on June 28, 2022. The Defendant, following an entry of an Order to Compel, served answers to Plaintiff's discovery on October 16, 2023.

Following this, on February 6, 2024, depositions were taken of Plaintiff Robert Prince and Lucretia Vinson, an employee of the Defendant and witness to the events herein. Subsquently, on March 19, 2024, Plaintiff served supplemental discovery requests to the Defendant, with specific questions about information discussed in Ms. Vinson’s deposition. After another Order to Compel, Defendant provided responses on June 17, 2024. As these responses were insufficient, the instant motion followed.

At issue herein are Defendant Norton’s responses to the following discovery requests:

Interrogatory No. 2 — Other Falls in the Registration Area Plaintiffs’ Supplemental Interrogatory No. 2:

Please identify any and all falls resulting in injury of any form which occurred in the second-floor outpatient registration area of Norton Hospital Downtown (as identified in Lucretia Vinson’s deposition pages 21-26) in the five (5) years preceding the fall at issue herein.

Defendant’s Response to Plaintiff’s Supplemental Interrogatory No. 2:

Objection. The Defendant objects to this Interrogatory on the grounds that it is overly broad, unduly burdensome, and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. The Defendant further objects to this Interrogatory to the extent it seeks Protected Health Information prohibited from disclosure under HIPAA. C.F.R § 164.502.




Defendant argues that the request for identifying other falls in the registration area is overly broad, unduly burdensome, irrelevant, and a violation of HIPAA. However, these objections are unsubstantiated.

First, the request is narrowly tailored to specific falls in a specific area—the outpatient registration area—over a reasonable period of five years. The Defendant has not demonstrated that identifying falls in this limited area over five years would cause undue hardship. If such numerous falls have occurred in this area, that fact would reinforce the relevance of this information to the Plaintiff’s claim.

In terms of relevance, the Plaintiff has alleged both that Norton failed to recognize Mrs. Prince as a fall risk and that it maintained unsafe conditions in the registration area, making this a premises liability case. Identifying other falls is directly relevant to whether Norton was aware of ongoing hazards or risks to patients, including fall risks in that area. This information could establish a pattern of incidents and Norton’s knowledge of fall risks, supporting Plaintiff’s claims.

As for the HIPAA objection, Plaintiff did not request any protected health information or identifying information about other patients. Rather, the request seeks factual data about the occurrence of falls. This information can be provided in a manner that complies with HIPAA, such as through anonymized data or under a protective order, as is common in cases involving sensitive

information. The Defendant’s reliance on HIPAA as an absolute bar to discovery is misplaced.

Document Request No. 2 — Compliance and Continuing Training for Ms. Vinson

Plaintiff's Supplemental Document Request No. 2:

Please produce any and all compliance, in-service, or other continuing training documents identified on page 17 of Lucretia Vinson’s deposition.




Defendant’s Response to Plaintiff’s Supplemental Document Request No. 2:

Objection. The Defendant objects to this Interrogatory on the grounds that it is overly

broad, unduly burdensome, and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of

admissible evidence. Without waiving objection, no documents were identified on page 17

of Lucretia Vinson’s deposition.

Defendant asserts that no specific compliance or in-service training documents were identified in Ms. Vinson’s deposition and claims to have already provided a checklist of her onboarding training. However, this response is both insufficient and incomplete. While the checklist may cover general onboarding, Ms. Vinson’s deposition clearly indicates that compliance training varies by department and occurs annually, which suggests that training specific to the registration department and patient safety protocols should be available.” The checklist provided does not address any continuing or compliance training related to critical patient safety issues, such as fall risks, that Ms. Vinson might have received during her employment.

Plaintiff’s request is not overly broad or burdensome; it is a narrowly focused inquiry into whether Norton provided ongoing training specific to fall prevention for registration staff. It is entirely reasonable to seek documentation of compliance training, especially given the nature of Mrs. Prince’s vulnerability and the hospital’s duty of care in preventing falls.

The documents sought are essential to determining whether Norton sufficiently prepared its staff for handling fall risks, and Defendant has failed to demonstrate any legitimate basis for withholding this critical information. The absence of training speaks volumes about Norton’s negligence, and the request for compliance documents is very reasonable. The Court should

therefore compel production of these materials. As Kentucky courts have previously held, the

Document Request No. 4 — Policies Specific to the Registration Department

Plaintiff's Supplemental Document Request No. 4:

Please produce any and all policies specific to the registration department and its staff, as identified on page 40 of Lucretia Vinson’s deposition.

Defendant’s Response to Plaintiff’s Supplemental Document Request No. 4: Objection. The Defendant objects to this Interrogatory on the grounds that it is overly broad, unduly burdensome, and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s request for policies specific to the registration department is overly broad and unduly burdensome, asserting that policies related to patient registration are irrelevant. However, Defendant’s argument fails to recognize that policies governing the registration process may directly relate to patient safety and the procedures in place when assessing patients in vulnerable conditions, such as Mrs. Prince.

Mrs. Prince’s fall occurred while she was in the registration area, and policies governing this area’s staff could very well influence whether staff members were properly trained to recognize potential fall risks. Plaintiff is not seeking a fishing expedition into Norton’s operations but seeks those policies pertinent to patient interaction, safety protocols, and fall prevention within the registration department. The information requested quite clearly is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence because it goes directly to what Norton and its

employees should do and are required to do in situations similar to the instant one, and, thus, are




likely to help identify and reveal further information about what Norton did or did not do with regard to the incident at issue herein.

There is no good faith basis on the part of Defendants, who have or should have the requested information in their possession or control, to refuse production of the information sought on the grounds that the scope of discovery is too broad or that it would be unduly burdened if required to produce this highly relevant information. Defendant should therefore be compelled to

produce these discoverable documents.
