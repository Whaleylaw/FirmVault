BRIEF SUMMARY OF FACTS 
On July 14, 2020, Betty Prince arrived at Norton Hospital for a scheduled medical 
procedure. Mrs. Prince required assistance from her husband, Robert Prince, to walk from the 
parking area into the hospital and into the second-floor registration area. Despite her obvious 
unsteadiness and difficulty ambulating, no Norton staff member offered a wheelchair, assistance, 
or any fall precautions. Mr. Prince was then instructed he could not accompany his wife beyond 
the waiting room due to COVID-19 policies. 
Mrs. Prince should have been identified and classified as a fall risk immediately upon 
arrival and check-in, based on her visible difficulty walking and need for assistance. Additionally, 
Norton staff had just registered her for a procedure, meaning they were fully aware of her medical 
status and potential risks. Instead, she was left to navigate the hallway unassisted after registration.

As she attempted to return to the waiting area, Mrs. Prince fell backward and struck her head on 
the hard floor, sustaining a laceration and head injury. Mrs. Prince was admitted to the emergency 
department, where she was diagnosed with a small subdural hematoma and remained hospitalized 
for observation before discharge two days later. 
The facts of this incident are clear. Mrs. Prince’s fall and resulting injuries occurred only 
because she was left unassisted despite presenting with visible mobility challenges and an obvious 
need for support. The following section outlines how Norton Hospital’s actions and omissions fell 
below the standard of care owed to Mrs. Prince under these circumstances. 
STATEMENT REGARDING LIABILITY 
The conduct of Norton Hospital staff fell below the applicable standard of care for patient 
safety and fall prevention. As detailed in the affidavit of Donna Jones, RN, Mrs. Prince presented 
to Norton with clear mobility deficits and a recent history of falls, yet staff failed to identify her as 
a fall risk or implement any fall precautions. Mrs. Prince was visibly weak, required assistance 
from her husband to ambulate upon arrival, and had a documented pattern of instability and recent 
falls, as reflected in her home health records. Despite these clear indications, Norton staff neither 
offered nor provided a wheelchair, assistance, or any other safeguard to prevent injury. 
More troubling, Norton had direct, documented knowledge of Mrs. Prince’s fall risk status 
well before the date of her injury. On February 21, 2020, during a visit to Norton King’s Daughters’ 
Health, she was formally identified as a fall risk due to balance and walking difficulties. That 
designation, based on the same mobility issues that ultimately caused her fall, was part of her 
medical chart and should have been available to Norton Hospital staff at the time of her check-in. 
This was not a new or unforeseen condition. It was a known and recorded risk within Norton’s 
own healthcare system. The failure to recognize and act on that information reflects a serious lapse 
in patient safety and a clear violation of the standard of care. 
The affidavit of Ms. Jones, a qualified nursing expert, is offered to demonstrate that 
Norton’s conduct fell below the standards of care expected of healthcare providers in similar 
circumstances. Even though registration personnel may not have been responsible for direct patient 
handling, the standard of care required any healthcare professional within the facility to recognize 
fall risks and immediately notify appropriate staff to provide contact guard assist, stand-by assist, 
or a wheelchair. Norton staff failed to take these basic and necessary steps. Mrs. Prince was instead 
left to walk unassisted in an unfamiliar environment, resulting in her preventable fall and 
subsequent injuries. 
Having discussed liability, the remainder of this demand will focus on Mrs. Prince’s 
damages.

instability. A Morse Fall Risk assessment completed in the emergency department assigned her a 
fall risk score of 40. Her medical records repeatedly document pre-existing weakness, bilateral 
lower extremity instability, and impaired ambulation. Despite these risk factors, it is noted that no 
fall prevention protocols were in place prior to the incident. 
Following her discharge on July 16, 2020, Mrs. Prince required extensive home health care 
through Kindred Healthcare. From July 2020 through March 2021, she received skilled nursing 
and physical therapy services focused on fall prevention, balance training, and recovery of mobility 
deficits resulting from the incident. Providers consistently documented her classification as a high 
fall risk, her use of assistive devices, and her need for assistance to safely ambulate. Her home 
therapy regimen included supervised exercises, gait and transfer training, and home safety 
education. Despite this care, Mrs. Prince continued to experience significant instability, including 
another reported fall in March 2021 that necessitated further therapy. Her home health records 
confirm that the majority of services provided during this period were directed at treating the 
ongoing effects of her fall.
