Purpose:
The purpose of the Security Management Plan (SMP) is to describe the processes used to provide a secure and safe 
environment at Norton Healthcare Hospitals and their operating entities by managing risks of personal injury, property 
loss or information loss due to criminal activity, or workplace violence.
Policy:
The Security Management Plan describes the methods of providing security for people, equipment, and other material 
through risk assessment and management for Norton Healthcare. Security protects individuals and property against 
harm, loss, or vandalism, including workplace violence, theft, infant / child abduction and unrestricted access to 
medications. Additionally the Plan describes the hospitals role within the community and how it integrates in the Norton 
Healthcare System Comprehensive Emergency Management Plan
This SMP covers risk assessment, access control, loss prevention, identification, specialized security measures and critical 
incident response processes for Norton Healthcare Hospitals and their operating entities within the main hospital or in 
other lease operational spaces.
1. Key Functions 
a. A visible security presence in the hospitals to reduce crime and increase feelings of security by patients, 
staff, and guests.
b. The assessment of risks to identify potential problems is central to reducing crime, injury, and other 
incidents.
c. Analysis of security incidents provides information to track, trend, and prevent crime, injury, and other 
incidents.
d. Training hospital staff is critical to ensuring their performance. Staff are trained to recognize and report 
either potential or actual incidents to ensure a timely response.
e. Staff in sensitive areas are trained on the protective measures designed for those areas and their 
responsibilities to assist in protection of patients, staff, guests, and property.
f. Manage the risk of workplace violence. 
g. Provide identification for all staff, patients, designated vendors, visitors, and volunteers to restrict 
access to unauthorized individuals.
h. Provide vehicular access to all emergency areas, i.e. fire lanes and the Emergency Department.
i. Provide for emergency security procedures in response to emergency situations, Very Important Person 
services, abductions, media relations and other disruptive situations.
2. Objectives 
a. The objectives for the SMP are developed from information gathered during routine and risk assessment 
activities, annual evaluation of the previous year’s program activities, performance measures, incident 
reports, and rounds. The objectives for this SMP are: 
i. Monitor the incidents of theft and focus on reduction
ii. Increase preventative patrols to assist in theft reduction
iii. Increase daily rounds to identify security risks

3. Organization / Responsibility 
a. The Board of Trustees receives regular reports of the activities of the Safety Management Program 
including information on the Security Management Plan from the multidisciplinary improvement team, 
the System Environment of Care (EOC) Committee, which is responsible for the physical environment 
issues. They review reports and, as appropriate, communicate concerns about identified issues and 
regulatory compliance back to the EOC for evaluation or action. They also provide financial and 
administrative support to facilitate the ongoing activities of the Safety Management Program.
b. The System Director for Safety, Security and Emergency Preparedness, collaborates with the System EOC 
Committee Chairperson to establish operating and capital budgets for the System Security Management 
Program.
c. The System Director for Safety, Security and Emergency Preparedness, in collaboration with the Facility 
based and System Environment of Care Committees are responsible for monitoring all aspects of the 
Security Management Program. Facility Security leaders, through the System Director of Safety, Security 
and Emergency Preparedness advises the System EOC Committee regarding security issues which may 
necessitate changes to policies and procedures, orientation or education, or expenditure of funds.
d. Facility Security leaders are charged with the risk analysis, initial design and for ensuring ongoing testing 
and maintenance of building security systems and protocols.
e. Department heads are responsible for orienting new staff members to their department and, as
appropriate, to job and task specific to unit security and security response procedures. They are also 
responsible for the investigation of incidents occurring in their departments. When necessary, the 
security leaders provides department heads with assistance in developing department security 
programs or policies. 
f. Managers are responsible for sending the Facility EOC Committee copies of their department safety 
policies / procedures to ensure there are no conflicts with system policies / procedures.
g. Individual staff members are responsible for learning, retaining and following job and task-specific 
procedures for safe operations.
4. Process for managing security risks and compliance 
a. Security Risk Assessment 
i. Security leaders manage the security risk assessment process for hospitals and outpatient 
service locations. They are designated to manage risk, coordinate risk reduction activities in the 
physical environment, collect deficiency information, and disseminate summaries of actions and 
results. The assessment of the hospital identifies security risk associated with the environment 
of care. 
ii. Risk are identified from internal resources such as ongoing rounds of the environment, results of 
root cause analyses, results of annual proactive risk assessments of high risk processes and from 
credible external sources such as Sentinel Event Alerts.
iii. The risk assessment is used to evaluate the impact of the environment of care on the ability of 
the hospital to perform clinical and business activities. The impact may include disruption of 
normal functions or injury to individuals. The assessment will evaluate the risk from a variety of 
functions, including structure of the environment, the performance of everyday tasks, 
workplace violence, theft, infant / child abduction, and unrestricted access to medications. 
b. Use of Risk Assessment Results 
i. The Security Director, Manager or their designee gathers data for conducting proactive risk 
assessments of the external environment or changes in services offered to evaluate the 
potential adverse impacts (including the potential for violence in the workplace) on the security 
of patients, staff and others who come into Norton Healthcare Hospitals and their operating 
entities within the hospital or in other leased / owned operational spaces. A preliminary review 
will be developed by the Security Director, Manager or their designee and reported to the 
Environment of Care Committee for further review, discussion and development.

c. Identification (ID) Program 
i. The Human Resource (HR) department is the responsible business owner for the identification 
policy. Security leaders coordinate the identification program. The Materials Management 
department provides vendor identification via the Simplr system. The HR department and all 
supervisory personnel manage enforcement of the identification program.
ii. All employees, volunteers, vendors, and independent practitioners are provided and required to 
display an identification badge on their upper body while on duty. ID badges are to be displayed 
with the picture showing. Their department head counsels employees who fail to properly 
display their ID badge. ID badges are retrieved from employees upon termination.
iii. Appropriate identification includes laminated picture badges, stick-on labels or clip-on badges, 
wristbands and contractor identification approved by Facilities and Construction for 
construction personnel. Color coded stripes and cartoon characters are added to ID badges that 
denote the role of the employee who work in security sensitive areas (i.e. working with children 
or infants) or as temporary employment.
iv. Where required, patient ID is provided where they are first admitted. If a patient wristband is 
damaged it is replaced by the nursing staff. Patient ID is not removed upon discharge. Patients 
are instructed to remove the ID wristband at home. 
v. Visitors are issued an ID badge or may be required to adhere to finger print scanning to 
designated security sensitive areas (e.g. labor & delivery, infant units, etc.) during and / or after 
normal business hours.
d. Security Sensitive Areas 
i. As part of the security risk assessment process, areas are evaluated for controlled access and 
egress or for inherent security risks. Typically expensive equipment, medications, risk of patient 
elopement, or a propensity for visitor / patient altercation or restrictions are considered to be 
the determining factors for sensitive area status. Areas determined as sensitive will receive 
special consideration for enhanced physical security systems or services.
ii. Security leaders work with department heads to identify security sensitive areas by utilizing risk 
assessments and analysis of incident reports. A security sensitive area is defined as one that has 
special security needs beyond the general hospital areas. Typically this will be due to issues such 
as vulnerable patients, financial issues, drug issues, and access to protected information and 
critical equipment. 
iii. The following areas are currently designated as security sensitive / high risk areas: 
1. Administration and Human Resources
2. Emergency department
3. Health Information Management
4. Infant / Pediatric units
5. Information Services
6. Nuclear Medicine
7. Pharmacy
8. Surgery department
9. Cash Handling Areas (business office, cafeteria, gift shop, banks / credit unions, etc.)
iv. Employees assigned to work in security sensitive areas receive department level continuing 
education on an annual basis that focuses on special precautions or responses that pertain to 
their area.

i. The facility maintains standard operational practices that are followed in the event of a security 
incident. Some may involve only members of the Security Department (thefts, Dr. Quick, auto 
accident), while others, such as an infant or pediatric abduction (Code Pink) will involve multiple 
departments within the organization and have therefore been incorporated in the 
Comprehensive Emergency Operations Plan. 
ii. Security leaders and department heads assist in the development of department-specific 
security procedures for risk unique to their area of responsibility. Department heads are 
responsible for distribution of department level procedures to their staff and for ensuring 
enforcement of those procedures. Each employee is responsible for following those procedures.
f. Security Incident Response 
i. Upon notification of a security incident, the security leader will assess the situation and 
implement the appropriate response procedures. The security leader will notify Administration 
if necessary to obtain additional support. 
ii. In the event of an infant or child reported missing, a Code Pink is announced over the internal 
page system and other emergency notification systems. Designated staff responds to entry and 
exit doors and specified areas to observe for persons with an infant or child or packages, and call 
security if such cases occur. Other staff check designated areas, respond to the unit involved to 
document information, and provide support to the parents. The plan is tested at least once a 
year, and the responses documented, evaluated, critiqued, and as appropriate corrective action, 
additional training, or program improvements are made.
iii. Following any security incident a written report will be filed by the responding security officer 
managing the incident. The appropriate security supervisor will review the report and ensure 
corrections are made if necessary. A summary of these reports will be furnished to the 
Environment of Care Committee during scheduled meetings. 
5. Performance Improvement 
a. The Security Program monitors several activity indicators of performance and staff knowledge. The 
Program has established security indicators that are meaningful and will have impacts toward improved 
security at Norton Healthcare Hospitals and their operating entities within the hospital or in other 
leased/owned operational spaces. These indicators are drawn from historic data, occurrences or 
associated risks and the means identified to monitor compliance to and effectiveness of established 
processes and procedures. The Committee follows the performance improvement process utilized by 
Norton Healthcare. Indicators for monitoring performance for this plan will focus on staff knowledge, 
occurrences, adherence to policies and security system preventative maintenance.
6. Education and Training 
a. Each new staff member of Norton Healthcare participates in a general orientation program which 
includes information related to the Security Management Plan. Examples of such information include: 
identification, controlled access, alarm systems, emergency codes, and loss prevention. The Human 
Resources Department is responsible for conducting the general orientation program and maintaining 
attendance records.
b. New staff members also receive a department-specific orientation to the department where they are 
assigned to work. Each department manager is responsible for providing their new staff members with a 
department-specific orientation to the Security Management Plan. The goal of the department 
orientation program is to provide new staff members with current information regarding area-specific 
issues, and departmental responsibilities. New staff members should complete their departmentspecific safety orientation within thirty (30) days of employment. 
c. Staff members participate in a mandatory Essential Staff Training program. Information specific to the 
Security Management Plan is included within the scope of the education program.
