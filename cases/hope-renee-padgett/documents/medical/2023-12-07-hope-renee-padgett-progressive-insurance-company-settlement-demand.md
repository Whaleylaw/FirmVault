1. FACTS & LIABILITY




On January 16, 2023, Ms. Padgett was the restrained driver of a silver Kia Soul traveling eastbound on West College Avenue in Stanton, Powell County, Kentucky. At the same time, Mr. Barnes was the driver of a vehicle traveling in the opposite direction on West College Avenue. Suddenly and without warning, Mr. Barnes attempted to make a left-hand turn onto Sipple Street and struck the driver’s side door and front fender of Ms. Padgett’s vehicle. Upon impact, Ms. Padgett was trapped inside her vehicle and had to be removed by the Stanton Fire Department by prying her door open. The collision resulted in Ms. Padgett sustaining significant personal injuries and other damages.

As exposed in the Kentucky Uniform Police Traffic Collision Report,! Officer William Dobbs of the Stanton Police Department investigated the collision.

The conditions for establishing liability, in this case, are clear. Mr. Barnes owed a duty of care to the other drivers on the road. Pursuant to KY Rev Stat § 189.390(2), “[a]n operator of a

vehicle upon a highway shall not drive at a greater speed than is reasonable and prudent, having regard for the traffic and for the condition and use of the highway.” On the date of loss, Mr. Barnes lacked attentiveness, operated his vehicle at an excessive rate of speed for traffic conditions, lacked all due care, and caused a collision with Ms. Padgett’s vehicle. As a result of Mr. Barnes’s acts and/or omissions on January 16, 2023, Ms. Padgett suffered significant personal injuries and damages for which Mr. Barnes is undoubtedly liable.

As evidenced by the photographs below,” the collision resulted in significant bodily injury.

3.3. Past and Future Pain and Suffering

There is no question that the January 16, 2023, incident caused Ms. Padgett a great deal of physical and emotional pain and suffering. As a result of the incident, Ms. Padgett suffered serious injuries that affected her entire life. Ms. Padgett experienced difficulty and pain when trying to complete simple daily activities that she used to be able to complete with ease prior to the January 16, 2023, incident.

Simply put, Ms. Padgett will be reminded of the January 16, 2023, incident every single day for the rest of her life when she awakens, when she drives in a car, when she goes to work, and when she tries to interact with friends and family or meet new people. The pain and impact of this ordeal will never leave Ms. Padgett, and this should remain at the forefront of your consideration as you evaluate Ms. Padgett’s significant noneconomic damages.

The goal of compensatory damages is to make the injured party whole to the extent it is possible to measure the injury in terms of money. Ky. Cent. Ins. Co. v. Schneider, 15 S.W.3d 373, 374 (Ky. 2000). Damages should include fair compensation for any physical or mental suffering caused by the injury. Ky. Cent. Ins. Co. v. Schneider, 15 S.W.3d 373, 374 (Ky. 1995). There is no set rule for computing damages for pain and suffering. The general rule is that the damages must be reasonable, free from sentiment and free from punishment so as not to amount to punitive damages. Noel v. Creary, 385 S.W.2d 951, 953 (Ky. 1965). The probability of future pain and suffering may be considered when calculating damages if the evidence shows that future pain and suffering may stem from the injury. Nussbaum v. Caskey, 235 Ky. 640, 32 S.W.2d 18, 19 (Ky. App. 1930).




Liability is clear. Should this case go to a jury, the sole question to answer would be how much money Ms. Padgett is entitled to receive under Kentucky law.
