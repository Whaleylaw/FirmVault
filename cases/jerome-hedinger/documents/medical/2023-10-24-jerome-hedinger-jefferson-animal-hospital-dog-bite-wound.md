Rory has been seen for concerns regarding a dog fight incident that occurred this afternoon.
If your dog has been involved in a dog fight, it can sometimes be challenging to determine the extent of the injuries. Dog bites can
cause significant injury to the skin and soft tissues. The dog's teeth and jaws are very powerful and the wounds they inflict can crush
or tear muscles and skin, penetrate through body walls or cause serious or fatal damage to underlying organs. Even a bite that does
not break the skin can cause crushing or bruising injuries to the underlying soft tissues. Imaging (radiographs or ultrasound) may be
recommended to try to evaluate underlying structures. Continued monitoring for signs of bleeding are important (ie blood in urine or
stool or vomit).
Since the dog's mouth is full of bacteria, any bite that does puncture the skin will introduce bacteria. All animal bite wounds are
considered to be contaminated and/or infected.
Most wounds are painful, your pet will be given some sort of pain medication, sedative, or anesthetic before attempting to assess the
extent of the injuries. The goal of treatment is to reduce the severity of any infection that develops. This will be accomplished by
cleaning the wounds, removing any dead or severely damaged tissue, and potentially surgically closing the wounds.
Small puncture wounds will usually be left open so that any infection can drain out. Lacerations will be sutured up; temporary drains
may be placed if the underlying tissues are damaged or if there is a chance that fluid will build up in the area. In many cases, skin
wounds will be enlarged to allow the underlying tissues to be thoroughly examined and cleaned.
If the wound has been left open to heal, or if a drain was placed in the wound, you will need to clean away any draining material on a
regular basis. You can use a clean, soft washcloth or cotton balls with warm water to remove debris. In some cases, your veterinarian
will prescribe a mild disinfectant cleanser (Nolvasan solution) to assist with keeping the area clean. Only use products that are
recommended by your veterinarian. Never use hydrogen peroxide or rubbing alcohol to clean a bite wound. Scratches or mild
abrasions may benefit from a thin layer of triple antibiotic ointment twice daily until healed.
