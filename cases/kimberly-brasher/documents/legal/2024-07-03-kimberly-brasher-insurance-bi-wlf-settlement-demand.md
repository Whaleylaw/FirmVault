1. FACTS & LIABILITY

On February 25, 2023, Ms. Brasher was a lawful invitee upon the commercial premises located at 115 KY-81 in Calhoun, McLean County, Kentucky (the “premises” or “subject premises”). The subject premises was owned and operated by Walgreens (“Walgreens”) in accordance with the previously-provided Agreed Order. As Ms. Brasher was walking in the parking lot, she suddenly and unexpectedly tripped over an unmarked and blacktop-covered speed bump and fell to the ground, landing on her left hip. Ms. Brasher’s encounter with the dangerous condition on the subject premises resulted in her suffering a fractured hip and other significant personal injuries.

The conditions for establishing liability, in this case, are clear. Walgreens, as the commercial owner/operator of the subject premises, was at all times relevant to this matter subject

to the duty of care owed by a commercial premises owner to lawful invitees under Kentucky law. In breaching its duty, Walgreens, directly and/or vicariously, created a dangerous or otherwise hazardous condition that was neither open nor obvious to Ms. Brasher on February 25, 2023, when she fell due to an unmarked parking lot speed bump. Walgreens’ reckless or otherwise negligent failures proximately resulted in Ms. Brasher sustaining substantial personal injuries and other damages for which Walgreens is undoubtedly liable.

A commercial premises owner “has a duty to conduct its activities in such a way as not to expose its business invitees to what in the circumstances would be an unreasonable risk of harm.” West v. KKI, LLC, 300 S.W.3d 184, 190-91 (Ky. Ct. App. 2008) (internal citations omitted) (an “invitee” is one who enters the owner’s premises by express or implied invitation connected with the owner’s business or with “an activity the owner conducts or permits to be conducted” on its premises for mutual benefit or benefit of the owner).

Under common law premises liability principles, the duty owed by the premises owner to an invitee is a general duty to exercise ordinary care to keep the premises in a reasonably safe condition and to warn invitees of dangers that are latent, unknown, or not obvious. Lewis v. B & R Corp., 56 S.W.3d 432, 438 (Ky. Ct. App. 2001). This includes the owner’s duty to invitees to discover the existence of dangerous conditions on the premises and either correct or warn of them. Johnson v. Lone Star Steakhouse & Saloon of Kentucky, Inc., 997 S.W.2d 490, 492 (Ky. Ct. App. 1999).

Here, it is clear that liability rests with Walgreens. Walgreens was negligent when it failed to use reasonable care to keep its premises in a reasonably safe condition, failed to use reasonable care to discover the unsafe condition created by the unmarked speed bump, and failed to exercise reasonable care to protect or warn Ms. Brasher against the dangerous condition at issue. Certainly, a jury will easily glean from the facts and circumstances leading to Ms. Brasher’s




resulting injuries and damages that Walgreens knew or should have known that the dangerous condition upon its premises created a high risk of harm to individuals like Ms. Brasher, and that, had it exercised remedial action in the form of a reasonable, timely inspection for which it had control, or a provided a reasonable warning of the dangerous condition at issue, Walgreens could have prevented Ms. Brasher’s encounter with the dangerous condition upon the subject premises and her resulting harm.

Again, in this instance, Walgreens knew the dangerous condition in its parking lot was present, or that the dangerous condition at issue existed for such a length of time that, had it exercised ordinary care, should have known of the condition, or that the condition occurred with regularity and was therefore foreseeable so as to cause injury to the public. Walgreens’ negligence created an unsafe and dangerous environment that placed the well-being of all its customers at risk. As a direct result of Walgreens’s negligence, Ms. Brasher was severely injured and sustained significant damages for which Walgreens is certainly liable.

The goal of compensatory damages is to make the injured party whole to the extent it is possible to measure the injury in terms of money. Ky. Cent. Ins. Co. v. Schneider, 15 S.W.3d 373, 374 (Ky. 2000). Damages should include fair compensation for any physical or mental suffering caused by the injury. Ky. Cent. Ins. Co. v. The general rule is that the damages must be reasonable, free from sentiment and free from punishment so as not to amount to punitive damages. Noel v. Creary, 385 S.W.2d 951, 953 (Ky. 1965). The probability of future pain and suffering may be considered when calculating damages if the evidence shows that future pain and suffering may stem from the injury. Nussbaum v. Caskey, 235 Ky. 640, 32 S.W.2d 18, 19 (Ky. App. 1930).
