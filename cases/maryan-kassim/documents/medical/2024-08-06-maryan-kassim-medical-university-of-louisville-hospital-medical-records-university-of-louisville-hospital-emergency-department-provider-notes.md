Tooth Displacement
Tooth displacement means that one of your teeth has been moved out of its normal position, but has not fallen out or been knocked out
of your mouth. Tooth displacement is also called luxation. It is different from a tooth that has fallen out, which is called avulsion.
Displacement can happen to a baby tooth (primary tooth) or an adult tooth (permanent tooth). Usually, it happens to the front teeth.
There are three types of tooth displacement:
• Extrusion. This is when the tooth is pulled out of its normal position. The tooth may appear longer.
• Intrusion. This is when the tooth is pushed into the gum. The tooth appears shorter.
• Lateral displacement. This is when the tooth moves in front or behind the normal row of teeth. Teeth that are intruded or
extruded can also be laterally displaced.
What are the causes?
Common causes of this condition include trauma or injury to the mouth or jaw. A rare cause of this condition is disease, such as:
• Gum disease.
• Tooth or bone infections.
• Other diseases that affect parts of the body or the entire body.
What increases the risk?
The following factors may make you more likely to develop this condition:
• Doing activities with high risk of injury. These include football, downhill skiing, or boxing.

Tooth Injuries
Tooth injuries are injuries that happen because a strong force:
• Cracked a tooth.
• Moved a tooth out of place.
• Knocked a tooth out of the mouth.
A tooth injury needs to be treated quickly to save the tooth.
What are the causes?
This condition may be caused by:
• Anything that chips or breaks a tooth.
• Anything that moves a tooth out of its place or knocks it out of the mouth.
The injuries may come from accidents, falls, or fights.
What increases the risk?
• Playing sports without using a mouth guard. These sports include football or boxing.
• Medical conditions that cause falling or fainting.
• Anything that injures the face.
• Medical conditions that weaken the root of the tooth.
What are the signs or symptoms?
The main symptoms of this condition are:
• A tooth that has moved out of its place.
• A tooth that has moved into the gums or out of the gums.
• A tooth that you can no longer see because it is badly broken.
Other symptoms may include:
• Pain when chewing.
• A loose tooth.
• Bleeding in the tooth.
• Swelling or bruising of the tooth or lip.

What is a heart attack?
A heart attack is damage or death to a part of the heart from severely decreased or lack of blood flow to the heart. Over time, arteries
can become narrow from the buildup of fat and cholesterol, which is called plaque. The plaque can rupture causing a blood clot to form.

• Heart attack is the leading cause of death in the United States.
