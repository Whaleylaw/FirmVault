**1.  FACTS  LIABILITY**

On January 1, 2022, Mr. Weber was the restrained driver of a vehicle traveling eastbound on La Grange Road in Louisville, Jefferson County, Kentucky. At the same time, Mr. Fenwick was the driver of a vehicle traveling westbound on La Grange Road. Suddenly and without warning, Mr. Fenwick failed to yield to the right of way and made an unsafe left turn, striking the driver’s side of Mr. Weber’s vehicle. EMS personnel attended the scene and transferred Mr. Weber to Norton Brownsboro for further evaluation of his injuries. The collision resulted in Mr. Weber sustaining significant personal injuries and other damages.

[**NTD**: No police diagram was available.]

As exposed in the Kentucky Uniform Police Traffic Collision Report, Officer Kenneth Koester of the Louisville Metro Police Department investigated the collision and determined that Mr. Fenwick was the sole party responsible for the collision due to his failure to yield to the right of way.

<u>**The conditions for establishing liability, in this case, are clear**</u>. Mr. Fenwick owed a duty of care to the other drivers on the road. Pursuant to **KY Rev Stat § 189.380(1),** “[a] person shall not turn a vehicle or move right or left upon a roadway until the movement can be made with reasonable safety nor without giving an appropriate signal in the manner hereinafter provided.” Further, pursuant to **KY Rev Stat § 189.390(2)**, “[a]n operator of a vehicle upon a highway shall not drive at a greater speed than is reasonable and prudent, having regard for the traffic and for the condition and use of the highway.”

On the date of loss, Mr. Fenwick lacked attentiveness, failed to yield to the right of way, lacked all due care, and caused a collision with Mr. Weber’s vehicle. As a result of Mr. Fenwick’s acts and/or omissions on January 1, 2022, Mr. Weber suffered significant personal injuries and damages for which Mr. Fenwick is undoubtedly liable.

**3.4.  Past and Future Pain and Suffering**

There is no question that the January 1, 2022, incident caused Mr. Weber a great deal of physical and emotional pain and suffering. As a result of the incident, Mr. Weber suffered serious injuries that affected, and continue to affect, his quality of life. Mr. Weber experienced difficulty and pain when trying to complete simple daily activities that he used to be able to complete with ease prior to the January 1, 2022, incident. 

Physically, Mr. Weber’s mobility has been greatly impaired, making it difficult for him to perform basic tasks. Simple household chores such as cooking, cleaning, doing laundry, and taking out the trash have now become arduous and painful. The pain and discomfort Mr. Weber experiences on a daily basis make it nearly impossible for him to carry out these once-routine tasks and have left him reliant on others for support, depriving him of his independence. Furthermore, the inability to participate in activities he once enjoyed, such as socializing with friends and family, has left him feeling isolated and helpless, taking a toll on his mental well-being.

Furthermore, Mr. Weber experiences challenges in getting a restful night’s sleep as he is unable to remain in one position for long without pain and discomfort. This lack of quality sleep has a profound impact on his overall well-being and ability to function during the day. It affects his energy levels, concentration, and mood, making it difficult for him to engage in daily activities and maintain a normal routine.

As such, Mr. Weber’s pain and suffering as a result of the January 1, 2022, incident should remain at the forefront of your consideration as you evaluate his non-economic damages.

The goal of compensatory damages is to make the injured party whole to the extent it is possible to measure the injury in terms of money. *Ky. Cent. Ins. Co. v. Schneider*, 15 S.W.3d 373, 374 (Ky. 2000). Damages should include fair compensation for any physical or mental suffering caused by the injury. *Ky. Cent. Ins. Co. v. Schneider*, 15 S.W.3d 373, 374 (Ky. 1995). There is no set rule for computing damages for pain and suffering. The general rule is that the damages must be reasonable, free from sentiment and free from punishment so as not to amount to punitive damages. *Noel v. Creary*, 385 S.W.2d 951, 953 (Ky. 1965). The probability of future pain and suffering may be considered when calculating damages if the evidence shows that future pain and suffering may stem from the injury. *Nussbaum v. Caskey*, 235 Ky. 640, 32 S.W.2d 18, 19 (Ky. App. 1930).

Liability is clear. Should this case go to a jury, the sole question to answer would be how much money Mr. Weber is entitled to receive under Kentucky law.
