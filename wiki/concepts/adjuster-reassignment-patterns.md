# Adjuster Reassignment Patterns

> Insurance carriers frequently reassign adjusters during the life of a PI claim, sometimes multiple times. Each reassignment resets the negotiation relationship and often causes delays as the new adjuster reviews the file from scratch. In long-running cases, the firm may interact with 3-5 different adjusters on a single claim. Understanding this pattern helps manage expectations and maintain negotiation momentum.

## Pattern

Adjuster reassignment follows observable patterns:

1. **Initial Adjuster**: Handles the claim from opening through liability investigation. May make the first offer.
2. **Mid-Case Reassignment**: Common at 6-12 months. May coincide with carrier restructuring or adjuster caseload rebalancing.
3. **Stale File Reassignment**: When a case has had no movement for extended periods, carriers may reassign to a "closer" adjuster who proactively reaches out to resolve.
4. **New Adjuster Introduction**: New adjusters typically introduce themselves, note they've been "reassigned" to the file, and request updated documentation.
5. **Fresh Eyes Effect**: A new adjuster sometimes brings movement — they re-evaluate the file without the baggage of prior negotiations and may make a new offer that differs from the prior adjuster's position.

### Impact on Negotiations

- Each reassignment adds 2-4 weeks of delay as the new adjuster reviews the file
- New adjusters may not honor informal commitments made by prior adjusters
- A new adjuster may request documentation already provided to the prior adjuster
- However, reassignment can be an opportunity: the new adjuster may be more willing to negotiate than a predecessor who was entrenched in a position

## Evidence

- In Case D, the BI claim went through at least 3 adjusters over 2+ years:
  - Adjuster 1 (initial): Handled the claim during the liability investigation phase, while coverage was still "under review"
  - Adjuster 2: Made the initial offer. After receiving updated records, the claim was "reassigned to a different adjuster" with the hope it would "cause some movement."
  - Adjuster 3: Self-identified as "the new adjuster on this file." Left multiple voicemails proactively reaching out, noting "there really has not been any movement" and "I was hoping to... try to follow up." This adjuster made multiple attempts to reach the attorney over several weeks before connecting.
  - Adjuster 4: Subsequently reassigned again; the new adjuster reviewed additional treatment records and made the carrier's "top offer."
- The Adjuster 3 reassignment was particularly notable: this adjuster was proactive, called multiple times, left detailed voicemails expressing willingness to negotiate, and explicitly said "I think that if we could have a phone conversation about this, we could get it resolved."
- After submitting updated records, the carrier reassigned to Adjuster 4, who raised the offer but also introduced a new defense (attributing treatment to an unrelated slip-and-fall incident).
- In an auto accident case (DOI 9/2021, KY), the UIM claim went through multiple adjuster reassignments at a national insurance carrier. The PIP adjuster, BI adjuster, and UIM adjuster were all separate individuals at different carrier entities. Over the 3+ year case timeline, at least 3-4 adjusters handled the UIM claim track alone. This confirms that adjuster reassignment is not limited to BI claims — UIM adjusters are also subject to frequent reassignment, and the pattern of multiple adjusters per claim track compounds in multi-carrier cases.
- In an auto accident case (DOI 12/2022, KY), the PIP and UM claims were managed through the same carrier but the case saw 7 named adjusters across all tracks (PIP, BI, and UM), including adjusters from the carrier's parent company. The UM adjuster managed the claim through active litigation including discovery completion. This is the highest adjuster count observed per case and confirms that complex multi-track cases generate the most adjuster churn.
- In a small MVA case (DOI 11/2025, KY, file setup phase), 3 different adjusters were named on the case within the first few months: the BI adjuster (Auto Owners Insurance), the PIP adjuster (Progressive), plus an initial BI contact who was replaced before any substantive communication occurred. The case also had the PIP adjuster request recorded statements from both the client and a companion passenger as part of a "proof of loss" investigation. This confirms that adjuster reassignment can happen before the negotiation phase — even during file setup — and that cases with split BI/PIP carriers inherently involve multiple adjuster relationships from day one.
- In a companion case pair (DOI 11/2025, KY), both cases shared the same BI adjuster (State Farm) and the same PIP team (State Farm centralized PIP team at a dedicated extension). The PIP team was distinct from the BI adjuster, confirming the "PIP team" model where PIP adjusting is handled by a centralized team rather than an individual adjuster. This creates a different reassignment dynamic: individual BI adjusters may change, but the PIP team remains constant.

## Agent Guidance

1. When a new adjuster introduces themselves, log their name, direct phone, extension, and email immediately.
2. Proactively send the new adjuster a summary of the case status and any prior correspondence/offers.
3. Treat a new adjuster as an opportunity — respond promptly to their outreach.
4. If a new adjuster calls and leaves a voicemail, return the call within 24 hours (they are motivated to work the file).
5. Keep a chronological log of all adjusters who have handled the claim.
6. When the new adjuster requests documentation, send it promptly rather than assuming they have the prior adjuster's files.
7. Alert the attorney when an adjuster change occurs, as this may be a strategic opportunity to re-engage.

## Pitfalls

- Ignoring a new adjuster's outreach (especially when they are proactively trying to settle)
- Assuming the new adjuster has all documentation from the prior adjuster
- Not updating contact information when a reassignment occurs
- Missing the window of opportunity when a "closer" adjuster is assigned and motivated
- Letting weeks pass without responding to a new adjuster's repeated outreach
