# Adjuster Persistent Unreturned Calls Pattern

> Insurance adjusters sometimes repeatedly call the firm, leaving multiple voicemails over weeks or months without getting a return call. This pattern can occur on both sides: adjusters trying to settle may be unable to reach the attorney, or the firm may deliberately deprioritize a carrier's calls while focused on other aspects of the case. Both directions carry risk.

## Pattern

Two distinct patterns emerge:

### Adjuster-Initiated Persistence
When an adjuster repeatedly calls and leaves voicemails "to discuss settlement," this may indicate:
1. The adjuster has settlement authority and wants to close the file
2. The carrier is under pressure to resolve the claim
3. The adjuster's repeated calls create a documented record of their attempts

### Firm Non-Response
When the firm does not return adjuster calls promptly, this may be because:
1. The attorney is focused on litigation with a different carrier for the same case
2. The case is not ready for negotiation on that particular claim
3. The calls are being logged as "missed calls" without a follow-up task being created
4. Staff turnover means no one is monitoring that specific carrier's communications

## Evidence

- In Case D, the Progressive Insurance adjuster called the attorney repeatedly over a 3-month period (August through October 2025), leaving 7+ voicemails. Each voicemail was transcribed and logged as a "missed call" in Filevine, but no return calls were documented during this period. The voicemails progressively indicated the adjuster wanted "to discuss settlement" and "to talk this one over." Meanwhile, the firm was actively engaged in litigation against a different defendant (West Bend) on the same case. Eventually, the attorney did engage in email correspondence with this adjuster about the claim. The pattern suggests that when a firm is managing multiple parallel tracks, some carriers' outreach can fall through the cracks.

## Agent Guidance

1. Log every adjuster voicemail with the transcription and create a follow-up task
2. Even if the case is not ready for negotiation on that claim, return calls within 48 hours with a brief status update
3. Track the number of unreturned adjuster calls — more than 3 should trigger an alert to the attorney
4. When an adjuster says they want to "discuss settlement," this is a priority callback
5. Not responding to adjuster outreach can result in the adjuster closing the file or reducing their authority
6. If the firm is deliberately delaying engagement with a carrier for strategic reasons, document the attorney's direction to do so

## Pitfalls

- Letting adjuster calls go unreturned for weeks (damages the relationship and may reduce settlement authority)
- Not creating follow-up tasks from missed call logs (the pattern just accumulates without action)
- Assuming the adjuster will keep calling forever (they may close the file or reassign)
- Missing the signal that the adjuster is ready to settle while the firm is focused on litigation elsewhere
- Not distinguishing between strategic non-response (attorney-directed) and operational failure (nobody noticed)

### Batch 3 Evidence (5 additional cases)

- In an MVA case (DOI 1/2023, KY), the PIP adjuster (Shelter Insurance) left 5+ voicemails over a 6-month period, each escalating in urgency. The final voicemails explicitly threatened file closure: "if I do not hear back from anyone from your firm, I am going to go ahead and assume to close this file." The firm had been focused on the BI/Medicare track and was not monitoring PIP adjuster outreach. The adjuster also identified specific blocking issues (need for updated Medicare lien, doctor's release) that the firm could have resolved promptly.
- In an MVA case (DOI 7/2024, KY), the BI adjuster (Travelers) called the firm 5+ times over 2 months requesting the signed settlement release. The adjuster explained in voicemails that the PIP carrier was requesting reimbursement and could not be paid without the signed release — creating carrier-to-carrier pressure from the firm's non-response.
- In 3 additional cases, BI adjusters followed up 2-3 times after demand to confirm receipt or request additional documentation, without receiving timely responses. The standard adjuster follow-up cadence appears to be: initial attempt, 2-week follow-up, 4-week escalation.
