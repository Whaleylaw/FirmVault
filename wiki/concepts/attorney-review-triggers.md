# Attorney Review Triggers

> Certain events in a PI case require the attorney's direct involvement and approval. These are decision points where paralegal-level handling is insufficient and attorney judgment is needed. Identifying and routing these triggers efficiently prevents bottlenecks and ensures quality control.

## Pattern

Attorney review is triggered by:

1. **Initial Case Evaluation**: When a transfer case file arrives, the attorney reviews the prior firm's work, demand status, and financial picture before directing next steps
2. **Stale Case Flag**: When the attorney notices no activity on a case for an extended period (2+ months), they direct staff to check in with the client
3. **Lien Disputes**: When unrelated charges appear on a health insurance lien, the attorney directs which items to dispute
4. **Settlement Authority**: Client gives authority to "negotiate to LBF" (Last Best and Final) — the attorney must approve the negotiation range
5. **Case Complexity Flags**: Pre-settlement funding obligations, minimal treatment, incarcerated clients — these require attorney guidance on strategy
6. **Demand Approval**: Before a demand is sent, the attorney reviews and approves
7. **File Completion Escalation**: When records collection stalls, the attorney escalates to ensure action
8. **Litigation Decision**: When initial offers are inadequate (e.g., BI offer considering only ER visit despite extensive treatment), the attorney must decide whether to file suit
9. **IME Coordination**: When defense orders Independent Medical Examinations, the attorney must review the orders, advise the client, and manage scheduling
10. **Concurrent Claim Strategy**: When a client has injuries from multiple accidents, the attorney must decide apportionment strategy across claims

## Evidence

- In Case A, the attorney reviewed the transfer case and identified key issues: prior demand already sent, treatment gap, pre-settlement funding, and minimal treatment. He then directed staff on next steps.
- In Case B, the attorney flagged the case noting "no activity since the middle of August" and directed a check-in with the client. Later, the attorney changed the phase from Treatment to Demand in Progress.
- In Case C, the attorney identified that unrelated cardiac treatment was included in the Anthem lien and directed its removal. He also escalated when records requests had "no action" for a month.
- In Case D, the attorney intervened at multiple points in a VA lien case: (1) directed paralegal to sort VA records for "relatedness" to separate accident-related from unrelated treatment; (2) asked "What is going on here? Just need chiro records and can send demand" when records collection stalled; (3) ordered "prepare and send the demand" when the case had been pending for over 2 years, making a judgment call to proceed with incomplete VA records; (4) discussed the case with the client directly after the attorney call revealed "multiple other MRIs" and PT through the VA's IPRP program not in the file.
- In Case D (premises liability, litigation), the attorney directed: additional MRI imaging based on IME findings, IME amendment requests when the client's shoulder reached MMI, neurosurgery consultation, economist/vocational report ordering, and managed all litigation events (motions, mediation scheduling, discovery responses). The attorney also directly communicated with defense counsel on mediation logistics and discovery matters.
- In Case E (auto accident), the attorney flagged that there had been "NO attempted contact with client since December" and directed staff to check in. Later, the attorney personally called the adjuster and sent a lien payment request to the carrier. The attorney also had to reverse a premature phase change (Settlement -> Negotiation) when the client refused the offer.
- In Case D (chain-reaction MVA), the attorney was involved at multiple decision points: (1) deciding to file suit after West Bend's initial offer only considered the ER visit despite extensive treatment, (2) directly handling negotiation emails with Progressive's adjuster, (3) coordinating with defense counsel on IME scheduling and deposition dates, (4) responding to a provider's "case status inquiry" about payment prospects, and (5) directing the paralegal to "make sure records/bills in dropbox match what we have." The attorney's direct involvement with opposing counsel increased markedly once litigation was filed.
- In Case F (auto accident, dental injury), the attorney: (1) called the client to discuss the first settlement offer (9/2023), (2) noted the client had "high expectations" wanting $10K+ net, (3) directed staff to follow up on the Anthem lien and check ZipLiens, (4) directed updated dental records requests after continued treatment, (5) forwarded client voicemails to the associate for return calls during litigation. The attorney delegated deposition scheduling to the associate but stayed involved in strategic decisions.
- In Case G (auto accident, UIM), the attorney: (1) personally called the client to discuss the case and provided a detailed call summary, (2) directed UIM complaint filing, (3) sent courtesy copy of the UIM complaint to the adjuster, and (4) escalated when records requests stalled. The attorney's direct call to the client, with a written summary sent to the case file, demonstrates a best practice for attorney-client contact.
- In Case H (auto accident, coverage denial), the attorney: (1) engaged directly with the BI adjuster to challenge the stolen vehicle coverage denial, providing evidence the vehicle wasn't reported stolen until after the accident, (2) made the litigation decision based on a "Litigation Review" when coverage remained unresolved, (3) granted defense counsel a 60-day extension to answer while they attempted to get coverage accepted, (4) agreed during an EUO that certain bills would not be submitted to KAC, and (5) discussed deposition scheduling strategy with the paralegal team. This case required more attorney involvement at the intake/file setup stage than typical cases due to the coverage dispute.

## Agent Guidance

1. Route the following to the attorney immediately:
   - New transfer case files received (with summary memo)
   - Settlement offers received
   - Lien itemizations with potential disputes
   - Client requests for case status update (if case has been inactive 30+ days)
   - Bills going to collections
   - Pre-settlement funding documentation
2. When routing to the attorney, include a brief summary and recommended action
3. Track attorney-pending items and follow up if no response within 48 hours
4. Document the attorney's direction in the activity log verbatim

## Pitfalls

- Not routing complex issues to the attorney (trying to handle them at paralegal level)
- Routing too many routine items to the attorney (creating bottlenecks)
- Not including sufficient context when routing (attorney has to dig through the file)
- Not following up on items pending attorney review
- Not documenting the attorney's direction
