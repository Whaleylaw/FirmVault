# Attorney Withdrawal from Active Litigation

> When a case is in active litigation and the attorney-client relationship breaks down, the attorney cannot simply close the file — they must file a Motion to Withdraw with the court. This is a more complex closing process than pre-litigation decline because the firm is counsel of record. Client non-cooperation (especially failure to appear for depositions) is the primary trigger observed. Defense counsel may simultaneously file motions for sanctions and dismissal.

## Pattern

### Withdrawal Triggers in Litigation
1. **Client fails to appear for deposition**: Despite multiple reminders (texts, calls, in-person delivery), the client does not show up
2. **Client becomes unreachable during litigation**: Cannot be contacted to coordinate required appearances
3. **Client explicitly terminates**: Client fires the firm during active litigation

### Withdrawal Process
1. **Attorney decision**: Attorney determines the relationship is no longer viable
2. **Motion to Withdraw filed**: Formal motion filed with the circuit court
3. **Service on client**: Withdrawal letter served on the client (may require physical delivery to client's address)
4. **Court hearing**: Motion set for hearing at motion hour (typically 2-3 weeks out)
5. **Defense response**: Defense counsel may or may not object; may simultaneously file their own motions
6. **Order entered**: Court grants withdrawal; case proceeds with client pro se or is dismissed

### Defense Counsel Response
- Defense counsel may file a Motion to Dismiss for Failure to Prosecute
- Defense counsel may seek sanctions (costs for wasted deposition, travel)
- Defense counsel may note explicitly that "we do not think counsel did anything wrong" — directing sanctions at the client rather than the attorney
- Defense counsel typically does not object to the withdrawal itself

## Evidence

- In a litigated MVA case (DOI 12/2023, KY, Union Circuit Court), the client failed to appear for his scheduled deposition at the Union County Public Library. The attorney had:
  - Called the client 3 times the day before the deposition
  - Texted: "If you fail to appear for your deposition in the morning it will have a negative impact on your case... If you are unable to obtain transportation, please let me know, I will be in Union County around 8:30 in the morning"
  - Called 4+ times the morning of the deposition
  - Texted: "We are at the library for your deposition. Please advise if you need transportation"
  - The client did not respond or appear
  - The attorney filed a Motion to Withdraw with the court the next day, set for motion hour on 7/14/25
  - Defense counsel responded: "We will be filing a Motion to Dismiss for Failure to Prosecute and for costs related to the deposition. We will be seeking this sanction against Plaintiff directly and noting that we do not think counsel did anything wrong."
  - The withdrawal letter was physically "left at client door"
  - The case was changed from Litigation to Closing status
  - This case had already involved extensive litigation effort: prior discovery, defense counsel correspondence, and the attorney traveling to a rural county (Union County) for the deposition

## Agent Guidance

1. When a client fails to appear for a deposition, immediately notify the attorney
2. Document ALL outreach attempts (calls, texts, physical delivery) with timestamps — these become evidence supporting the Motion to Withdraw
3. If the attorney decides to withdraw, prepare the Motion to Withdraw for filing
4. Serve the withdrawal letter on the client via the most reliable method available (mail, email, AND physical delivery)
5. Notify defense counsel of the Motion to Withdraw and provide a copy
6. Continue tracking the case until the court grants the Motion — the firm remains counsel of record until the order is entered
7. After withdrawal is granted, follow the standard closing checklist (notify carriers, lien holders, archive documents)

## Pitfalls

- Not documenting outreach attempts before the deposition (weakens the Motion to Withdraw)
- Not filing the Motion to Withdraw promptly after the breakdown (leaves the firm exposed to defense motions)
- Not serving the client with the withdrawal letter (procedural deficiency)
- Assuming the case is closed before the court grants the Motion (firm is still counsel of record)
- Not notifying defense counsel of the withdrawal promptly
