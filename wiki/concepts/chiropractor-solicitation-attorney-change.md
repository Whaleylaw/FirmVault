# Chiropractor Solicitation Driving Attorney Changes

> Chiropractors who treat PI clients may solicit those clients to switch attorneys, typically to a firm with which the chiropractor has a referral relationship. This creates a distinct closing pathway where the client does not terminate due to dissatisfaction with legal services but is actively recruited away during treatment. The firm must respond quickly to assert lien rights and send proper disengagement letters.

## Pattern

1. **Client begins chiropractic treatment** through a provider recommended or self-selected during the treatment phase
2. **Chiropractor solicits the client** to switch to a different attorney — the chiropractor likely has a referral arrangement with the new firm
3. **Client notifies the firm** they are "going with another chiropractor and attorney"
4. **Firm asserts lien**: The case is moved to a "Lien" phase (distinct from standard Closing), and the firm sends a disengagement letter that explicitly preserves attorney lien rights
5. **Disengagement letter sent to all parties**: BI carrier, PIP carrier, and new attorney are all notified of the firm's lien
6. **Standard closing sequence follows**: Closing letter, GDrive archival, lien monitoring

### Key Differences from Standard Decline
- The client is not dissatisfied with legal representation per se — they are being recruited by the treatment provider
- The firm has already invested time in file setup, insurance claims, and records collection
- The firm has a legitimate attorney lien for work performed
- Companion cases (multiple passengers from the same MVA) may all switch simultaneously

## Evidence

- In two companion MVA cases (DOI 9/2024, KY), the driver and a passenger both called to say they were "going with another chiropractor and attorney." The paralegal confirmed: "clients were solicited by the chiropractor to retain a new attorney." The firm moved both cases from Treatment to "Lien" status, then sent disengagement letters to the BI carrier explicitly noting the firm's lien and the circumstances: "Please be advised that the clients were solicited by the chiropractor to retain a new attorney. Additionally, we have a lien for them." The firm then moved both to Closing with "Outstanding Liens Noted at Closing. Please Review for Payment Processing."
- In the companion case, the firm also had to notify Conduent (Medicaid lien holder) that they no longer represented the client, ensuring lien correspondence was redirected.
- In a separate case, a competing firm (Accident Injury) was identified as soliciting the firm's clients during treatment, prompting the attorney to be notified via internal note.
- In another case, the client texted that she "already had someone to take the case previously" — while not chiropractor-driven, this demonstrates the broader pattern of clients being recruited by other entities during open cases.

## Agent Guidance

1. When a client reports they are switching attorneys, immediately escalate to the attorney
2. Determine whether the switch was client-initiated or solicited by a third party (chiropractor, another firm)
3. If solicited, document the solicitation in the activity log — this may be relevant for bar complaint purposes
4. Move the case to "Lien" phase before Closing to ensure lien rights are preserved
5. Send disengagement letter to all parties (client, BI carrier, PIP carrier, lien holders, new attorney) that explicitly references the firm's attorney lien
6. If companion cases exist, check whether all companions are switching — the solicitation often targets all plaintiffs from the same MVA
7. Set a follow-up task to monitor whether the lien is satisfied when the case ultimately settles with new counsel

## Pitfalls

- Not asserting the attorney lien before sending the disengagement letter
- Not notifying all carriers and lien holders of the representation change
- Not documenting the solicitation (bar authorities may investigate the chiropractor-attorney referral arrangement)
- Assuming companion case clients are also switching without confirming individually
- Forgetting to notify Conduent or other government lien holders of the representation change
