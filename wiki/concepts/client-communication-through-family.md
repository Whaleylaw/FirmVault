# Client Communication Through Family Members

> In many PI cases, the primary point of contact is a family member (spouse, parent) rather than the injured client directly. This pattern creates both efficiencies (an engaged advocate) and risks (communication gaps, authorization requirements). Managing these relationships requires clear protocols around authorization, documentation, and expectation-setting.

## Pattern

Two common scenarios emerge:

**Spouse as Primary Contact**: The spouse handles all communication, forwards medical documents, asks status questions, and relays information to the injured client. The injured client may have signed the retainer but is less directly involved in day-to-day case management.

**Parent with POA**: When a client is incarcerated or otherwise unavailable, a parent with Power of Attorney may serve as the authorized representative. The POA document must be obtained and verified before the firm can discuss case details.

In both scenarios, the family member tends to:
- Be more proactive about asking for updates
- Forward bills and medical documents as they arrive
- Express urgency about resolution ("we want this to go away")
- Ask about the impact of unpaid bills on credit

## Evidence

- In Case B, the client's husband was the primary communicator throughout the entire case. He forwarded all medical bills, relayed appointment results, asked about case status repeatedly, and expressed concern about bills going to collections. The client herself was largely absent from communications.
- In Case A, the client was incarcerated and the client's mother (with POA) was the sole point of contact. The mother provided intake information, the POA document, and followed up on case status.
- In Case H (MVA, air evac), the client designated a go-between ("Anna"/Ann Dattillo) as the intermediary for all communications. The firm was instructed to communicate through Anna "per Client instructions." Anna was added as an associated contact. She requested copies of the deposition and case updates. When the attorney changed (Bryce Koon departed), the new attorney had to re-establish the communication chain through Anna. The client herself called separately, creating confusion about who was the primary contact.
- In Case G (MVA, companion cases), the client was the father managing his own case AND his daughter's companion case. He needed to coordinate daughter's medical records, hospital visits, and communicate on her behalf. Multiple calls over months tried to get the companion's hospital records — the father was asked to speak to the daughter and obtain them, but this remained incomplete for 6+ months.
- In Case H (MVA), initial intake was through a witness/companion ("Robert Grant") who called in. He was given the office number and told the client's case manager name. He provided pictures and information on behalf of the client. This shows that in some cases, even the initial referral comes through a third party.
- In a wrongful death MVA case (DOI 8/2023, KY), the decedent's family served as the primary contacts. The daughter-in-law and husband were identified as points of contact during intake. Communication went through the husband's phone number and the daughter-in-law's contact info. The firm coordinated with family members about the collision reconstruction expert, lien management (including a $89K+ union trust lien), and court filings. The family member pattern in wrongful death cases is distinct from the standard proxy pattern — the family members are the actual clients (as estate representatives) rather than mere intermediaries, and their emotional investment in a death case adds significant communication sensitivity.
- In Case N (premises liability estate, DOI 7/2020), the injured client died and the surviving spouse became the sole point of contact for the estate case. The spouse handled all communications, called for updates repeatedly, completed probate inventory, and relayed information from the home health provider that corrected a records request error (wrong provider name). The spouse showed classic frustration escalation — calling for updates, being told the attorney would call back, then calling again when no callback came. This is a distinct variant of the family-as-contact pattern: when the client has died, the family member is not just a proxy but the actual party (as estate representative), adding both legal and emotional complexity.

## Agent Guidance

1. During intake, identify who the primary point of contact will be
2. If it's a family member, verify authorization: POA for non-spouse, general authorization for spouse
3. Document the authorized contact in the case file with their contact info
4. Set expectations with the family member about case timeline and the process
5. When the family member forwards bills/records, acknowledge receipt promptly and explain next steps
6. When the family member expresses urgency, respond with specific status updates rather than vague reassurances
7. If bills are going to collections, explain the process (PIP submission, timeline) proactively

## Pitfalls

- Discussing case details with a family member who has not been properly authorized
- Assuming the family member is accurately relaying information to/from the client
- Not setting expectations with the family member about realistic case timelines
- Ignoring repeated follow-up inquiries from family members (this erodes trust)
- Not having a clear protocol for who can authorize settlement decisions
