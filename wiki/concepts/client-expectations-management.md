# Client Expectations Management

> PI clients (and their family members) frequently become anxious about case timelines, especially when medical bills go to collections. Proactively managing expectations about the process, timeline, and their role prevents client dissatisfaction and premature settlement pressure.

## Pattern

Client/family anxiety follows a predictable escalation:

1. **Early Phase (0-3 months)**: Client is patient, providing information as requested
2. **Mid Phase (3-6 months)**: Client begins asking "Is there anything we need to be doing?" and "Are we close to resolving this?"
3. **Late Phase (6+ months)**: Client receives collection notices and expresses urgency — "I don't want this to hurt our credit" and "We want this to go away"
4. **Critical Point**: Client may pressure for quick settlement at any amount to end the process

The firm's response pattern is typically reactive: responding to inquiries with brief status updates. Proactive communication is rare in the observed data.

## Evidence

- In Case B, the client's husband followed up approximately monthly with increasing urgency. His communications escalated from "Just checking in" (August) to "Do you feel like we are close?" (November) to "We received a letter from a debt collection agency... We want this to go away" (November) to "When can we reach a resolution? We really want to put this to bed" (December).
- In Case A, the client's mother followed up asking "he was asking if I have heard anything about his case" after months of apparent inactivity.
- In Case D, the client left repeated voicemails over a 2-month period (Nov-Dec 2025) that went unanswered, escalating from "trying to find out if there has been an update" to "I have been calling every single day and still have not had a telephone call back." The client explicitly stated "it has been over 3 years" and "I just want everything settled... with whatever" — indicating willingness to accept any amount to end the process. This is the most extreme observed case of client frustration from communication failure.
- In Case D (auto accident, negotiation), the client was told the case was "on settlement phase" and that they "can't disclose the amount yet because it might still change." The client then refused the settlement, creating a confusing situation where staff noted "appears was settled but client didn't accept so maybe ongoing negotiations." This demonstrates the danger of using vague settlement language without specifics.
- In Case E (premises liability, litigation spanning 3+ years), the client texted the attorney directly: "Haven't heard anything from you or an update since court on Monday, and just wanted to reach out." The attorney responded with a substantive update about next steps (records production deadline). This case shows that even in litigation, clients need regular updates after court events.

## Agent Guidance

1. Set expectations at intake: explain the typical case timeline (6-18 months for most PI cases)
2. Send proactive status updates at least monthly, even if the update is "waiting for records"
3. When bills go to collections, explain immediately: "We have submitted this to PIP for processing. It may take up to 30 days. The collection activity will be resolved through the settlement."
4. When a client asks "are we close?", give a specific answer: "We need X, Y, and Z before we can send the demand. I expect that to take approximately N weeks."
5. Never leave a client inquiry unanswered for more than 24 hours
6. Document client expectations and concerns in the activity log

## Pitfalls

- Being reactive instead of proactive with client communication
- Giving vague responses like "Not at this time" without explaining what the timeline looks like
- Ignoring the emotional impact of collection notices on clients
- Allowing months to pass without any client contact
- Not explaining the process steps that remain before resolution
