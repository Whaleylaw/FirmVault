# Client Frustration Escalation Pattern

> PI clients exhibit a predictable pattern of escalating frustration when they feel the firm is not communicating with them. This pattern typically begins with polite check-ins, escalates to demands for immediate response, and can culminate in threats to change attorneys. Preventing this escalation through proactive communication is more effective than responding to it after it begins.

## Pattern

The escalation follows a predictable trajectory:

### Stage 1: Polite Inquiry
- Client texts or calls asking for a general update
- "I would like to know where we are on this case"
- "Just checking in"

### Stage 2: Urgent Demand
- Client's tone shifts to urgency
- "Hey are you going to get back with me or do I need to take another route. Let me know today"
- Multiple calls in a single day (3+ calls on same day observed)
- Voicemails become longer and more frustrated

### Stage 3: Threats and Distrust
- Client explicitly threatens to leave
- "I am not settling for what you are"
- "You keep in touch with me or nothing"
- "If you will today, you just please call me back today and just give me something"
- Client may call multiple staff members when the attorney is unavailable

### Stage 4: Repeated Unanswered Attempts
- Client calls are logged as "missed calls" repeatedly over weeks/months
- Voicemail transcriptions become shorter and more resigned
- Client may call different staff members (attorney, paralegal, receptionist)

### Key Triggers
- Long periods of no proactive contact from the firm
- Settlement offer communicated without full explanation
- Being told the case is "on settlement" without a timeline
- Calls going unreturned or to voicemail

- In an auto accident case (negotiation phase), the client's frustration escalated over 12+ months:
  - 6/2024: "Hey are you going to get back with me or do I need to take another route. Let me know today"
  - 3/2025: Voicemail — "will you please get back in contact with me... you keep in touch with me or nothing... call me back today and just give me something. I told you I am not settling"
  - 3/2025-3/2026: Multiple missed calls logged (3/21/2025 alone had 2 missed calls + 1 outbound call + texts)
  - 8/2025: Multiple missed calls to different staff members
  - 2/2026, 3/2026: Continued missed calls with empty voicemail transcriptions
- In a premises liability case, the client texted the attorney directly: "Haven't heard anything from you or an update since court on Monday, and just wanted to reach out" — followed by the attorney providing a substantive update about next steps.
- In Case H (MVA, air evac, 2.5+ year case), the client communicated through a go-between (Ann Dattillo). Client called for updates repeatedly — "said she had not heard from anyone recently." Client was confused about deposition process ("said that her attorney was to come out to her residence"). After attorney departure, client needed reassurance about new point of contact. The intermediary requested a copy of the deposition and case update. This shows Stage 2-3 frustration compounded by staff turnover.
- In Case I (MVA, disputed liability, litigation), the client's calls went unreturned for extended periods — 4 missed calls logged between Feb-Mar 2026 with no callback documented. Client had previously been responsive and cooperative (provided provider info via text, completed deposition). The communication breakdown occurred post-deposition when the case was in a waiting period. This confirms the pattern that frustration peaks during litigation gaps.

The post-deposition waiting period triggered escalating frustration from both the client and a family proxy.

The client texted "Good Afternoon Mr. [attorney]" and "Is your office open today" — showing politeness that may mask frustration. The reverse pattern (firm unreachable to client) is equally damaging.

The firm made periodic outbound calls, but the volume of unreturned client calls suggests significant frustration.

The voicemail was forwarded internally, and the attorney returned the call the same day. This demonstrates that even when substantive litigation activity is ongoing (depositions being scheduled), clients still escalate if they are not proactively informed.

The pattern was remarkably persistent — multiple calls per week with no voicemail content logged, suggesting either hang-ups or voicemail not recorded. The emotional dimension is heightened — the spouse is dealing with both grief and the legal process. The 5+ year timeline makes sustained communication even more critical.
- In Case T (premises liability, DOI 9/2023), the client made repeated missed calls to the attorney over a concentrated period (Feb-Mar 2026) — 7+ missed calls in approximately 6 weeks. The calls came in pairs (back-to-back within minutes), suggesting the client was calling, not getting through, and immediately redialing. The case was in active negotiation with defense counsel during this period, confirming that clients escalate even when the case IS moving — the issue is the client doesn't know it's moving.
- In Case U (auto accident, employer liability/taxi company, DOI 9/2024), the client made an extraordinary number of missed calls — 8+ in the last 6 weeks of the activity log alone (Jan-Mar 2026), including back-to-back calls within minutes. The case involved bifurcated litigation issues and a Medicare lien, adding complexity. The client's frustration is notable because the case was relatively recent (DOI just 17 months prior), suggesting that litigation complexity and Medicare bureaucracy trigger faster frustration escalation even on newer cases.
- In Case V (auto accident, closed case, DOI 12/2024), the client made multiple missed calls in September 2025 and continued calling even after the settlement phase. The client expressed frustration about still receiving medical bills despite the case progressing, asking the firm to help with bill payment. The firm told the client to pay bills and submit receipts for reimbursement from PIP. This shows a unique frustration trigger: clients who are being contacted by bill collectors while their case is in settlement/disbursement — the frustration is not about case progress but about the tangible financial pain of unpaid bills.
- In a small MVA case (DOI 8/2025, negotiation phase), the client left a voicemail for the managing attorney after approximately 8 months of case activity, stating: "I have had a case going on with [paralegal] for several months now, like 8 months. I have not been able to get a hold of them for pretty much the past 2 months and I am trying to see what is going on with my case or at least get a call back from somebody." This confirms the Stage 2-3 escalation pattern: client bypasses the assigned paralegal and calls the attorney directly when the paralegal is unresponsive. - In a recent MVA case (DOI 2/2026, treatment/demand phase), the client left a voicemail: "I have been trying to call you and get in contact with you or somebody and I can not get through. If you could call me when you get a chance... I would greatly appreciate it." The voicemail transcription showed the client attempted to reach the firm by calling both the main number and the managing attorney directly. ## Agent Guidance

1. Set automated 2-week check-in reminders for every active case in demand/negotiation phase
2. When a client calls and you cannot provide an update, say WHEN they can expect one (e.g., "I will have an update by Friday")
3. If you cannot reach the client (their voicemail is full or not set up), send a text immediately
4. When a client shows Stage 2 behavior, escalate to the attorney for a personal call within 24 hours
5. Never tell a client the case is "on settlement" unless the client has already agreed to the amount
6. After every client contact, set the next follow-up date in the system
7. Document all contact attempts — even missed calls — in the activity log
8. When multiple missed calls from one client occur in a day, treat it as an urgent response needed

## Pitfalls

- Letting client calls go unreturned for days/weeks
- Client voicemail being full (preventing you from leaving a message) without pivoting to text
- Staff saying "I'm not sure on status" when client calls — everyone on the case should know the current status
- Responding to frustration with vague reassurances instead of specific next steps
- Not escalating frustrated clients to the attorney
- Allowing the pattern to reach Stage 4 before taking action
