# Client-Initiated Termination of Representation

> In some cases, the client fires the firm rather than the firm declining the case. This can happen at any phase — from early file setup through active litigation. The termination is typically communicated via email or text, and the firm must immediately change the case status to Closing, send the standard closing letter, and notify all carriers and lien holders. This pattern is distinct from firm-initiated decline (coverage gaps, client MIA) because the client is an active participant who has decided to leave.

## Pattern

### Termination Triggers
1. **Client dissatisfaction with progress**: Client perceives the case is not moving forward
2. **Client found another attorney**: Client has already retained new counsel
3. **Client no longer wants to pursue**: Client decides the case is not worth continuing
4. **Client disagrees with case strategy**: Client wants to pursue a different type of claim than what the firm is willing to handle

### Termination Communication
- Clients typically terminate via email, using language like:
  - "I am writing to officially notify you that I am terminating your services immediately"
  - "I'm no longer going to need you going any further"
  - "I appreciate your help... give me a call to discuss further action"
- Some terminations are polite and forward-looking; others are curt and immediate
- Guardian/parent may terminate on behalf of a minor client

### Firm Response
1. Immediately change case status to "Closing"
2. Standard closing task fires: "Send Closing letter to Client"
3. Notify all carriers (BI, PIP) and lien holders
4. Do NOT contest the termination — comply immediately
5. If in litigation, file a Motion to Withdraw with the court

## Evidence

- In a minor's MVA case (DOI 11/2024), the guardian (mother) emailed the firm: "I am writing you today for my minor behalf... to let you know I no longer need your service. I am writing to officially notify you that I am terminating your services immediately. You no longer represent me for this accident injury claim." The firm changed status to Closing the next business day and sent closing letters.
- In a WC/MVA case (DOI 8/2025), the client emailed: "This is [Client]. I'm no longer going to need you going any further. I appreciate your help in getting me represented and looking forward to working with you in the future if need be." The firm changed status to Closing the same day. Notably, this client had only been with the firm for 6 days (intake 8/6, termination 8/8).
- In a litigated MVA case (DOI 12/2023), the client became uncooperative during litigation — failing to appear for his scheduled deposition despite the attorney texting and calling 5+ times the day before and morning of. The attorney filed a Motion to Withdraw with the court. Defense counsel then filed a Motion to Dismiss for Failure to Prosecute and sought sanctions against the client directly, noting "we do not think counsel did anything wrong." The withdrawal letter was physically left at the client's door. This represents an involuntary termination driven by client non-cooperation rather than explicit client request.
- In a settled MVA case (DOI 1/2024), the client already had an attorney (another firm handling her case). She signed intake documents in error. The firm declined same-day upon discovering the existing representation.

## Agent Guidance

1. When a client sends a termination notice, do NOT try to talk them out of it — acknowledge and process immediately
2. Change case status to "Closing" the same day the termination is received
3. Follow the complete closing checklist: closing letter to client, copies to all carriers and lien holders
4. If the case is in litigation, immediately prepare a Motion to Withdraw and serve on all parties
5. Preserve all case documents — the client or their new attorney may request the file
6. Log the termination communication in the activity log with the exact date and method
7. Do NOT continue working the case after receiving the termination notice

## Pitfalls

- Delaying the closing process after receiving termination (creates liability)
- Not notifying carriers and lien holders promptly (they may continue directing communications to the firm)
- Not filing a Motion to Withdraw in litigated cases (leaves the firm as counsel of record)
- Assuming the client will change their mind and not processing the termination
- Not preserving the termination communication in the file (needed for potential malpractice defense)
