# Companion Case Management

> When multiple family members are injured in the same accident, each client requires a separate case file but the cases must be coordinated. Companion cases share the same defendant, insurance carriers, and litigation timeline, but each plaintiff has independent medical treatment, damages, and settlement negotiations. The primary client (typically the parent/driver) often serves as the coordinator for all cases.

## Pattern

1. **Intake**: One family member signs up first; firm discovers additional family members during the welcome call or initial contact
2. **File Creation**: Each companion gets a separate case file with separate intake documents
3. **Coordination**: Parent/primary client is expected to facilitate the companion's document signing, provide their information (SSN, medical providers, insurance)
4. **Records Collection**: Each companion's medical providers must be identified and records requested independently — this often falls on the primary client to facilitate
5. **Litigation**: Companions may be joined in one lawsuit (e.g., "Plaintiff A et al. vs. Defendant") or filed separately. Depositions are scheduled sequentially for companions
6. **Settlement**: Each companion's settlement is evaluated independently, but mediation may address all cases simultaneously

The primary client was asked repeatedly over 6+ months to obtain Children's Hospital records for the companion but failed to do so. Both cases were joined in one lawsuit (Boone 24-CI-00452). During mediation via Zoom, both cases received separate LBF (last best & final) offers. Mediation failed for both.
- In Case I (MVA), a companion witness (Dana Jackson) was deposed alongside the client. Depositions were scheduled sequentially via Zoom — the client was told "we are going to do Dana's deposition first so just chill and I will shoot you a text when we are about done." Coordinating two depositions on the same day required active text communication to manage timing.
- In 2 cases from this batch, companion cases were identified at intake: one MVA had a driver and passenger (mother/son) treated at the same ER, creating parallel files with shared BI carrier but separate demand tracks. The paralegal sent demands referencing both clients' records to the same BI adjuster. In another, a companion case was noted but the intake was sent to the companion "via vinesign" through a family member contact. Companion cases with shared chiropractic providers can have records/billing returned together, requiring manual separation.
- In an MVA case (multi-plaintiff, Floyd County IN), three plaintiffs from the same accident were joined in one lawsuit. The complaint was filed as a multi-plaintiff action. Deposition scheduling required coordination across all plaintiffs through court reporting services. Mediation was being scheduled for all cases simultaneously. The case crossed state lines (KY firm filing in IN), adding jurisdictional complexity for companion case coordination. Two of the settled BI claims were resolved separately per plaintiff, confirming that settlement amounts are independent even in joint litigation.
- In Case M (MVA, multi-plaintiff), three additional passengers in the same vehicle were each represented by different law firms (4 total plaintiffs). The at-fault driver's insurance tendered policy limits (25/50) to be divided among all four plaintiffs. The coordination challenge shifted from records collection to inter-counsel distribution: the firm had to email all three other firms to negotiate allocation. This process stalled for months (Jan-Apr 2024+) as counsel for one co-plaintiff was slow to provide medical bills needed for allocation. The delay frustrated the client, who called repeatedly asking for updates and was told "we are still waiting on a reply on the one person we're waiting for medical bills." When distribution could not be agreed upon, the case moved to litigation with all plaintiffs joined in one lawsuit. Depositions for multiple plaintiffs in the joint suit were coordinated through defense counsel.
- In a bus accident case (DOI 7/2024), two passengers (companions) were injured in the same incident. Both were filed as separate cases but shared the same defendant (commercial transportation company + unknown driver) and the same insurance carrier. Settlement demands were sent for both companions simultaneously. Defense counsel corresponded about both clients in single email threads (\"Abby Sitgraves & Nayram Adadevoh\"). The companion filing was confirmed as \"et al\" in the complaint (Jefferson 25-CI-000133). Each companion had independent medical providers and separate health insurance liens. The out-of-state companion's records collection required different providers than the primary client.
- In an MVA case (DOI 10/2023), the companion relationship was between spouses. The complaint was filed as a companion case in the same jurisdiction. Both clients shared PIP coverage but had separate BI claims. The firm tracked SOL independently for each companion.
- In an MVA case (negotiation phase), a non-English-speaking client had a companion case that was not yet uploaded. The case manager noted they would "wait for the companions case to be uploaded and will make it as the point of contact," indicating that the companion case setup was blocking the primary's file setup. The companion case was referenced by a co-counsel attorney negotiating jointly, with three attorneys (from three firms) coordinating a multi-party negotiation with the same BI carrier. This shows that companion cases can involve inter-firm coordination, not just intra-firm coordination.
- In an MVA case (litigation phase), three passengers from the same vehicle (client plus two companions) were represented by the same firm in a multi-plaintiff action filed in Floyd County. The lawsuit was styled as "et al. v. [Defendant]" and depositions were coordinated across all plaintiffs. Multiple BI carriers and a PIP carrier were involved, with each companion having independent claims against potentially different carriers. The case confirmed that companion case mediation scheduling requires all plaintiffs' availability.
- In an MVA case (closed/settlement), two spouses were injured and filed companion cases against the same BI carrier, with shared PIP and UIM coverage through the same insurer. The firm sent records/billing for both clients to the BI adjuster in the same communication, and followed up on offers for both simultaneously. The BI claim settled first, triggering a COOTS letter, while the UIM track continued separately. This demonstrates the companion case pattern where one claim type settles while others remain open across the same companion set.
- In an MVA case (treatment phase), the case involved 3 separate BI carriers (two at-fault drivers plus a third carrier), with liability accepted by one carrier while negotiations continued with others. The multi-carrier structure meant that each companion's recovery depended on different liability determinations from different carriers, adding complexity beyond typical companion cases that share a single defendant.
- In 3 companion case pairs from this batch (DOIs 12/2025 and 6/2025), companion cases shared the same accident, same BI carrier (Progressive), and same PIP carrier (Kentucky Farm Bureau) with the same claim numbers and adjusters. Each companion had independent medical providers — one companion treated at a physical therapy clinic while the other had orthopedic treatment. File setup for both companions occurred on the same day with overlapping insurance claim creation. This confirms that companion cases with shared carriers should have simultaneous file setup to avoid delays, and that PIP claims can share a single claim number across companions.

## Agent Guidance

1. During intake, ask whether other family members were in the vehicle and injured
2. Create separate case files for each companion immediately — do not delay
3. Send intake documents to companions as early as possible
4. Track records collection for each companion independently
5. When the primary client is the gateway to companion information, set explicit deadlines and follow up
6. For litigation, coordinate with the attorney on whether to file jointly or separately
7. Schedule depositions for companions on the same day when possible to minimize coordination burden
8. During mediation, prepare separate settlement evaluations for each companion

## Pitfalls

- Discovering companion cases days/weeks after the primary intake (records requests are delayed)
- In Case R (auto accident, DOI 4/2023), the client was identified as a companion case at intake — a note explicitly stated "Companion: [family member]" with a link to the related Filevine project. Both clients were joined in the same lawsuit. Depositions were scheduled separately for both parties at the same defense counsel's office. The companion's case had a distinct demand amount. Both depositions included the defendant, confirming that companion case litigation coordinates plaintiff, companion, and defendant depositions. The case also demonstrates that companion cases may require separate demand evaluations (the companion's demand was for $5K on minor injuries while the related case could have a different amount).
- Relying on the primary client to obtain companion's information without explicit deadlines
- Not creating a separate case file (tracking companion in notes on primary case)
- Not requesting companion's medical records independently (assuming they're covered by the primary request)
- Scheduling conflicts when companions need to attend the same deposition/mediation session

- In a multi-passenger MVA case (DOI 11/2025), four family members (two adults, two minor children) were involved in a rear-end collision. The BI and PIP claims shared claim numbers across all companions. The PIP adjuster (at Progressive) handled all four claimants in a single email thread, completing coverage investigation and issuing individualized payment decisions per claimant. Recorded interviews with the adult passengers were required as "proof of loss" for the minors' PIP claims. This confirms that companion cases with minor children require adult passengers to provide statements as proxy evidence. The PIP adjuster denied specific billing codes differently per companion (different denied CPT codes for each child vs. each adult), demonstrating that even within the same accident, PIP payment analysis is individualized per claimant.
- In the same case, the companion relationship was parent-children: one parent's phone number served as the contact for all four cases, and the firm used that single number for all communication. Reservation of Rights letters were issued for the minors but not the adults, creating a split coverage status across companions from the same accident.
- In an MVA companion pair (DOI 11/2025), a father (Sr) and son were injured in the same accident. Both cases shared identical BI and PIP claim numbers with the same State Farm adjusters. Both were treated at the same hospital ER and referred to the same chiropractic provider. Records and bills were collected independently per companion, and both reached "all records received" status around the same time. Both cases entered the demand phase simultaneously, confirming that companion cases with aligned treatment timelines should target synchronized demand preparation. The son's case had bills requested and received on the same day (same-day turnaround from the chiropractor), suggesting that when one companion's records are ready, the other's may be as well from shared providers.
- In an MVA case (DOI 9/2024), a mother and daughter were companion cases. The daughter's case was noted as "see companion file (mother)" at intake. Both shared a PIP carrier and the same BI carrier (Root Auto Insurance). During negotiation, the BI adjuster reviewed the PIP log for the daughter and found that "what PIP has paid is significantly higher than the records from the demand show," requesting additional records. The adjuster then sent "increased offers for your clients" (plural), indicating that companion case offer adjustments can be communicated in a single thread. This confirms the pattern that companion cases sharing a BI adjuster may receive coordinated offer updates.

### Batch 4 Evidence (11 companion cases)

- In 11 cases from this batch, companion case patterns were confirmed: same-accident family members (spouses, parents/children, siblings) filed separately but coordinated through the same BI adjuster and PIP carrier.
- In a multi-plaintiff MVA case (DOI 10/2021, KY), four vehicles were involved and one party filed in circuit court, forcing all parties into litigation. The firm updated the companion client: "your claim is taking a bit longer due to the number of parties involved and one party has filed their claim with the Hopkins County Circuit Court." Inter-counsel coordination stalled resolution for months.
- In companion case pairs (DOI 2/2024, KY), the same adjuster handled both adult and minor settlements, offering $4,250 for each companion in a single email thread. The firm accepted for both and requested check and release simultaneously.
- In companion case pairs (DOI 7/2023, KY), three siblings from the same accident were represented by the firm. The adjuster made offers for each sibling separately, with an adult case receiving a standard offer and a minor case receiving $500. The pattern of lower offers for minors in companion cases was consistent.
- In companion case pairs (DOI 3/2023, KY), the companion relationship included Medicaid lien coordination: Rawlings Company called "to see if I can get an update on the status of the case you all are handling for [adult] and minor child" — confirming that health insurance subrogation companies track companion cases as a unit.
