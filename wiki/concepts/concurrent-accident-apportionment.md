# Concurrent Accident Injury Apportionment

> When a client is involved in multiple accidents within a short time frame, each defendant's insurer will argue that the other accident caused the injuries. Apportionment — determining which accident caused which injuries — becomes a central issue that affects medical records organization, demand preparation, and negotiation strategy.

## Pattern

Concurrent accident cases create several challenges:

1. **Overlapping Treatment**: The client may see the same providers for injuries from both accidents, making it difficult to attribute specific treatment to a specific accident.
2. **Each Carrier Blames the Other**: Each BI carrier will argue the other accident caused the client's injuries, reducing their own liability.
3. **Separate Case Files**: The firm typically opens separate case files for each accident, but the medical history overlaps significantly.
4. **Provider Confusion**: Providers need clear guidance on which accident to attribute treatment to, as their records and billing will be used as evidence.
5. **IME Complications**: Defense-ordered Independent Medical Examinations in one case will inevitably address injuries from the other accident.

## Evidence

- In Case D, the client was involved in a chain-reaction MVA on 11/08/2022 and then a second MVA on 12/09/2022 (exactly one month later). Both cases were handled by the same firm. The second accident involved a NYC cab (American Transit Insurance). The client reported "concussion" injuries from the first accident and "neck & back" from the second. The firm set up separate case files but cross-referenced them in the activity logs ("Prior MVA 11/08/2022" and "New MVA 12/10/2022" notes). Some providers (e.g., Innovation Open MRI in Florida) were seeing the client for injuries from both accidents, and the firm had to determine which claim each provider visit should be attributed to. The attorney needed to manage apportionment strategy across both cases simultaneously. A court order in the first case required 2 defense IMEs, which would inevitably address injuries from both incidents.

## Agent Guidance

1. Open separate case files for each accident but cross-reference them prominently
2. During provider setup, note which accident each provider visit relates to
3. When requesting records, specify the date-of-accident range to avoid getting mixed records
4. Coordinate records requests for both cases simultaneously to ensure consistency
5. Flag any provider who treated the client for both accidents — their records will need careful review
6. Brief the attorney on the apportionment issue early, as it affects demand strategy for both cases
7. When an IME is ordered in one case, prepare the client for questions about the other accident
8. Track billings separately by accident to support clean demand preparation

## Pitfalls

- Mixing up which bills belong to which accident (undermines demand credibility)
- Not discovering the second accident during intake (the client may not mention it)
- Allowing defense to use the second accident to diminish recovery on the first
- Not coordinating strategy across both cases (settling one cheaply can affect the other)
- Failing to obtain records from providers who treated for the "other" accident to support your causation argument
