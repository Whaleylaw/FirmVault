# BI Coverage Denial and Forced Litigation

> When the at-fault driver's insurance carrier denies BI coverage — typically due to policy exclusions such as stolen vehicles, unlisted drivers, or policy lapses — the plaintiff's firm faces a fundamentally different case trajectory. Instead of the standard demand-negotiation flow, the firm must litigate to challenge the denial, force coverage, or pursue alternative recovery paths (KAC, UIM). Coverage denials can add years to case timelines and require attorney involvement from day one.

## Pattern

Coverage denial cases follow a distinct trajectory:

### Coverage Investigation Phase
1. **Initial Denial**: BI carrier issues a denial letter or communicates denial verbally during initial claim setup
2. **Evidence Gathering**: Firm investigates the basis for denial (e.g., comparing police report timeline with insured's theft report)
3. **Attorney Challenge**: Attorney directly engages with BI adjuster to dispute the denial, presenting contradicting evidence
4. **Extended Follow-Up**: Coverage decision may take months, with the adjuster needing to interview the insured, review police reports, and consult with their legal team

### Forced Litigation
- When coverage remains unresolved, the firm must file a complaint to preserve the claim
- Defense counsel may request extensions while attempting to get coverage accepted
- Examination Under Oath (EUO) of the defendant may be required to resolve the coverage question
- The complaint names the driver personally as a defendant (since coverage is denied)
- The carrier may retain defense counsel to handle the suit while maintaining coverage denial

### Alternative Recovery Paths
- **Kentucky Assigned Claims (KAC)**: When no BI coverage exists, the plaintiff can file through KAC for PIP benefits
- **UIM**: If the plaintiff has UIM coverage, they can pursue their own carrier
- **Personal assets**: If the defendant has assets, the judgment can be collected directly (rare in PI)

### Timeline Impact
Coverage denial cases have the longest observed timelines — 4+ years is common because:
- Coverage investigation adds 3-6 months before litigation can even begin
- Multiple defendants and defense counsel create scheduling complexity
- Depositions and EUOs are required to resolve coverage questions before reaching the merits

## Evidence

- In Case H (auto accident, DOI 4/16/2021), the defendant's vehicle was reported stolen 30 minutes prior to the collision per the police report. The driver fled the scene on foot. The BI carrier (Allstate) issued a denial letter citing the stolen vehicle.
  - The firm investigated and found that the vehicle was NOT reported stolen until AFTER the accident occurred — the insured falsely claimed the vehicle was stolen.
  - The attorney spoke to the BI adjuster and "forwarded additional information that the car wasn't reported stolen until after the accident."
  - Despite this evidence, coverage remained "under investigation" for months (10/2021 through 4/2022+).
  - The adjuster stated she was "waiting on coverage decision" and needed to speak with the insured.
  - A "Litigation Review" was conducted in 5/2022, noting: "Def reported vehicle stolen after police located the vehicle and MVA report was filed."
  - A complaint was filed in Jefferson Circuit Court (22-CI-002878) approximately 13 months after the accident.
  - Defense counsel (Campbell Ewen) requested and received a 60-day extension to answer while attempting to get Allstate to accept coverage.
  - Defense counsel conducted an EUO of the defendant regarding the stolen vehicle claim.
  - The case involved multiple co-plaintiffs (the client was a passenger in another client's vehicle — the firm represented both), adding additional complexity.
  - As of 2025, the case remained in active litigation with depositions being scheduled — 4+ years after the accident.

- In a file-setup case (auto accident, DOI 10/2025), the BI carrier (Liberty Mutual) initially acknowledged the claim with an adjuster assigned for "coverage and liability investigation." The adjuster stated: "I am working to reach my insured to verify loss details for our coverage and liability investigation." Approximately 2 weeks later, the firm's note read "NO COVERAGE ON EITHER VEHICLE." The client had no treatment to date and no PIP coverage. This represents a rapid coverage denial scenario where the entire claim collapses at file setup — unlike the stolen-vehicle case which took months of investigation. In this case, the coverage denial effectively ended the case before treatment or litigation could begin, demonstrating that coverage denial can occur at the earliest possible stage and may result in case decline rather than forced litigation.

## Agent Guidance

1. When a BI carrier issues a coverage denial, immediately escalate to the attorney
2. Investigate the basis for the denial: obtain the police report, review the timeline, and identify factual discrepancies
3. Document all communications with the BI adjuster regarding coverage status
4. Set follow-up tasks at 2-week intervals while coverage is under investigation
5. Begin exploring alternative recovery paths (KAC, UIM) immediately — do not wait for the denial to be resolved
6. File the complaint well before SOL if coverage remains unresolved
7. When multiple plaintiffs are involved in the same accident, coordinate case strategy across both files
8. Track the EUO process and ensure the attorney prepares for it
9. Be prepared for a 3-5+ year case timeline when coverage is denied

## Pitfalls

- Waiting too long for the coverage decision (can approach SOL without resolution)
- Not exploring alternative recovery paths (KAC, UIM) while coverage is disputed
- Not investigating the factual basis for the denial (the insured's story may be false)
- Failing to file a complaint timely because the firm is waiting for coverage
- Not coordinating strategy when the firm represents multiple plaintiffs in the same accident
- Underestimating the timeline impact — coverage denial cases are among the longest in the practice
