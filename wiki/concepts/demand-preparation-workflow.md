# Demand Preparation Workflow

> The demand phase begins when the client's treatment is complete and all medical records/bills have been collected. The transition to "Demand in Progress" is a formal phase change that triggers a series of tasks: final records requests, records review for completeness and relatedness, lien identification, and ultimately the drafting and sending of the demand package to the insurance adjuster.

## Pattern

### Prerequisites for Demand Phase Entry
1. Client confirms treatment is complete (via phone call, documented in log)
2. All known medical providers have been identified
3. Outstanding records/bills requests have been sent
4. Attorney or case manager authorizes the phase change

### Demand Preparation Sequence
1. **Phase Change**: Status changed from "Treatment" to "Demand in Progress" with reason logged
2. **Final Records Requests**: Any outstanding records/bills are requested or re-requested
3. **Records Review**: Medical records are reviewed for:
   - Relatedness to the accident (unrelated treatment must be separated)
   - Completeness (all visits documented)
   - Referrals to other providers discovered in records (new providers must be added)
4. **Lien Identification**: Health insurance liens identified and lien amounts requested (e.g., from Rawlings/WellCare)
5. **Demand Drafting**: Attorney prepares the demand package
6. **Demand Sent**: Package sent to the BI adjuster; follow-up task created

### Records Review Reveals New Providers
A critical pattern: when records from one provider are received and reviewed, they may reference visits to other providers not previously on file. These must be added and their records requested before the demand can proceed.

### Adjuster Proactive Contact During Demand Prep
Insurance adjusters may proactively call to check on claim status during the demand preparation period, especially as the case approaches SOL. This should be documented and the adjuster given a general update without committing to specific timelines.

## Evidence

- In an auto accident case (DOI 2/2023), the client confirmed treatment was complete during a phone call on 3/11/2024 ("done treating with all medical providers listed on file. No more hospital visits."). Phase was changed to "Demand in Progress" that same day. Records review revealed a referral to White House Clinics and King's Eye Care not previously on file — both had to be added and records requested. Lien request was sent to The Rawlings Company (WellCare) during this phase. The BI adjuster (Clearcover) proactively called to follow up on claim status.
- In the same case, records from providers were separated for relatedness (unrelated visits excluded) before demand preparation.
- The demand was eventually sent, but the case then moved to "Settlement" and then back to "Negotiation" when the client refused the offer, indicating the demand-to-settlement pipeline is not always linear.
- In Case M (MVA, DOI 8/2023), the demand preparation used EvenUP (a third-party AI demand tool) to generate the initial demand package. The attorney saved the EvenUP output to the file, then directed staff to edit it and send to both BI carriers. The demand was sent to both carriers on the same day (1/10/2024), with the case simultaneously transitioning from "Demand in Progress" to "Negotiation." The adjuster acknowledged receipt and promised evaluation "by the end of this week." The next day, policy limits (25/50) were tendered. This demonstrates the fastest observed demand-to-offer turnaround: 1 day from demand sent to limits tendered. EvenUP integration into demand preparation is a significant workflow accelerator.
- In a treatment-phase MVA case (DOI 8/2024), the attorney directed: "Please see records and bills that we are missing. She's probably done treating at this point, and we can send the demand." Records were then requested from a dental school. The pattern of attorney-initiated demand readiness check followed by staff records gap-fill is consistent across cases.
- In a treatment-phase MVA case (DOI 5/2024), the firm noted "spoke with client, he's sending over some bills he received. Need to review to make sure we have everything and move forward with demand." Follow-up requests for outstanding records/bills were sent. A PIP ledger update was requested. The SOL was 90 days away, creating urgency. The demand preparation was gated on receiving records from 3+ providers.
- In a treatment-phase MVA case (DOI 5/2024), the attorney noted at intake: "Will need 5 years of priors from all providers other than EMS." This expanded the records-collection scope well beyond accident-related treatment, requiring historical medical records that take longer to obtain and increase demand preparation timeline.
- In a trucking MVA case (DOI 2/2024), outstanding medical bills from a treatment provider were discussed in correspondence between the firm and the provider's billing office. The case was in litigation, but records completeness was still being addressed — demonstrating that demand preparation gaps can persist even after a case enters litigation.
- In 6 cases from this batch (DOIs mid-to-late 2025, KY), the standard demand preparation workflow was observed: treatment confirmed complete, records/bills requested from all providers, paralegal followed up with providers for outstanding items, then demand sent to BI adjuster with records/bills attached. In one case, the firm sent a narrative report as supplemental support and demanded "policy limits." In another, a second round of records was needed from a provider who had done additional treatment after the initial demand materials were assembled. The pattern of "sent demands on both but [client] went back for some additional treatment with [provider] which she just completed. Sent request over to [provider] this morning. Should be quick to get wrapped up" confirms that late treatment can delay demand even after an initial package is prepared.
- In cases with companion plaintiffs, the demand for both clients was sometimes sent to the same adjuster simultaneously — "I've attached the records/billing for [companion] as well. Please consider this our demand for policy limits." This batch-demand approach for companions sharing the same BI carrier is efficient but requires separate tracking.
- The pattern of PIP ledger requests before demand finalization was confirmed across 6+ cases: firms routinely request PIP ledgers from carriers to understand what has been paid and what remains before calculating the demand amount. "Please forward a PIP ledger for our client" is a standard pre-demand task.
- In cases with client at fault (1 case) and no BI/UM coverage (1 case), demand was never prepared — the case was declined before reaching that phase. This confirms that demand preparation requires viable BI or UM recovery path.
- In an MVA case (DOI 7/2025), the demand workflow proceeded rapidly: treatment complete, records/bills collected from 4 providers, PIP ledger requested, and BI carrier tendered policy limits ($25K). The firm then transitioned to the UM claim track, sending a COOTS letter. The demand-to-settlement on the BI track was completed in approximately 8 months from DOI, which is among the faster timelines observed for a case with 4 providers.
- In a demand-phase MVA case (DOI 8/2025), the firm sent demand materials directly to a third-party claims administrator (CCMSI) rather than a traditional insurance carrier. The demand requested policy limits tender. When the initial adjuster didn't respond, the demand was re-sent to a different adjuster at the same TPA. This confirms that TPA-managed BI claims follow the same demand workflow but may require adjuster escalation within the TPA structure.
- In a slip-and-fall demand case (DOI 4/2025), the demand was gated on records from a specialist (orthopedic) that the firm was having difficulty obtaining despite multiple fax requests over months. The entry "having issues obtaining norton ortho records. resending requests" illustrates how a single provider's non-responsiveness can block the entire demand timeline.
- In an MVA demand case (DOI 12/2025), the firm requested a PIP ledger and provided it to the treatment provider along with the message "Ledger attached. 7k paid to [provider]. Please resubmit billing." This confirms the PIP-ledger-to-provider coordination step before demand: providers need to know what PIP has already paid so they can adjust their billing, which then affects the demand amount calculation.
- In 7 additional small MVA cases (KY, DOIs 6/2025-11/2025), the demand preparation workflow was confirmed with consistent patterns: (1) treatment completion triggers the "Demand in Progress" or "Demand Sent" phase change; (2) records/bills from all providers must be collected before demand can proceed; (3) in cases with PIP exhaustion, the PIP exhaust letter was sent to providers simultaneously with final records requests, combining two steps; (4) demand was faxed to the BI adjuster with "please acknowledge receipt" follow-up email; (5) in multi-carrier cases (2 observed), demands were sent to each BI carrier independently; (6) in a companion case pair, both demands were prepared simultaneously once both clients completed treatment, confirming that synchronized demand preparation is more efficient; (7) PIP ledger requests were sent to the PIP carrier as a pre-demand step in every case with PIP coverage, consistent with earlier observations.
- In one case (DOI 8/2025, multi-carrier), the demand was sent to Progressive Insurance BI by fax, followed by an email follow-up 3 days later: "Please confirm receipt. If you're no longer the bodily injury adjuster assigned to this claim, please provide the updated adjuster's name and contact information." This follow-up template addresses the common problem of adjuster reassignment that can cause demands to go unacknowledged.
- In one case (DOI 8/2025), the firm added a companion's records/billing to the same demand communication: "I've attached the records/billing for [companion] as well. Please consider this our demand for policy limits." This shows that in companion cases with the same BI carrier, the demand for multiple plaintiffs can be combined in a single communication.

## Agent Guidance

1. When a client reports treatment is complete, confirm via phone and document the call
2. Trigger the phase change to "Demand in Progress" and log who authorized it
3. Immediately create tasks for any outstanding records/bills requests
4. When records arrive, review them for references to other providers — add any new providers found
5. Separate records by relatedness to the accident
6. Send final health insurance lien requests (to Rawlings, Optum, etc.) to get lien amounts
7. Compile all records, bills, and lien information for the attorney's demand preparation
8. When an adjuster calls proactively, document the contact and provide a general update
9. After demand is sent, create a 30-day follow-up task for adjuster response

## Pitfalls

- Moving to demand before all providers are identified (records review may reveal new ones)
- Not separating related vs. unrelated treatment in the medical records
- Forgetting to request health insurance lien amounts before the demand
- Not documenting the client's confirmation that treatment is complete
- Failing to follow up with the adjuster after demand is sent
- Assuming the demand-to-settlement path is linear (client may reject the offer)
