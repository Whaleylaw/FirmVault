# Dental Injury Case Patterns

> Auto accidents that cause dental and jaw injuries (TMJ, tooth fractures, jaw pain) follow a distinct treatment pattern compared to typical soft-tissue PI cases. Dental treatment involves multiple specialist providers (general dentist, endodontist, oral surgeon), staged procedures spanning 12+ months (root canals, extractions, implants, crowns), and creates unique challenges for case timeline management — particularly the risk of treatment extending near or beyond the SOL.

## Pattern

### Provider Chain
Dental injury cases involve a chain of specialized providers:
1. **ER/PCP**: Initial complaint of jaw pain and facial injury documented
2. **Imaging**: X-rays/orthopantogram to assess jaw and dental damage
3. **General Dentist**: Initial assessment, TMJ treatment, temporary solutions
4. **Endodontist**: Root canal treatment for damaged teeth
5. **Oral & Maxillofacial Surgeon**: Extraction, implant placement, bone grafting
6. **General Dentist (return)**: Crown placement on implant

### Staged Treatment Timeline
Dental implant procedures require biological healing time between stages:
- Root canal/extraction: Month 0-2
- Healing/bone graft settling: Month 2-12+
- Implant placement: Month 12+
- Osseointegration (implant fusing to bone): Month 12-18
- Crown placement: Month 18+

This staged timeline means treatment may span 18+ months from accident — significantly longer than typical chiropractic/PT treatment courses.

### Impact on Case Timeline
- Treatment completion is a prerequisite for demand preparation
- The extended dental treatment timeline pushes cases toward the 2-year SOL
- The firm may need to file a complaint before treatment is complete to preserve the claim
- The "treatment gap" between stages is medically necessary but may be challenged by adjusters

### Insurance Considerations
- Dental insurance liens (e.g., Delta Dental) may apply in addition to health insurance liens
- PIP may cover dental treatment but requires explicit direction to pay
- Dental treatment costs (extractions, implants, crowns) add significant medical specials

## Evidence

- In Case F (auto accident, DOI 4/7/2023), the client suffered jaw and dental injuries. The treatment chain involved:
  - Initial jaw pain complaint and X-rays (orthopantogram) at Baptist Health (5/2023)
  - General dentist visits at Aiken Family Dentistry (5/2023-7/2023) for TMJ assessment
  - Physical therapy at ProRehab (6/2023-7/2023) for jaw rehabilitation
  - Root canal treatment at Louisville Endodontics (10/2023)
  - Oral surgery consult and treatment at Kentuckiana Oral Maxillofacial & Dental Implant Surgery (10/2023-3/2024)
  - Client reported in 5/2024: "Have an appointment to get the crown for my replacement tooth on June 20"
  - In 6/2024, the client explained he was "finally in a position to get his tooth replaced July 15" — 15 months after the accident
  - The case went to litigation (complaint filed 1/2025, 22 months post-accident) without a formal demand being sent, because treatment extended near the SOL
  - Total dental/medical billings: $8,266.18 across 7 providers
  - The case involved liens from Anthem BCBS ($425.41), Anthem Medicaid ($425.41), and Delta Dental of Kentucky
  - The first settlement offer was discussed with the client (9/2023) but the client had "high expectations" wanting $10K+ net — at that point, dental treatment was far from complete
- In an MVA case (DOI 8/2024, KY), the client sustained dental injuries requiring treatment at a university dental school. The dental treatment was ongoing 15+ months post-accident. The Medicaid lien holder (Conduent) called for case status — "client still having dental treatment." The attorney noted: "Probably done with all her treatment. Follow up and get records for demand." However, in Dec 2025 the client texted: "I wanted to talk about my treatment?" — suggesting treatment may not have been complete. Records were requested from the dental school in Oct 2025 (14 months post-accident). The 2-year SOL countdown was at 180 days. This confirms the pattern: dental treatment timelines push cases dangerously close to SOL, and treatment completion is difficult to confirm without direct client communication

## Agent Guidance

1. When dental injuries are identified at intake, plan for an extended treatment timeline (18+ months)
2. Track the dental provider chain: identify the general dentist, endodontist, and oral surgeon early
3. Set regular check-in tasks with the client about dental treatment milestones (healing stages, implant timing)
4. When requesting records, note that each dental provider bills and maintains records independently
5. Alert the attorney early that the case may require litigation filing before treatment is complete
6. Track SOL countdown carefully — dental cases have the highest risk of approaching SOL during active treatment
7. Check for dental insurance (Delta Dental, etc.) in addition to health insurance — dental liens are separate
8. When the client reports "treatment gap" periods, document the medical justification (healing between stages)

## Pitfalls

- Assuming dental treatment will conclude on the same timeline as chiropractic/PT (it won't)
- Not anticipating that implant procedures require 12+ months of staged treatment
- Failing to file a complaint before SOL because the firm is waiting for treatment to complete
- Not identifying Delta Dental or other dental insurance liens (distinct from health insurance)
- Not documenting the medical necessity of gaps between dental treatment stages
- Sending a demand before dental treatment is complete (understates the damages)
