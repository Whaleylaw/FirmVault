# Dog Bite Case Patterns

> Dog bite cases follow a distinct pattern from auto accidents: liability depends on identifying the animal owner and their homeowner's insurance (not auto insurance), the client is frequently a minor (requiring guardian communication), and identifying insurance coverage can be a prolonged investigation. These cases may require creative investigation (address searches, certified mail to property/animal owners) and frequently proceed to litigation when the dog owner is uncooperative or uninsured.

## Pattern

### Liability Investigation
Dog bite cases require identifying:
1. **The animal owner** — may be different from the property owner where the attack occurred
2. **The property owner** — relevant for property liability theories
3. **Homeowner's insurance** — the primary coverage source (not auto insurance)

Investigation tools include:
- Address searches to identify residents at the animal's location
- Certified mail to property/dog owners requesting insurance information (LOR with turnover letter)
- Return receipt tracking (green cards) — if unclaimed, must resend to alternate addresses
- Direct contact with identified parties to request homeowner's insurance card
- Video evidence from home security cameras (e.g., Vivint) obtained from neighbors

### Insurance Identification Challenge
Unlike auto accidents where insurance is identified through the police report, dog bite cases require the firm to independently identify insurance:
- Send LOR/turnover letter to animal owner via certified mail
- Allow 30 days for defendant to provide insurance information
- If no response, investigate property owner (who may be different from animal owner)
- Homeowner's insurance may not be immediately identifiable — multiple attempts may be needed

### Minor Client Considerations
Dog bite clients are frequently minors, creating special requirements:
- Guardian (typically parent) is the primary point of contact
- All communication goes through the guardian
- Settlement of a minor's claim may require court approval
- Both guardian and minor may need to be deposed in litigation

### Treatment Pattern
- ER visit for initial treatment (wound care, infection check)
- Follow-up with PCP/pediatrician within days
- Monitoring for infection/inflammation
- Treatment is often brief compared to auto accident cases (1-2 provider visits)

## Evidence

- In a dog bite/premises liability case (DOI 10/2023, KY jurisdiction), the client was an adult walking his dog when attacked by a neighbor's pit bull that escaped from under a chain-link fence. The case was classified as both "dog_bite" and "premise" liability. The firm identified the dog owner's address, sent LOR via certified mail, and contacted the owner's homeowner's insurer (a major national carrier). The client was treated at an ER/urgent care and a follow-up medical provider. The health insurance lien was minimal ($15). The case moved to litigation with complaint filed in Jefferson Circuit Court against both the dog owner and the property LLC where the animal was housed. Discovery correspondence was exchanged with defense counsel from a major law firm. Depositions were scheduled, and the case generated heavy correspondence between plaintiff and defense counsel over months. The attorney contacted the client to evaluate the case for settlement value. This case confirms that adult dog bite cases follow the same liability investigation pattern as minor cases, and that property owner LLCs can be named as separate defendants in addition to the animal owner — creating multi-defendant litigation complexity.
- In a dog bite case involving a minor (DOI 8/2022), the firm identified the animal's location (adjacent property), performed an address search to identify residents, and sent certified mail LOR to the identified occupants. The first certified letter was unclaimed and returned; a second letter was signed by a defendant. After 30+ days with no insurance response, the firm texted the defendant directly requesting a photo of their homeowner's insurance card. Insurance was eventually identified as a regional farm bureau carrier. The guardian provided video evidence from a neighbor's home security system showing the animal running loose and attacking the client. The case moved to litigation when settlement appeared insufficient. Depositions of both guardian and minor were scheduled, cancelled by defense counsel, then rescheduled. A jury trial date was ultimately set. The case spanned 3+ years from DOI to ongoing litigation.

## Agent Guidance

1. At intake for dog bite cases, immediately confirm the animal's location address and identify all residents/property owners
2. Perform address search to identify potential defendants
3. Send LOR + turnover letter via certified mail to all identified parties
4. Track certified mail via USPS — if unclaimed, attempt alternative addresses
5. Allow 30 days for insurance response, then escalate (text, call, or investigate property owner separately)
6. Request video/photo evidence from the guardian immediately (neighbors may have security cameras)
7. Communicate exclusively through the guardian when the client is a minor
8. Prepare for litigation: dog bite cases with uncooperative owners frequently require complaint filing
9. When scheduling depositions for minor clients, coordinate with guardian for both depositions

## Pitfalls

- Assuming the animal owner has homeowner's insurance — they may be a renter without coverage
- Not distinguishing between the animal owner and the property owner
- Failing to preserve video evidence promptly — home security systems may overwrite footage
- Not tracking certified mail return receipts — proof of service may be needed for litigation
- Communicating with the minor client directly instead of through the guardian
- Underestimating case value because injuries appear minor — scarring and emotional impact may be significant
