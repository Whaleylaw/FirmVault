# Employer and Commercial Vehicle Liability

> When a PI case involves a commercial vehicle (taxi, transit bus, delivery vehicle) or an employer's vehicle, the liability analysis shifts from individual driver negligence to employer/respondeat superior liability. These cases name the employing entity (LLC, government agency) as a defendant and involve distinct defense postures including third-party claims administrators, government risk management, bifurcation motions, and specialized defense counsel.

## Pattern

Commercial/employer vehicle cases differ from standard individual-driver MVA cases in several ways:

### Defendant Identification
- The at-fault driver's employer or the vehicle owner LLC is named as a defendant
- Government entities (transit authorities) are named through their risk management companies
- Multiple defendants may be named: the individual driver AND the employing entity

### Insurance and Claims Handling
- Commercial entities often use third-party claims administrators or risk management services (not standard auto insurance adjusters)
- Government entities have dedicated risk management attorneys (government employees, not private counsel)
- Coverage investigation may involve employment records, vehicle ownership, and scope-of-employment analysis

### Litigation Posture
- **Bifurcation**: Commercial defendants may seek to bifurcate liability and damages into separate trial phases — a defense tactic that adds complexity and timeline
- **Defense counsel**: National or specialized defense firms handle these cases (e.g., LMS for taxi companies, government counsel for transit)
- **Discovery**: May include employment records, vehicle maintenance records, driver training documentation
- **Government defendants**: Correspondence goes through government attorneys (e.g., city law department) rather than insurance adjusters

### Liability Complexity
- Single-vehicle accidents involving the employer's vehicle (e.g., taxi driver injured by vehicle defect or unsafe condition) create product liability or premises liability hybrid claims
- Scope-of-employment analysis determines whether the employer is vicariously liable
- Workers' compensation may overlap when the injured party is the employee

## Evidence

- In Case U (auto accident, DOI 9/2024), the client was a taxi company employee injured while driving an employer-owned vehicle. The vehicle was unfamiliar to the client (had not driven it before). The defendant was the taxi company LLC. The case involved bifurcated litigation issues — the defense filed a brief on bifurcation (2/2026), indicating the defense sought to separate liability from damages. Defense counsel operated through LMS (Litigation Management Services). Medicare liens from multiple entities (Aetna Medicare, Medicare BCRC) complicated the lien resolution. The client made repeated missed calls (8+ in 6 weeks), suggesting frustration with the complexity and timeline. This is the first observed case involving an employer-owned commercial vehicle as the liability basis.
- In Case S (auto accident, government transit defendant, DOI 9/2021), the defendant was a government transit agency whose risk management was handled by a dedicated risk management services company. The firm corresponded with a government attorney (city government email domain) rather than a private insurance adjuster. A BI settlement offer of $15K was received during active litigation. The case had multiple liens: Medicare, Humana ($1,513.45), and additional health insurance. The government defendant's attorney operated through the city law department, creating a different communication dynamic than private insurance — government attorneys have different response patterns and authority structures. Court filing and court reporting expenses were documented. The BI claim was marked as "settled" in the case file, but the case remained in litigation status with ongoing correspondence about additional claims.

## Agent Guidance

1. At intake, determine whether the at-fault vehicle was a commercial/employer vehicle (taxi, bus, delivery, etc.)
2. Identify the employing entity and add as a separate defendant
3. Determine whether the claim goes through a standard insurer or a third-party claims administrator/risk management company
4. For government defendants, identify the risk management entity and government attorney
5. Prepare for bifurcation motions — commercial defendants commonly seek to separate liability and damages
6. Track defense counsel separately (may be a specialized firm, not the usual local defense bar)
7. If the client was an employee of the commercial entity, evaluate workers' compensation overlap
8. Set client expectations for a longer timeline — commercial defendant litigation is more complex than individual-driver cases

## Pitfalls

- Treating a commercial vehicle case like a standard individual-driver MVA
- Not naming the employing entity as a defendant (respondeat superior)
- Not recognizing that government defendants have different procedures and timelines
- Failing to prepare for bifurcation motions early in litigation
- Not identifying all potential defendants (driver, employer LLC, vehicle owner if different)
- Missing workers' compensation overlap when the client is also an employee
