# Employer Workers' Compensation Claim Obstruction

> When employers fail to initiate workers' compensation claims or actively resist them, injured employees face a compounding set of problems: employer-directed medical care at occupational clinics (e.g., Concentra) that prioritize return-to-work over treatment, forced use of PTO/vacation time instead of TTD benefits, and inability to get work restrictions accommodated. This pattern often drives the employee to seek legal representation.

## Pattern

1. **Workplace injury occurs** — employer transports employee to employer-directed occupational clinic (not employee's choice of provider)
2. **Occupational clinic minimizes**: Clinic examines only the primary complaint (e.g., arm) while ignoring secondary injuries (e.g., back), provides "work as tolerated" notes, and refuses to place employee off work, claiming they are "not allowed by law" due to the employer account relationship
3. **Employee seeks independent care**: When pain persists, employee goes to an independent ER/hospital on their own, where more thorough evaluation occurs and off-work orders are issued
4. **Employer resists WC claim**: Despite the employee being off work for more than 3 days (which should trigger WC claim initiation), the employer does not file a WC claim and instead forces the employee to use vacation time and PTO
5. **Employer cannot accommodate restrictions**: Even when medical restrictions are issued, the employer claims inability to accommodate them while still pushing the employee to come in
6. **Employee seeks legal representation**: The combination of obstruction, forced PTO usage, and unaccommodated restrictions drives the employee to contact a PI/WC firm

## Evidence

- In a workplace injury case (WC, DOI 2/2025), an employee of a major national shipping/logistics company fell down metal stairs, injuring her back and both arms/elbows. The employer transported her to Concentra (employer-directed occupational clinic), which examined only her arm, took X-rays, ignored her back injury, and provided a "work as tolerated" note. When pain persisted, the client independently visited a hospital where she was placed off work. She returned to Concentra for PT, where she was told they were "not allowed by law to place off work due to account with [employer]" — instead providing work restrictions. The employer could not accommodate restrictions but pushed her to come in, forcing her to use vacation time and PTO instead of initiating a WC claim. The client was off work since the DOI with no WC benefits. The firm noted that the employer "should have initiated WC claim once off work more than 3 days." The client was initially placed on hold while deciding whether to proceed, then signed and the firm engaged defense counsel. Later, the client became frustrated when calls to the assigned attorney went unreturned for over a month, leaving multiple voicemails with no response, and a second attorney eventually forwarded the voicemail to re-engage.

## Agent Guidance

1. During WC intake, ask whether the employer transported the employee to a specific clinic (employer-directed medical is a red flag)
2. Document whether the employer has initiated a WC claim — if not, note the date the employee was first off work (3-day threshold is critical)
3. Ask whether the client has been forced to use PTO/vacation instead of receiving TTD benefits
4. Identify whether the employer-directed clinic performed a complete evaluation or only addressed partial complaints
5. Document any statements from employer-directed clinics about limitations on placing employees off work
6. Ensure the client seeks independent medical evaluation for all injuries, not just those the occupational clinic addressed
7. Flag the case for attorney review if the employer has not filed a WC claim within the first week post-injury

## Pitfalls

- Assuming the employer-directed clinic's assessment is complete (they may have examined only one body part)
- Not documenting the employer's failure to initiate a WC claim — this is a key legal fact
- Delayed engagement: placing the case on hold while the client decides can result in lost evidence or statute issues
- Communication gaps during the hold period can cause client attrition — the client may feel abandoned before the case even starts
- Unreturned calls to the assigned attorney after engagement can compound the client's frustration with the employer's obstruction, creating a "nobody is helping me" perception
