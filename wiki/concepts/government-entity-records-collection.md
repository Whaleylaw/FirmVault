# Government Entity Medical Records Collection

> Collecting medical records from government healthcare providers (VA Medical Centers, military treatment facilities, TRICARE) is fundamentally slower and more procedurally complex than collecting from private providers. Government entities have separate fax lines for records vs. billing, specialized departments (VA OGC Revenue Law Group), and response times measured in months rather than days. Cases involving government providers require distinct tracking and escalation strategies.

## Pattern

Government entity records collection differs from private providers in several critical ways:

1. **Multiple Contact Points**: Unlike private providers where one records department handles everything, government entities split responsibilities:
   - Records requests go to the VA facility's Release of Information (ROI) department
   - Billing requests go to either the facility billing manager or the VA OGC Revenue Law Group (if a lien case)
   - Lien correspondence goes to the assigned OGC Paralegal Specialist
   - New case intake has a separate fax line from existing cases

2. **Extended Timeline**: In observed cases, the records collection from the VA took 12-18 months from initial request to receipt:
   - Initial lien request mailed: Month 0
   - Records request mailed: Month 4
   - Follow-up records/bills request: Month 6
   - VA OGC responds with lien amount: Month 12
   - Updated records request faxed: Month 13
   - Records finally received: Month 17+

3. **Incomplete Records**: VA records often arrive incomplete. The client may have ongoing treatment (monthly visits, injections, physical therapy programs like IPRP) that does not appear in the records received. Client self-report may reveal treatment not captured in the initial records release.

4. **Client as Records Source**: Veterans can access and download their own health records through MyHealtheVet (https://www.myhealth.va.gov/). This is often faster than the formal request process and can serve as a bridge while waiting for official records.

5. **Relatedness Sorting Required**: VA records include ALL treatment, not just accident-related care. A paralegal must sort VA records for relatedness before they can be used in demand preparation.

## Evidence

- In Case D, the initial lien request to VA Healthcare was mailed in July 2023. A formal records/bills request was sent in November 2023, then again in December 2023. The VA OGC Revenue Law Group did not respond with the lien amount until July 2024. Updated records were requested in July 2024. VA records were still arriving in late 2024/early 2025.
- The attorney directed staff to "sort the VA records for relatedness" in November 2024, indicating the records included a mix of accident-related and unrelated treatment.
- In October 2025, the attorney noted "We're missing a lot of stuff, and that's just part and parcel to the VA" — the client reported multiple MRIs, physical therapy through the VA's IPRP program, injections, and ongoing treatment that was not in the file. The firm had to "hand hold the VA to get all this stuff."
- The firm sent the client all VA records on file and asked the client to "review and provide me with a list of what is missing along with any other providers which we may need to obtain records from."
- A records request faxed to the wrong VA fax line (the new-case intake line) was redirected by the OGC case manager, who admonished: "This case is not a new case... the intake fax line is for new cases only."

## Agent Guidance

1. At file setup, identify whether any providers are government entities (VA, military, TRICARE).
2. If VA is involved, send the initial lien notification AND records request as two separate communications on day 1.
3. Maintain a separate tracking timeline for government entities — use 30-day follow-up intervals instead of 14-day.
4. Record all VA-specific reference numbers: GCL case number, facility case number, case manager name/phone/email.
5. Advise the client to log into MyHealtheVet and download their records as a parallel path.
6. When VA records arrive, have a dedicated paralegal sort them for accident-relatedness before adding to the demand file.
7. Ask the client to provide a complete list of all VA treatment since the accident — do not rely solely on the records you receive.
8. Confirm which VA fax number is correct for your specific request type before faxing.
9. When demand preparation is blocked by incomplete VA records, consider sending a demand with available records and supplementing later, rather than waiting indefinitely.
10. Expect that you will need at least 2-3 follow-up requests before receiving complete VA records.

## Pitfalls

- Using the same follow-up cadence for VA as for private providers (VA requires much longer timelines)
- Assuming the VA records received are complete (they are almost certainly not)
- Not involving the client in identifying missing treatment
- Faxing to the wrong VA department (billing vs. records vs. intake)
- Not sorting records for relatedness before use in demand
- Letting VA records delays block the entire case indefinitely — consider sending demand with what you have
- Not asking the client to use MyHealtheVet as a parallel records source
