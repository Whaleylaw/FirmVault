# Litigation Process Patterns

> When PI cases proceed to litigation, they enter a structured legal process involving complaint filing, discovery (interrogatories, document production), depositions, motions practice, and potential mediation or trial. Litigation dramatically increases case complexity, timeline, and administrative burden on the paralegal team.

## Pattern

The litigation lifecycle follows a predictable sequence:

### 1. Complaint Filing
- Draft complaint with interrogatories (IROG)
- File with the circuit court
- Check service of process on defendant
- Court assigns case number and division

### 2. Discovery Phase
- **Interrogatories**: Written questions exchanged between parties
- **Supplemental responses**: As new information becomes available, supplemental discovery responses must be filed
- **Document production**: Medical records, tax returns, employment records
- **Subpoenas**: Third-party records (e.g., bank records from Hometown Bank) obtained via subpoena
- **Timeline**: Court sets discovery deadlines; joint motions to extend are common

### 3. Motions Practice
- Motions to compel (when opposing party doesn't respond to discovery)
- Motions to withdraw counsel
- Joint motions for deadline extensions
- Motion hearings may be remote or rescheduled

### 4. Mediation
- Typically scheduled after discovery is substantially complete
- Mediator selected through national organizations (e.g., NADN)
- Both parties must agree on mediator and date
- Half-day sessions are common; can be via Zoom
- Mediation may be cancelled and rescheduled

### 5. Expert Coordination
- FCE and IME reports ordered during litigation
- Expert invoices must be tracked and paid
- Reports may need amendments as treatment progresses

### Court Filing System
- Kentucky courts use eFiling with automated NEF (Notice of Electronic Filing) and NCP notifications
- Multiple attorneys and paralegals receive notifications simultaneously

- In a premises liability case in litigation (Knox Circuit Court), the litigation spanned 3+ years (2022-2025+). Activities included: complaint filing with IROG, checking service, receiving motions, joint deadline extension motions, mediation scheduling and cancellation, IME amendment requests, FCE scheduling and rescheduling, economist report ordering, deposition scheduling, subpoena responses, and supplemental discovery responses.
- Motion hearings were rescheduled multiple times (from 1/20/22 to 2/14/22 via Facebook notice from clerk).
- Certified records were required for litigation — regular copies insufficient. Certification forms had to be corrected (date range "04/2014 thru Current").
- Mediation was scheduled through the National Academy of Distinguished Neutrals (NADN) with a retired judge mediator, coordinated with defense counsel schedules.
- In Case G (MVA, Boone County), complaint filed ~1 year post-accident. Full litigation lifecycle including discovery exchanges with defense counsel (Richardson Law Group), deposition scheduling via Kentuckiana Reporters, and mediation via Zoom. Mediation failed — defense offered "last best & final" amounts that were inadequate. Zoom mediation required coordination with clients on technical setup (muting, call-in numbers). The case remained active post-mediation failure.
- In Case H (MVA, Barren County), complaint filed in Barren Circuit Court (23-CI-00659). The defendant's insurer (Kentucky Farm Bureau) filed a parallel subrogation action in District Court. Depositions were scheduled via Kentuckiana Reporters. Mediation was being scheduled via the same service. Service of process required checking via alias — "Check for Service - Issue Alias is not perfected" — indicating the defendant was difficult to serve.
- In Case I (MVA, Jefferson Circuit Court), complaint filed ~8 months post-accident (24-CI-005774) due to disputed liability (BI only accepting 25%). Discovery followed, with defense counsel reviewing dash cam video. Depositions were scheduled via Zoom for both the client and a witness (companion passenger). Client nearly missed deposition — required multiple urgent texts ("Cecil, you good?", "Cecil you ready?", "Cecil?") before responding. Deposition completed successfully. Defense counsel served interrogatories and additional discovery.
- In Case F (WC case), litigation followed a different track — DWC (Department of Workers' Compensation) proceedings with BRC (Benefit Review Conferences) scheduled telephonically. Defense counsel (Pohl & Aubrey) exchanged correspondence about Form 106 requests and IME scheduling. This WC litigation model is distinct from civil circuit court litigation.
- In Case F (auto accident, litigation), discovery involved interrogatories and requests for production exchanged between plaintiff and defendant. Defense counsel (Dilbeck & Myers) engaged actively in scheduling depositions. The defendant's deposition was scheduled via Zoom, with the plaintiff's deposition to follow immediately after. The firm communicated with the client via text and phone to prepare for deposition. Defense counsel's responses to production requests were tracked through email exchanges.
- In Case G (auto accident, UIM litigation), a UIM complaint was filed separately against the client's own insurer (Allstate) in Fayette Circuit Court. The insurer filed an answer to the complaint. Discovery commenced with the plaintiff's first set of discovery. The UIM adjuster received a courtesy copy of the complaint. This demonstrates that UIM litigation follows a parallel track to BI claims.
- In Case H (auto accident, litigation with stolen vehicle), the case (22-CI-002878) involved multiple defendants and defense counsel from different firms. Key litigation events included: defense counsel requesting and receiving a 60-day extension to answer the complaint while attempting to get the insurer to accept coverage; an Examination Under Oath (EUO) of the defendant regarding the stolen vehicle claim; deposition scheduling disputes where defense counsel was "adamant" about timing constraints; unilateral scheduling of depositions when counsel could not agree; and audio/video recording requests from the court. The case lasted 4+ years from DOI through ongoing litigation.
- In a PL case (Jefferson Circuit Court, DOI 9/2023) transferred from prior counsel, complaint was filed on the same day as file setup due to SOL within 60 days. Multiple defendants were named (individual parties + a commercial carrier LLC). Service of process required coordination with an out-of-state sheriff (Floyd County, IN) with associated fees. Multiple defense counsel from separate law firms entered appearances. Records requests were sent to 15+ providers simultaneously with discovery. The case generated heavy correspondence including call summaries with defense counsel. Defense counsel coordinated motion practice within weeks of filing.
- In a dog bite case (Jefferson Circuit Court, DOI 8/2022), the case involved a minor client with guardian as contact. Homeowner's insurance was identified through KY Farm Bureau. After the BI claim settled, a separate complaint was filed. Discovery, depositions (of both guardian and minor client), and defense counsel rescheduling occurred. Deposition was cancelled and rescheduled multiple times by defense counsel. A jury trial date and final pretrial conference were set. The court system generated NEF/NCP notifications through kycourts.net eFiling.

Despite extensive treatment (21+ providers including specialists), the demand was emailed to defense attorney during active litigation. The client's voicemail was full during case updates, creating communication challenges during litigation.

The client passed away (11/2/2025) before the deposition (scheduled 11/14/2025). Family member called to notify firm of client's death. The firm then notified defense counsel of the death. Defense counsel from a large firm actively engaged in email correspondence about case status. Mediation was conducted but was unsuccessful. Court reporter costs across multiple depositions exceeded $1.1K. Settlement release negotiations spanned months with back-and-forth emails. A Medicare question arose during settlement, requiring coordination with defense counsel. This confirms that wrongful death cases in litigation incur substantially higher expert/investigation costs.

Deposition scheduling involved coordination through court reporting services. The case progressed through discovery to mediation scheduling. - In Case N (premises liability estate, Jefferson Circuit Court 21-CI-003798, DOI 7/2020), litigation spanned 5+ years. The case included: complaint filing with IROG, checking service of process, defense discovery requests via e-filing (CourtNet) and email, plaintiff's responses to interrogatories and RFAs (drafted by paralegal, reviewed by attorney), defendant's notice of intent to subpoena third-party medical records from multiple providers, defendant's use of Dropbox to share obtained records, plaintiff's discovery requests to the defendant, defense discovery production responses, and ongoing correspondence. Court notifications were tracked via NEF/NCP electronic filing system. The case was referred out to specialist counsel at one point, then continued. This is the longest continuously active premises liability litigation observed (5+ years).
- In Case P (auto accident, DOI 4/2021), the complaint was filed in Jefferson Circuit Court (22-CI-002878) naming multiple defendants. Litigation activity included: NEF/NCP electronic filing notifications, unilateral scheduling of defendant depositions ("Unilaterally Scheduled Def Turner Depo"), deposition transcripts forwarded to the client, and multi-party email correspondence between multiple defense counsel firms. Defense counsel subpoenaed additional medical records from providers not in the plaintiff's file (hospital records, third-party records). The discovery phase included document production via LMS (Litigation Management Services) and defense counsel correspondence. Bifurcation of issues in PI litigation — separating liability and damages into distinct trial phases — is a defense tactic not previously documented. This adds to the pattern that commercial defendants (taxi companies, transit agencies) bring more complex litigation postures than individual drivers.

## Agent Guidance

1. Create a litigation calendar at filing with all deadlines from the court's scheduling order
2. Track every court notification (NEF, NCP) and file appropriately
3. Maintain a discovery checklist: what's been requested, what's been produced, what's outstanding
4. For supplemental discovery responses, track what new information needs to be disclosed
5. Coordinate expert scheduling well in advance — FCE and IME providers have limited availability
6. When mediator sends a Zoom link or scheduling confirmation, immediately distribute to all parties
7. Monitor motion hearing schedule — courts frequently reschedule
8. Keep a running litigation expense tracker (expert fees, filing fees, mediator fees)

## Pitfalls

- Missing court deadlines (can result in sanctions or adverse rulings)
- Not tracking supplemental discovery obligations
- Allowing expert reports to become stale (e.g., new treatment occurred after IME)
- Not verifying certified records meet litigation requirements
- Failing to respond to mediator scheduling promptly (can delay case months)
- Not updating the trial calendar when deadlines change
