# Medical Malpractice Case Patterns

> Medical malpractice cases differ fundamentally from auto accident PI cases in case setup, provider relationships, record collection, and insurance dynamics. The at-fault party IS the medical provider, which means the standard BI/PIP insurance framework does not apply. Instead, the firm pursues a claim against the provider's malpractice insurance. These cases often involve minors as the injured party with a parent/guardian as the primary contact.

## Pattern

Key differences from auto accident cases observed:

1. **No BI/PIP Insurance Framework**: Unlike auto accidents, there are no BI or PIP claims to file. The case targets the provider's professional liability (malpractice) coverage. Insurance claims setup is marked as not applicable.
2. **Provider Is Both Treater and Defendant**: The medical provider whose records are needed is also the defendant. This creates a unique dynamic where records requests go to the party being sued.
3. **Minor Client with Guardian Contact**: The observed case involved a minor (pediatric patient) with a parent as the primary contact and signatory on all documents.
4. **Specialized Provider Types**: Treatment involves pediatric specialists (children's hospital, pediatric urology, women's/children's hospital) rather than the chiropractic/ER/urgent care providers common in auto cases.
5. **Health Insurance Lien Present**: The minor's health insurer (in the observed case, a national carrier) has a lien on any recovery, similar to auto accident cases.
6. **Extended Timeline**: The observed case had a DOI over 2 years before the last activity, with the case still in treatment phase — suggesting med mal cases have longer pre-demand timelines.
7. **All Records Received Early**: Records and bills were received from all providers within approximately one month of the request, faster than the typical auto accident case. This may be because hospital systems have more structured records departments.

## Evidence

- In one case involving a pediatric medical malpractice claim (DOI in spring 2023, KY jurisdiction), the minor was treated at a children's hospital system with multiple departments (medical group, urology, women's and children's hospital). Records from all three departments were received on the same day approximately 5 weeks after intake. A national health insurer lien was identified. No BI or PIP claims were filed. The parent served as primary contact. Case was still in treatment phase nearly 2 years post-DOI. Activity volume was low (45 logs) compared to auto accident cases of similar age, suggesting a monitoring posture.

## Agent Guidance

1. Do NOT attempt to set up BI or PIP claims for medical malpractice cases — there are no such claims
2. Verify the minor's guardian/parent has legal authority to sign intake documents
3. Records requests go to the defendant provider — handle with awareness that these records may later be contested
4. Identify health insurance liens early, as they apply the same way as in auto accident cases
5. Expect a longer pre-demand timeline — med mal cases require expert review before demand
6. Track the statute of limitations carefully — med mal SOL rules differ from auto accident SOL in most jurisdictions

## Pitfalls

- Attempting to file BI/PIP claims when none apply
- Not identifying that the treating provider IS the defendant early enough
- Missing the different SOL rules for medical malpractice vs. auto accident cases
- Not establishing guardian/parent communication protocols from intake
