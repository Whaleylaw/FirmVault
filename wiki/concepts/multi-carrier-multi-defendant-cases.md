# Multi-Carrier Multi-Defendant Case Management

> When an accident involves multiple at-fault parties (e.g., chain-reaction collisions), the plaintiff's firm must manage separate BI claims against each defendant's carrier simultaneously. Each carrier may have different adjusters, different liability postures, different policy limits, and different negotiation timelines. Coordination across these parallel tracks is critical to maximize recovery.

## Pattern

Multi-carrier cases follow a parallel-track structure:

1. **Liability Determination per Defendant**: Each carrier independently evaluates their insured's liability percentage. One carrier may accept liability quickly while another delays.
2. **Separate LOR/Claim Setup**: A Letter of Representation and claim must be established with each carrier independently.
3. **Independent Negotiation Tracks**: Each carrier negotiates independently. One carrier may make an early offer while another has not engaged. Litigation may be filed against one defendant while negotiating with another.
4. **Adjuster Turnover Risk**: With more carriers come more adjusters, and turnover is more likely over the extended timeline these cases require.
5. **Common Medical Records**: All carriers receive the same medical records/bills, but each may evaluate them differently based on their insured's alleged liability share.

## Evidence

- In Case D (3-vehicle chain-reaction MVA), the firm managed separate BI claims against West Bend Mutual Insurance, Progressive Insurance Company, and American Transit Insurance, plus PIP claims against Farmers Insurance and American Transit. West Bend accepted 100% liability within 2 weeks, while the other carriers moved at different speeds. West Bend's first offer (~$13K) was rejected, leading to litigation. Meanwhile, Progressive's adjuster initiated contact for settlement discussions approximately 2.5 years later. By late 2025, the adjuster from Progressive was repeatedly calling and leaving voicemails to discuss settlement — while the West Bend litigation was in active discovery with IMEs and depositions. The case also involved a 3rd-party vehicle claim with USAA (no demand). Each carrier required separate tracking.
- In Case M (MVA, multi-plaintiff), the firm managed three BI claims (two major carriers plus a third carrier) plus a PIP claim — four total insurance tracks for a single client. Policy limits were tendered quickly (25/50 split) but had to be divided between four injured plaintiffs represented by different law firms. The demand was sent to both primary BI carriers on the same day. The distribution negotiation between co-plaintiffs' counsel stalled progress for months, ultimately triggering litigation when the parties could not agree on allocation. This illustrates that multi-carrier complexity is compounded when multiple plaintiffs share the same policy limits.

## Agent Guidance

1. Create a separate insurance claim record for each carrier during file setup
2. Track each carrier's negotiation status independently (offer history, adjuster contacts, last communication)
3. File a single set of LORs but maintain separate follow-up schedules for each carrier
4. When litigation is filed against one defendant, continue negotiating with other carriers
5. Track adjuster changes — request the new adjuster's contact info promptly when reassignment occurs
6. Ensure the attorney has a consolidated view of all carriers' positions for strategic decisions
7. Keep a matrix of which carriers have been sent records, which have made offers, and which are in litigation

## Pitfalls

- Treating all carriers as a single negotiation (they have independent liability analyses)
- Letting one carrier's intransigence stall progress with the others
- Not tracking which adjusters are currently assigned to each claim (turnover is common)
- Failing to notice when one carrier's adjuster is proactively reaching out while attention is focused on litigation with another
- Not considering the total recovery across all carriers when evaluating individual offers
