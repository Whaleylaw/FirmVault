# Negotiation After Demand

> After a demand is sent, the case enters negotiation with the insurance adjuster. This phase involves waiting for and evaluating the initial offer, communicating with the client about the offer, potential counter-offers, and either settlement or escalation to litigation. The negotiation phase can be complicated by client dissatisfaction, offer rejection, and lien resolution.

## Pattern

### Post-Demand Sequence
1. **Demand Sent**: Follow-up task created for adjuster response
2. **Adjuster Response**: Initial offer received (or adjuster requests more time/information)
3. **Attorney Evaluation**: Attorney evaluates the offer against case value, medical specials, liens
4. **Client Communication**: Client informed of offer and attorney recommendation
5. **Decision Point**: Accept, counter-offer, or reject and prepare for litigation

### Client Rejection Pattern
A significant pattern emerges when clients reject settlement offers:
- The case may be labeled "Settlement" prematurely (before client accepts)
- Client calls in asking for an update, is told the case is "on settlement"
- Client explicitly rejects the offer ("I am not settling")
- The case must be re-classified from "Settlement" back to "Negotiation"
- This back-and-forth creates frustration for all parties

### Adjuster Follow-Up
- Adjusters may need to be prompted for a response after demand
- The firm sends follow-up communications to the adjuster
- Lien amounts must be resolved during this phase to calculate the client's net recovery

### Communication Challenges
- Clients in negotiation become increasingly frustrated with the pace
- Missed calls and voicemails escalate in urgency and frustration
- Clients may threaten to "take another route" (change attorneys)
- Regular, proactive communication is critical during this phase

The negotiation shifted from firm-vs-insurer to inter-counsel allocation. The attorney emailed all co-plaintiffs' counsel to negotiate distribution. When inter-counsel agreement was not reached, the case moved to litigation. This shows a novel negotiation pattern: the insurer has already paid limits, but the money cannot be distributed without all parties' agreement.
- In Case R (auto accident, minor injury — airbag rash, DOI 4/2023), a $5,000 demand was sent to the BI carrier. The carrier responded with a $500 "nuisance offer." The firm refused to negotiate further until the defendant had been deposed. After the defendant's deposition (3/2025 — nearly 2 years after the accident), the negotiation was expected to resume. This demonstrates a pattern where the firm deliberately pauses negotiation to build evidentiary leverage through litigation discovery, rather than accepting lowball offers on minor injury cases.
- In Case S (auto accident, government transit defendant, DOI 9/2021), a BI settlement offer of $15,000 was received from a government risk management entity during active litigation. The firm was simultaneously corresponding with the government entity's attorney about the case. The negotiation during litigation pattern — where offers continue even after complaint filing — is confirmed by this case. Additionally, multiple liens (Medicare, Humana at $1,513, and health insurance) had to be resolved in parallel with the negotiation, complicating the settlement math on a government-defendant case.
- In 4 cases from this batch (DOIs mid-2025 to late-2025, KY), demand was sent via fax/email to BI adjusters with attached records/bills, and the paralegal followed up requesting offer status. In one case, the firm demanded "policy limits" and attached a narrative report as supplemental support. The adjuster responded but the offer details were not yet visible. In another, both sides denied liability (rental vehicle driver), with the paralegal reporting to the attorney "both sides are denying liability on this. Not sure how to proceed" — demonstrating that disputed liability can stall negotiation entirely. In a third case, the firm sent demand to the BI adjuster and then followed up 2 weeks later asking to "confirm received" — the follow-up cadence of 2 weeks post-demand is a consistent pattern.
- In a negotiation-phase case (DOI mid-2025, KY), the client was discovered to be incarcerated (in county jail) during active negotiation.
- In 4 additional small MVA cases (KY, DOIs 6/2025-9/2025, negotiation phase), post-demand patterns confirmed: (1) In one case, the BI adjuster reviewed the PIP log after receiving the demand and found discrepancies: "what PIP has paid is significantly higher than the records from the demand show" — the adjuster requested additional records before making an offer. The firm provided the records and the adjuster responded with "increased offers for your clients" (companion case with coordinated offers). This confirms that adjusters use PIP payment data as a cross-check against demand records, and discrepancies delay offers. (2) In a multi-carrier case (2 BI carriers + 1 PIP), the firm sent demands to each BI carrier independently and followed up 3 days later requesting receipt confirmation. One carrier (Progressive) had not responded after 2 follow-ups over 2 weeks. (3) In one case, the demand was sent to National General BI via fax and email. No response was documented in the activity logs over 5+ months post-demand — confirming the "black hole" pattern where some carriers simply do not acknowledge demands. (4) In a companion case pair, the demand follow-up was "Demand Sent - Follow up with BI Adjuster" — a standardized Filevine task auto-generated upon demand dispatch.

## Agent Guidance

1. After demand is sent, do NOT prematurely label the case as "Settlement" until the client has agreed to accept an offer
2. When an offer is received, schedule a call with the client to discuss — do not communicate offers via text
3. If the client rejects an offer, document the rejection clearly and move the case back to "Negotiation"
4. Maintain regular contact during negotiation (at minimum every 2 weeks)
5. When the client asks for updates, provide specific information about what is happening and what's next
6. During negotiation, resolve liens in parallel — don't wait until after settlement to address them
7. If the client threatens to leave, escalate to the attorney immediately for a personal call
8. Track every client contact attempt (successful or missed) in the activity log

## Pitfalls

- Premature phase change to "Settlement" before client agreement
- Telling the client the case is in "settlement" when it's actually in negotiation (creates false expectations)
- Not communicating offer details to the client promptly
- Allowing weeks/months of silence during active negotiation

### Batch 2 Evidence (30 additional cases)

- In 29 cases from this batch, the standard demand-to-offer sequence was observed: demand sent, 2-week follow-up, initial offer received. Initial offers ranged from very low ($250 on a minor case, $1,500 on a soft-tissue case) to near-policy-limits ($41K, $100K policy tender). The typical time from demand to initial offer was 2-4 weeks when adjusters were responsive.
- In a soft-tissue case (DOI 3/2024), the initial BI offer was $1,500. The firm obtained client authority to negotiate but "not pull trigger" without final approval. A new adjuster was assigned mid-negotiation, requiring file re-review. The adjuster ultimately offered $2,500-$3,000 range. The client was "iffy" on $3,000 but needed rental reimbursement resolved first — showing how ancillary PD/rental issues can delay BI settlement.
- In a case (DOI 4/2022), the carrier's demand response included specific defense arguments: "can't place any value on the future medical expenses... she has not treated in almost two years... already treating for her knee injury when the accident occurred." The offer was "exclusive of PIP" with conditions: indemnity/hold harmless, no admission, confidentiality, complete release. This demonstrates sophisticated adjuster evaluation on pre-existing condition cases.
- In a case (DOI 6/2024), after a counter of $10,500, the carrier responded: "It appears we are very far apart... extending our top and final offer of $4,000, exclusive of PIP, inclusive of all other liens." This "top and final" language is a common carrier tactic to shut down further negotiation.
- "LBF" (Last Best and Final) is used extensively internally — the firm obtains client "authority to negotiate to LBF" before engaging. In 6+ cases, the pattern was: receive offer, contact client for authority, negotiate to LBF, present final number for client acceptance.
- In 2 cases, the adjuster proactively called to follow up on whether the client had accepted the offer — showing adjusters track response timing and may push for closure.
- In a case involving prior counsel (DOI 6/2022), the firm had to obtain PIP ledger urgently to "confirm SOL" before proceeding, adding SOL verification as a prerequisite to negotiation when taking over cases.
- Automated Filevine task "Demand Sent - Follow up with BI Adjuster" was observed in 10+ cases, confirming standardized post-demand workflow.
- In 3 cases, the attorney personally called the adjuster to negotiate after the paralegal's initial contact, confirming attorney involvement is triggered when initial offers are far below case value.
- Staff not knowing the current case status when client calls in
- Not resolving liens during negotiation (delays final settlement)
- Client losing trust due to poor communication (may terminate representation)

### Batch 3 Evidence (15 additional cases from batch 1)

- In a litigated MVA case (DOI 8/2019, KY, Nelson Circuit Court), a complete negotiation sequence was documented: demand sent 3/2024 -> defense counsel responded with $6,000 offer (inclusive of liens excl. PIP, with hold harmless/indemnity + agreed order of dismissal) -> firm countered at $20,000 -> defense raised to $6,500 -> firm countered at $13,000 -> defense raised to $7,000 -> attorney predicted they could reach $10,000 -> defense LBF at $7,500. Client rejected the LBF, citing ongoing neck issues and the 5+ year duration. This case shows the classic narrowing-gap pattern: defense starts at 30% of demand and slowly moves up, while the firm reduces demand by roughly 35% each round.
- In a companion MVA case (DOI 4/2024, KY), the negotiation was resolved through attorney-to-adjuster phone call: initial offer $3,000, settled at $4,500. The attorney obtained authority from the client's POA holder (client was nonverbal/disabled). This confirms that for cases with POA representatives, the authority-to-settle process must go through the legal representative.
- In a UM claim (DOI 9/2023, KY), the adjuster made an initial offer of $4,350 but was missing billing. After resending bills, the firm made repeated "calm" (called and left message) attempts over 3 months (18+ call attempts logged). LBF was $8,000. This case demonstrates extreme adjuster delay in responding — the firm logged "calm" nearly daily for weeks without response.
- In an MVA case (DOI 11/2024, KY), the adjuster emailed a detailed counter: "$7,500 counter demand... We are able to extend an offer of $6,031... You advised that if we can get to $7,000 we can get this done." This shows adjusters documenting the entire phone negotiation in follow-up emails — creating a paper trail of verbal commitments.
- In an MVA case (DOI 6/2023, KY), a prior demand existed at $15K and the carrier had an existing offer. The firm sent an EvenUp-generated demand and received a $21,670.50 initial offer. Client authorized "pull trigger at LBF." Settled at $40,000. This EvenUp demand achieved roughly 2x the initial offer, suggesting AI-generated demands may produce higher initial offers from carriers.
- In a case (DOI 11/2023, KY), the firm received an initial offer of $40,502 on $100K policy limits. Settled at $50,000. The settlement at 50% of policy limits after initial offer at 40% shows limited negotiation headroom on higher-limit policies.
- In an MVA case (DOI 8/2024, KY), the firm sent demand and received an initial offer of $4,000 within one month. Client was unreachable for weeks (phone not working, texts wouldn't go through, emails sent). Client eventually accepted at $4,900 "top offer." This confirms that client unreachability during negotiation can result in accepting lower offers due to inability to coordinate counter-strategy.
- In a multi-track case (DOI 1/2023, KY), the PIP adjuster (Shelter Insurance) called the firm repeatedly (5+ voicemails over months) asking whether to close the PIP file, whether a PIP demand would be sent, and requesting an updated Medicare lien. The firm did not return calls for extended periods. The adjuster escalated to threatening file closure. This is a pattern where firm non-responsiveness to PIP adjusters creates risk of losing PIP recovery.
- Settlement values observed in this batch: $4,500 (companion case), $7,000 (settled at mediation), $9,500 (MVA), $10,000 (WC), $15,000 (MVA with med pay for lien), $40,000 (EvenUp demand), $50,000 (high-limit case). The range confirms that Kentucky PI settlements for standard MVA soft-tissue cases cluster between $4,000-$15,000, with higher values requiring extensive treatment or EvenUp demands.
- In 7+ cases, the "settled inclusive" vs "settled exclusive of PIP/med pay" distinction was critical: carriers specified whether the settlement amount included or excluded PIP/med pay obligations. The default in Kentucky appears to be "exclusive of PIP" (settlement is new money on top of PIP payments), but some releases include language about PIP subrogation indemnity that creates post-settlement complications.
- In 4 cases, the release signing process was itself a bottleneck: clients failed to sign releases promptly (one case had multiple vinesign resend attempts), and in one case the BI carrier had not received the signed release months after settlement, delaying PIP reimbursement to the carrier. The adjuster called repeatedly requesting the signed release.

### Batch 3 Evidence (25 additional cases)

- In 25+ cases from this batch, detailed negotiation sequences were documented with specific offer amounts. The typical negotiation pattern: demand sent -> initial offer at 30-50% of demand -> firm counters -> 1-3 rounds of back-and-forth -> settlement at 50-70% of original demand.
- In a case (KY, BI claim), detailed negotiation notes showed: initial offer $22K -> firm countered at $35K -> carrier raised to $25K -> settled at $27K. This 3-round negotiation pattern is the most commonly observed sequence.
- In a KY Farm Bureau BI case, the initial offer was $35,500 on what appears to be a higher-value case. The adjuster requested additional records (PCP records from Valley View Health) before continuing evaluation. The firm sent updated records, and the adjuster confirmed receipt — but the follow-up cadence extended to months rather than weeks when additional records were involved.
- In a case with Allstate BI, the demand produced an initial offer of $9,000. The firm noted "2/20 calm" (called and left message) — the "calm" abbreviation for adjuster follow-up calls is used consistently across 10+ cases in this batch.
- In a case involving a prior accident (client had a 2nd MVA after the subject accident), the adjuster refused to consider chiropractic treatment, saying they would only value "DOL treatment only" at $3,500. The adjuster cited the subsequent accident as the basis for excluding the chiropractic. This confirms that subsequent accidents are used as a defense to limit treatment valuation in the same way that prior accidents are.
- In a KY MVA case, the demand was sent and the adjuster tendered policy limits ($25K) within 1-2 days, triggering the COOTS letter process for UIM claim. The demand-to-tender timeline of 1-2 days for clear-liability cases with significant treatment is now confirmed across multiple cases.
- In a case with a nuisance offer ($650 on a demand), the client was unreachable (phone disconnected, email bounced back, Lexis search confirmed disconnected numbers). The firm eventually settled at $1,200 — suggesting that unreachable clients force the firm to accept lower offers.
- In a companion case pair (same accident, guardian and minor), both demands were sent to the same UM adjuster. The adjuster made an initial offer of $3,234.95 for the minor and a separate evaluation for the adult. The firm demanded $22,000 for the minor and waited for the adult's demand to go out simultaneously. This batch-demand approach for companions remains consistent.
- In a BI claim that settled, the adjuster sent a formal email: "settlement agreement was for $2,000 exclusive of PIP... I will need the release signed and returned to me prior to making payment." The requirement to return the signed release BEFORE payment is issued is a consistent carrier requirement across 10+ cases.
- In a UIM case (same carrier as PIP), the UIM adjuster offered $21,000 exclusive of PIP after computing that "after PIP payment and tortfeasor payment, he has been compensated around $4,800." The UIM adjuster's math explicitly deducted prior PIP and BI payments from the UIM evaluation, confirming that UIM value is calculated as incremental over prior recovery.
- In a case with pre-settlement funding, the funding company (Barrister Capital) proactively contacted the firm asking about the case status and whether "there is acceptance from at fault" and "Is PIP paying?" — demonstrating that funding companies actively monitor cases and create additional communication overhead.
- In 5+ cases, the attorney obtained "authority to pull trigger" from the client before accepting offers. This language is used consistently to confirm client authorization for settlement acceptance.

### Batch 4 Evidence (55 settlement activity cases)

- In 55 cases from this batch, the standard post-demand negotiation sequence was observed across multiple carriers (Progressive, State Farm, Allstate, Liberty Mutual, USAA, Canal, Safeco, Erie, Shelter). Settlement values ranged from $2,150 (minor soft-tissue case) to $38,000+ (multi-provider treatment with significant medicals).
- In a premises liability case (DOI 8/2023, KY), a detailed counter-offer exchange was documented: initial offer $9,600 -> firm countered at $25,000 -> carrier raised to $7,600 -> firm countered at $15,000 -> settled at $15,000. This 4-round negotiation pattern confirms the narrowing-gap convergence at 50-70% of demand.
- In a high-value MVA case (DOI 1/2024, KY), the initial offer was $29,000 from Kentucky Farm Bureau. The firm logged "2/20 calm" (called and left message) and subsequent notes showed the offer increased to $38,000. The CALM abbreviation for adjuster follow-up calls continues to be used consistently.
- In a UM case (DOI 11/2022, KY), the negotiation involved escalation to the UM adjuster's supervisor when the initial adjuster was unresponsive. A new UM adjuster was then assigned with "demand review in progress." The authority pattern confirmed: the firm noted getting "authority at $5K but I think I can push the adjuster up a little bit."
- In a soft-tissue MVA case (DOI 7/2022, KY), the negotiation was simple: initial offer received, the firm asked "If you can get to $2,500" and it settled at $2,150. This demonstrates that low-value cases have minimal negotiation rounds.
- In a multi-plaintiff MVA case (DOI 10/2021, KY), the negotiation was complicated by multiple parties represented by different firms: the BI carrier had to coordinate with co-plaintiff counsel for split agreement. The client's paralegal noted "your claim is taking a bit longer due to the number of parties involved."
- In an MVA case (DOI 9/2021, KY), a SOL near-miss was documented: the attorney emailed staff "You have to pay attention to these. - In 36 cases, settlement checks were issued, confirming the pipeline from offer acceptance through release signing to check issuance. VineSign was used for release signing in 29 cases.
- In companion case pairs (DOI 2/2024, 7/2023), offers were made for both companions simultaneously by the same adjuster, with separate settlement amounts. One adjuster offered $4,250 for both adult and minor companion.
- In 4+ cases, the adjuster's formal settlement email included specific conditions: "exclusive of PIP subrogation claim," "inclusive of all other claims including Medicare/Medicaid," "hold harmless/indemnity." The "exclusive of PIP" language remains the standard in Kentucky settlements.
