# Police Records and Open Records Requests

> Obtaining police records (crash reports, body camera footage, dispatch logs, RTCC footage) requires Open Records Requests (ORR) to the relevant law enforcement agency. Unlike medical records requests, ORRs go through government public records processes with distinct timelines, portals, and follow-up requirements. Multiple ORRs may be needed for different evidence types, and processing times vary from days to months.

## Pattern

### Types of Police Records
1. **Crash/Incident Report**: The basic police report documenting the accident
2. **Body Camera Footage**: Officer body-cam video from the scene, delivered via Evidence.com (Axon platform)
3. **Dash Camera Footage**: In-car video from patrol vehicles
4. **RTCC Footage**: Real-Time Crime Center camera footage if available in the area
5. **Dispatch/CAD Logs**: Computer-aided dispatch records
6. **Related Reports**: If the accident involved a stolen vehicle or other criminal activity, separate reports exist

### ORR Process — Louisville Metro (LMPD)
1. Submit ORR via Louisville Metro Government public records portal (NextRequest platform)
2. Receive submission confirmation with request number (e.g., "24-15008")
3. Wait for processing — timeline varies dramatically (days to months)
4. May receive partial fulfillment (e.g., one officer's body cam but not another's)
5. Submit additional ORRs for remaining evidence
6. Download evidence via secure links (Axon Evidence.com for body cam)

### ORR Process — Smaller Departments
- Smaller police departments (e.g., Bowling Green PD) may process ORRs by phone
- May require follow-up calls to check status
- Contact information provided during the request may become stale over time
- Department may call back months later asking if the request is still needed

### Multiple ORRs for One Case
A single case may require 3-5+ separate ORRs:
- 1st ORR: Initial crash report and primary officer body cam
- 2nd ORR: Additional officer body cam footage
- 3rd ORR: RTCC footage
- 4th ORR: Footage from related incidents (e.g., theft report body cam)
- Each ORR is tracked with a separate request number

### Body Cam Review
When body cam footage is received:
- Download from the Axon Evidence.com secure link (link expires)
- Review footage and create a summary noting key observations
- Forward relevant footage to defense counsel in litigation
- Note client/witness behavior, officer observations, and scene conditions

## Evidence

- In a litigation case (DOI 1/2021), at least 4 separate ORRs were submitted to LMPD over several months. The 1st ORR yielded the crash report and one officer's body cam. Review of the body cam revealed key details about the scene and driver behavior. A 2nd ORR was needed for the second officer's body cam, RTCC footage, and a related theft report's body/dash cam. Each ORR was tracked via the Louisville Metro NextRequest portal. Evidence was downloaded via Axon Evidence.com links and forwarded to defense counsel.
- In another case (DOI 3/2024), an ORR to Bowling Green PD was submitted but took months to process. The PD called back asking for updated contact information — the original contact info had become stale. Even after the case was being closed, the ORR was still being processed, with the PD "finally finishing up" the request months later.

## Agent Guidance

1. Submit ORRs during file setup — do not wait until litigation
2. Track each ORR with its request number and submitting portal
3. For LMPD: use the Louisville Metro NextRequest portal; for other departments, check if they have an online portal or require email/fax
4. Set follow-up reminders: check ORR status every 2 weeks for the first month, then monthly
5. When partial evidence is received, immediately identify what's still missing and submit a follow-up ORR
6. Download body cam footage immediately when the Axon link is provided — links may expire
7. Create a written summary of body cam footage highlighting key observations
8. In litigation, forward all evidence to defense counsel promptly
9. Even on closed/declined cases, complete pending ORRs — the records may be needed

- In a file-setup case (DOI 2/2026), the police report was not immediately available — the paralegal noted "unable to locate PR." A crash report request was then faxed to the relevant jurisdiction. The report was returned via fax within weeks. This confirms the pattern that police reports may not be available through standard online channels (BuyCrash or similar) for recent accidents, requiring a direct fax request to the law enforcement agency.

## Pitfalls

- Submitting a single ORR and assuming all evidence will be included — multiple ORRs are usually needed
- Not tracking ORR request numbers and follow-up dates
- Letting Axon Evidence.com download links expire
- Not providing updated contact information to the police department
- Assuming small-town PDs will process ORRs as quickly as larger departments
- Not reviewing body cam footage for case-relevant details before forwarding
