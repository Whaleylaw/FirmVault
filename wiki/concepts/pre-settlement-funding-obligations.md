# Pre-Settlement Funding Obligations

> Some PI clients obtain "pre-settlement funding" (lawsuit loans) from third-party lenders while their case is pending. These loans create obligations that must be satisfied from the settlement proceeds, reducing the client's net recovery. The firm must understand its obligation to the funder and factor the loan into settlement math.

## Pattern

Pre-settlement funding creates a complex financial picture at settlement:

1. Client takes a loan from a funding company (e.g., "Claims Angel") during the pendency of the case
2. The loan accrues interest and fees over time
3. At settlement, the funding must be repaid from the client's share of proceeds
4. The attorney must factor the loan payoff into the disbursement sheet

Settlement math with pre-settlement funding:
- Gross settlement
- Less: Attorney fees
- Less: Case costs
- Less: Medical provider payments/liens
- Less: Pre-settlement funding payoff
- Equals: Client's net recovery

When the settlement offer is modest, pre-settlement funding can consume a large portion of what's left, leaving the client with very little.

## Evidence

- In Case A, the client had "loans" from a company named "Claims Angel" totaling $6,537.05. With an $18K offer, after paying the loan ($6,537.05) and 50% of the medical provider bill ($5,397.50), and attorney fees, the client would receive less than $3K. The paralegal noted: "I'm not sure what obligation we have to satisfy the presettlement funding."
- In Case D (long-running litigation/negotiation), a loan contract was sent to the client in May 2025 — approximately 2.5 years after the accident. The funding occurred during active litigation while awaiting IMEs and depositions, illustrating that funding pressure increases the longer a case takes to resolve.
- In Case G (auto accident, UIM litigation), a $31,200 lien from Legal Funding Partners was identified. The case involved extensive treatment ($45K+ hospital bills alone) and a UIM claim, suggesting the client sought funding during the prolonged treatment/litigation period. The high funding amount relative to case value demonstrates the risk of pre-settlement funding in complex cases — it can consume a large portion of the recovery.
- In Case G, the client's expenses included $5,000 listed as a payment to the client, plus $500 for EvenUP and $495 for Digital Owl (case evaluation tools), illustrating the range of costs that accumulate during complex litigation beyond just the funding itself.

## Agent Guidance

1. During intake, ask if the client has obtained any pre-settlement funding or lawsuit loans
2. If yes, request the funding agreement and current payoff amount
3. Log the funder name, original loan amount, and current payoff in the case financial records
4. Factor the funding payoff into all settlement evaluations
5. When presenting settlement options to the client, show the complete disbursement breakdown including the funding payoff
6. Ask the attorney about the firm's legal obligation to satisfy the funding (varies by jurisdiction and agreement)
7. If the settlement barely covers obligations, flag this to the attorney early

## Pitfalls

- Not discovering the pre-settlement funding until settlement disbursement
- Not understanding the firm's legal obligation to the funder
- Not factoring funding into the settlement evaluation, leading to unrealistic client expectations
- Assuming the payoff amount is the same as the original loan (interest and fees accrue)
