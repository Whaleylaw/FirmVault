# Settlement Disbursement Workflow

> After a settlement amount is agreed upon, the case enters the disbursement workflow: the carrier sends release documents, the client signs the release, the carrier issues the settlement check, the firm deposits the check, resolves liens, and distributes the net recovery to the client. Each step in this chain can become a bottleneck, and the period between "settled" and "money in client's pocket" is a frequent source of client frustration.

## Pattern

### Standard Disbursement Sequence
1. **Settlement Agreement**: Verbal or email agreement on amount and terms
2. **Release Preparation**: Carrier or defense counsel sends release documents with specific terms (inclusive/exclusive of PIP, hold harmless, indemnity language, confidentiality)
3. **Release Signing**: Client signs release via VineSign (electronic signature) or in person. May require notarization for some carriers/court cases
4. **Release Return**: Firm sends signed release back to carrier/defense counsel
5. **Check Issuance**: Carrier issues settlement check (payable to client and firm jointly)
6. **Check Receipt & Deposit**: Firm receives and deposits check (clearing time applies)
7. **Lien Resolution**: Firm pays outstanding liens (health insurance, PIP subrogation, Anthem/Carelon, Medicaid, etc.)
8. **Fee Calculation**: Attorney fee (typically 33.3% pre-litigation, 40% post-litigation) deducted
9. **Client Disbursement**: Net amount distributed to client via check or wire
10. **Case Closing**: File moved to Closed phase

### Common Bottlenecks
- **Client fails to sign release**: VineSign resends are common; some clients need multiple reminders
- **Release terms disputed**: PIP subrogation indemnity language or hold harmless clauses may require negotiation between the firm and defense counsel
- **Check not received**: Carriers may delay check issuance; firm must follow up
- **Lien not finalized**: Health insurance lien amounts may change after settlement, requiring updated ledgers
- **Client urgency**: Clients want their money immediately but liens must be resolved first

### Release Terms Observed
- "Exclusive of PIP/med pay" — settlement is new money, PIP is separate
- "Inclusive of all liens" — settlement amount must cover lien payments
- "Hold harmless and indemnity" — client indemnifies carrier against future lien claims
- "Agreed Order of Dismissal" — for litigated cases, requires court filing
- "Confidentiality" — some releases include non-disclosure terms

## Evidence

- In a settled MVA case (DOI 6/2023, KY), the release was sent to the client via VineSign. The client did not see it. Staff asked Justin to resend. Client was told to check email. This pattern of VineSign non-receipt/non-awareness was repeated in multiple cases.
- In an MVA case (DOI 6/2023, KY), the settlement check (#7590) for over $22K was issued. The firm called the client to notify her; she requested the check be mailed rather than picked up. A tracking number was sent via email. This shows the client preference for check delivery varies and should be asked.
- In a settled MVA case (DOI 4/2024, KY), faxed signed and notarized releases (3 releases for 3 plaintiffs) were sent to the adjuster. The adjuster confirmed receipt. However, the client called via translator saying she had 3 days to pay medical bills before collections and needed her settlement check. The firm explained the insurance check had not yet been received. This demonstrates the gap between "settled" and "check received" causes acute client distress.
- In a case (DOI 7/2024, KY), the BI carrier (Travelers) settled the claim but the firm had not returned the signed release months later. The adjuster called repeatedly (5+ voicemails) requesting the release because the PIP carrier was requesting reimbursement and could not be paid until the release was signed. This shows release delays create cascading problems for carriers too.
- In a multi-plaintiff case (DOI 10/2021, KY), defense counsel sent settlement checks but expressly asked "they not be negotiated until your clients signed the releases." The checks were cashed/deposited without releases being returned, prompting defense counsel to demand compliance. This is a serious compliance issue — settlement checks should not be deposited until releases are executed.
- In a case (DOI 3/2023, KY), the settlement at mediation required releases from multiple parties. The release negotiation itself took months — defense counsel and the firm exchanged emails about PIP subrogation indemnity language that was present in one release but not a companion's release. The firm pushed back: "our pre-mediation demands clearly stated that any settlement would be exclusive of PIP or indemnity obligations."
- In 5+ cases, the firm used ZipLiens to process health insurance lien resolution. After settlement, the firm contacted the lien holder (Anthem/Carelon, Medicaid via Conduent, Optum) to obtain final lien amounts and negotiate reductions before disbursement.
- In a case (DOI 5/2022, KY), the BI settled for $7K and the firm then had to determine if a UIM claim was open/pending before fully closing the file, demonstrating that settlement of one claim track does not necessarily close the case.
- Across 39 additional KY cases (batch 2/3), settlement check delivery patterns confirmed: (1) Settlement checks were delivered by client pick-up or Priority Mail, with tracking numbers provided via email. (2) In a PL case (restaurant franchise, DOI 8/2023), the settlement check was for $16,247.48 — the client checked with her spouse about whether to pick up or have it mailed, demonstrating the standard delivery preference inquiry. (3) Post-settlement defense counsel filings (Agreed Order of Dismissal, Notice of Settlement) were processed even after the client received their check — defense counsel may file court documents weeks after disbursement. (4) "Ready to disburse?" internal queries appeared across multiple cases — the firm's internal check on whether the settlement check was received and liens resolved before contacting the client. (5) In a WC/MVA hybrid case, the settlement mechanism was Form 110 (not a standard BI release), requiring DWC approval of attorney fees before disbursement — WC settlements follow a fundamentally different disbursement pathway. (6) Multiple cases showed the attorney explicitly asking "Have we received settlement check?" before authorizing disbursement, confirming the check-receipt-gates-disbursement pattern. (7) Release documents were sometimes signed by defense counsel on behalf of their client ("You may sign the AOD on my behalf"), streamlining the process.

## Agent Guidance

1. When settlement is agreed, immediately send the release to the client via VineSign
2. Follow up within 48 hours if the client has not signed — call and explain the document
3. Once the release is signed, return it to the carrier/defense counsel the same day
4. Track the settlement check issuance — set a 14-day follow-up for check receipt
5. Before disbursement, confirm ALL liens are resolved: health insurance, PIP subrogation, Medicaid, child support, pre-settlement funding
6. Ask the client their preference for receiving the check (mail vs. pickup)
7. Do NOT deposit settlement checks until all releases are fully executed and returned
8. Send the client a clear disbursement statement showing: gross settlement, attorney fees, lien payments, expenses, and net to client
9. For multi-plaintiff cases, coordinate release signing across all plaintiffs before disbursement

## Pitfalls

- Depositing settlement checks before releases are returned (creates legal exposure)
- Not following up on VineSign non-response (releases sit unsigned for weeks)
- Client distress during the "settled but no check yet" window (proactively communicate the timeline)
- Release terms not matching the negotiated agreement (review release language carefully)
- Not resolving liens before disbursement (may require holdback or supplemental payment)
- Defense counsel sending checks before releases are signed (creates compliance risk)

- In a case (DOI 11/2022, KY), the check-to-Conduent workflow was documented: "mailed check #7638 to Conduent - $3,047.28 on behalf of [Client]." When the first check was not received, a second check was mailed: "check #7685 (#2) mailed to Conduent." This confirms that lien payment checks require tracking and potential re-issuance.
- In a case (DOI 8/2022, KY), the firm mailed a lien payment check directly to Conduent: "check #7488 for $1,250.67 on behalf of [Client]." The Conduent lien was negotiated down from $12,000+ to $1,250.67.
- In 29 cases, VineSign was used for release signing, confirming universal VineSign usage for settlement documents. Release completion was logged with timestamps and signer details.
- The release-before-check pattern continues: the BI carrier requires "release signed and returned to me prior to making payment" across all carriers observed.
- Post-settlement provider payment was documented: funeral home providers calling months after settlement requesting remaining balance payment, confirming that post-settlement lien/provider issues can persist for months.

### Batch 5 Evidence (6 supplementary cases)

- In a minor's MVA settlement (DOI 6/2021, KY), the disbursement required a blocked bank account at a local bank (Peoples Bank). The bank sent account documents (agreement, disclosures, W-9, online banking forms) that required the guardian's signature. The bank then required proof of address when the guardian's address didn't match her ID — adding a round-trip delay. The firm and bank exchanged follow-up emails over 3+ months, with bank staff turnover causing further delays. The guardian was difficult to reach (phone number changed, "Stop" text received). This confirms that minor settlement disbursement through blocked accounts introduces a distinct multi-party bottleneck: firm <-> bank <-> guardian, each capable of stalling the process.
- In a settled MVA case (DOI 8/2021, KY), the client disputed the settlement check amount at pick-up, stating her agreement was to receive a specific net amount "in her pocket." The check reflected 40% attorney fee but the client believed she was promised a different amount. The paralegal escalated: "even if we reduced fee to 33.3% — only brings her [less than expected]." This demonstrates a fee-agreement dispute at the moment of disbursement — a risk when the client's recollection of fee terms differs from the signed agreement.
- In a settled MVA case (DOI 2/2021, KY), the client left multiple voicemails over weeks asking about her settlement check, saying she was "getting worried." The check was ultimately mailed with tracking. The wait between "settlement agreed" and "check in hand" was several weeks — the client's frustration was driven by the attorney being unavailable to sign the check during that window.

### Batch 6 Evidence (3 disbursement-to-closing cases)

- In a rideshare MVA case (DOI 5/2024, KY), the client had called "several times regarding her settlement check" but the firm had not received the check. The client "did not know amount of her settlement," indicating the firm had not communicated the settlement terms clearly. The client escalated with a formal email challenging the firm's handling. This demonstrates that the settled-but-no-check-yet window is particularly dangerous when the client does not even know the settlement amount — proactively providing a disbursement breakdown at settlement prevents this.
- In a minor MVA case (DOI 9/2021, KY), the disbursement-to-closing transition was the longest observed: 6+ months from settlement to final case closure due to the blocked bank account process. The bank required wet signatures, updated ID, proof of address, and underwent staff turnover during the process. The paralegal asked the bank: "Is there a reason this has taken 3 months?" This confirms that minor settlement disbursement through blocked accounts can extend the closing timeline far beyond standard adult disbursements.
- In a premises case (DOI 7/2024, KY), the firm was forwarding lien payment information (Optum ledger) to opposing counsel even after filing a motion to withdraw — demonstrating that lien resolution obligations persist through and beyond the closing process when the case was in litigation.
