# Treatment Gap as Adjuster Defense

> Insurance adjusters routinely cite gaps in medical treatment as justification for lower settlement offers. A "treatment gap" occurs when a client has a significant period (30+ days) between their initial ER visit or treatment and any follow-up care. This is one of the most common defense tactics in soft-tissue PI cases.

## Pattern

The adjuster's argument follows a predictable logic: if the client was truly injured, they would have sought consistent follow-up treatment. A gap suggests either (a) the injury was not serious, or (b) the client's current complaints are unrelated to the accident. This defense is particularly effective when:

- Client only had a single ER visit with no follow-up
- Client was recommended for further treatment (chiropractic, PT) but did not follow through
- The gap spans several months between the accident and any subsequent care

The treatment gap defense directly reduces the perceived value of the case and is often the primary justification for low initial offers.

## Evidence

- In Case A (negotiation phase), the adjuster explicitly cited "the gap in treatment" as justification for the offer amount. The client had treatment at only one provider with no additional follow-up care.
- In Case B (treatment phase), the client's doctor recommended chiropractic care, but the client decided against it due to difficulty coordinating treatment around work and parenting. The client's spouse acknowledged this "might be an area of risk."
- In Case D (negotiation phase), the adjuster cited a related but distinct defense: rather than a treatment gap, the adjuster reviewed additional VA treatment records and found "the claimant never mentioned this accident and all complaints were in reference to a slip and fall" at a different time. The adjuster attributed all treatment to the unrelated incident and made a "top offer" based on that position. This is a variant of the treatment gap defense — the "unrelated treatment attribution" defense — where the adjuster does not deny treatment occurred but argues it was for a different injury.
- In Case F (auto accident, dental injury), the client had a prolonged treatment gap for dental implant surgery — the client was "finally in a position to get his tooth replaced" over a year after the accident (6/2024 for a 4/2023 accident). While this gap was medically justified (waiting for healing before implant placement), it extended the case timeline significantly and delayed the demand/litigation process. The dental treatment gap pattern differs from chiropractic/PT gaps because dental implant procedures have legitimate medical staging requirements.
- In a slip-and-fall case (DOI 1/2026), a chiropractic provider reported that "all the treatment client had there was related to a previous MVA and not the slip and fall" — creating a treatment-relatedness dispute rather than a treatment gap per se. The client had concurrent treatment at the same provider for two separate incidents, and the provider's own records attributed all treatment to the earlier MVA. This is a variant of the unrelated-treatment-attribution defense where the treating provider itself confirms the attribution, making the adjuster's position significantly stronger than when the adjuster unilaterally attributes treatment to a different cause.
- In an auto accident case (DOI 8/2025), records and bills were collected but the client had only one provider (chiropractic) with relatively low treatment ($5K range). The BI adjuster's investigation included asking for the client's "reasoning for making a sudden hard stop" — a liability-focused inquiry that, combined with limited treatment, could enable a gap/minimal-treatment defense. This confirms that limited single-provider treatment creates vulnerability to the treatment gap defense even without an actual time gap.

## Agent Guidance

1. During treatment monitoring, flag any client who has not followed up with recommended care within 30 days
2. If a client indicates they are stopping treatment, document the reason clearly in the activity log
3. When preparing the demand, proactively address any treatment gap with an explanation (work constraints, childcare obligations, incarceration, etc.)
4. Advise the attorney when a treatment gap exceeds 30 days so they can counsel the client about the impact on case value
5. In negotiation, prepare counterarguments to the gap defense before the adjuster raises it

## Pitfalls

- Failing to document the client's reason for not pursuing recommended treatment
- Not flagging the gap early enough for the attorney to address it with the client
- Allowing the adjuster's gap argument to go unchallenged in negotiation
- Not distinguishing between treatment gaps caused by client choice vs. external circumstances (incarceration, financial constraints, scheduling difficulties)
