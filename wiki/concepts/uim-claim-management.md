# Underinsured Motorist (UIM) Claim Management

> When the at-fault driver's BI policy limits are insufficient to cover the plaintiff's damages, the plaintiff may pursue an Underinsured Motorist (UIM) claim against their own auto insurance carrier. UIM claims run as a parallel track to the BI claim and require distinct procedural steps including consent-to-settle notifications, separate complaint filing, and independent adjuster management — often with the same carrier that provides the client's PIP coverage.

## Pattern

UIM claim management follows a parallel-track structure alongside the BI claim:

### Setup
1. **Identify UIM Coverage**: During file setup, check whether the client's own auto policy includes UIM coverage. If the at-fault driver's BI limits are low relative to the client's injuries, UIM becomes relevant.
2. **Separate Claim Setup**: The UIM claim requires its own claim number and adjuster, even when the UIM carrier is the same company providing PIP.
3. **Consent to Settle**: Before accepting the BI settlement, the UIM carrier must be notified and given an opportunity to consent (or subrogate). This is a critical procedural requirement.

### Parallel Management
- The UIM adjuster is separate from the PIP adjuster at the same carrier
- The UIM claim number may be the same as or different from the PIP claim number
- The UIM complaint is filed in the appropriate court (may be different jurisdiction than BI)
- UIM litigation follows standard discovery and deposition procedures
- The UIM carrier files an answer to the complaint and may retain defense counsel

### Litigation
- UIM complaints are filed against the client's own insurer — an adversarial posture against the client's own carrier
- A courtesy copy of the complaint should be sent to the UIM adjuster
- Discovery includes the plaintiff's first set of interrogatories/production requests to the UIM carrier
- UIM litigation can proceed independently from BI settlement

- In Case G (auto accident, DOI 7/30/2023), the client's extensive injuries (including $45,830+ hospital bills, physical therapy, chiropractic care, and surgery) exceeded the at-fault driver's BI policy limits (State Farm, claim #1754K417G). The client had UIM coverage through Allstate Insurance (claim #723447272). The UIM claim required separate management:
  - PIP adjuster (Margaret Sharp) handled PIP claim through Allstate
  - UIM adjuster (Nick Burchette) handled the UIM claim separately
  - A UIM complaint was filed in Fayette Circuit Court (24-CI-03580, "McCay v. Allstate Property and Casualty Insurance")
  - Allstate filed an answer to the complaint
  - A consent-to-settle form was obtained from Allstate before the BI claim could proceed
  - Discovery commenced with plaintiff's first set of discovery to Allstate
  - The case involved $319.82 in court filing fees, plus expenses for EvenUP ($500) and Digital Owl ($495) for case evaluation
  - The client also had a Humana health insurance lien ($11,809) and pre-settlement funding ($31,200 from Legal Funding Partners)
  - Defense counsel (Jennifer Ann Peterson) was retained by Allstate
  - A separate "waiving subrogation" document was filed
- In an auto accident case (DOI 10/2023, KY), the client's injuries included treatment at 8 providers (ER, chiropractic at multiple locations, radiology). The at-fault driver's BI coverage (through a state mutual insurer) was insufficient, triggering a UM claim through the client's own national carrier. A separate UM claim number was assigned and a distinct adjuster handled it. PIP was also through the same national carrier with yet another adjuster (the PIP team). The case entered litigation with all three insurance tracks active. This confirms the three-adjuster pattern at a single carrier: BI adjuster at the at-fault party's carrier, PIP team adjuster at the client's carrier, and UM adjuster at the client's carrier — each requiring separate tracking.
- In an auto accident case (DOI 9/2021, KY), the case involved a UIM claim alongside the BI claim. The UIM carrier (a national insurance company) was the same as the PIP carrier. The UIM claim had its own claim number (same as PIP) but required a dedicated adjuster. Multiple adjuster reassignments occurred on the UIM claim. The case entered litigation after negotiation produced inadequate offers. This confirms that UIM adjuster reassignment follows the same pattern as BI adjuster reassignment, with the "fresh eyes" effect potentially unlocking movement.
- In an MVA case (DOI 1/2023, KY), the at-fault driver carried Root Auto Insurance (BI), while the client's own Allstate policy provided both PIP and UM coverage. The UM claim shared the same claim number as PIP but had a different adjuster. The UM claim was designated "demand_pending" while the BI was "no_demand" — the primary recovery path was against the client's own UM carrier because the at-fault driver's BI coverage was insufficient. The case entered litigation (Jefferson 24-CI-004424) as a UM lawsuit against Allstate, with defense counsel retained by Allstate. The client also had a child support enforcement lien ($46K+), creating an unusual lien composition alongside the UM claim. Correspondence between plaintiff's counsel and defense counsel regarding scheduling/discovery confirms that UM litigation follows standard civil litigation procedures.
- In a multi-carrier MVA case (DOI 1/2023, KY), the client had UIM coverage through Progressive (same carrier as PIP and BI). The BI claim against KFB was settled, while the UIM claim against Progressive went to litigation. The UIM adjuster was distinct from both the PIP adjuster and the BI adjuster — confirming the three-adjuster-at-one-carrier pattern. The case also had Medicare and health insurance liens, showing how UIM cases layer complexity on top of already complex lien management.
- In Case Q (auto accident, VA veteran, DOI 5/2023), a three-carrier structure was observed: BI (Kentucky Farm Bureau), PIP (Everett Cash Mutual Insurance via a third-party administrator), and UIM (Erie Insurance Group). The UIM claim was managed by a dedicated adjuster at Erie. While the BI claim was in active litigation (Morgan County Circuit Court 24-CI-00065), the UIM claim remained on a parallel track with separate correspondence to the UIM adjuster. The case demonstrates that UIM can coexist with VA liens — the firm had to manage BI litigation, UIM parallel negotiation, PIP benefit tracking, AND a $86K+ VA lien simultaneously. The attorney was working on "distribution of funds" as of early 2025, indicating the BI component may have settled while UIM remained open. This confirms the pattern where UIM claims outlast BI resolution.
- In an MVA case (DOI 7/2025), the BI carrier tendered its policy limits ($25K). After settling the BI claim, a COOTS (consent-to-settle) letter was sent, and a UM claim was opened with the client's own carrier (same carrier as PIP). The UM adjuster was assigned separately from the PIP adjuster. The firm began corresponding with the UM adjuster about medical records to support the UM claim. This confirms the standard BI-exhaustion-to-UIM-transition workflow: BI settles at limits → COOTS letter → UM claim opened → separate adjuster engagement → records provided to UM adjuster.
- In an MVA case (DOI 10/2023), the companion case involved a BI claim and a UIM claim against the same national carrier. The firm contacted the carrier to confirm no arbitration had been initiated. The case was complicated by disputed liability — both drivers blamed each other. The UIM claim remained in limbo while the liability dispute was unresolved, confirming that disputed liability can stall UIM claims just as it stalls BI claims.
- In an MVA case (DOI 6/2025), the case involved two BI carriers for two at-fault drivers, plus the client's own PIP carrier. While not a traditional UIM case, the multi-carrier structure demonstrates the same parallel-track management patterns. The firm tracked separate claim numbers and adjusters for each carrier independently.
- In an MVA case (closed/settlement, DOI 12/2024), the client had three insurance tracks: BI (at-fault driver's carrier), PIP (client's carrier), and UIM (same carrier as PIP, same claim number). The BI claim was settled, and the firm sent records/billing to the UIM adjuster requesting a "good faith offer upon completion" of review. The UIM adjuster responded and acknowledged receipt. A "Demand Sent - Follow up with BI Adjuster" note indicated the demand process was initiated. The BI claim subsequently settled, triggering a COOTS letter. The UIM process continued in parallel with its own adjuster, confirming the standard BI-settle-then-UIM-pursue workflow. The case also showed a companion spouse receiving the same treatment — both spouses' UIM claims were managed through the same carrier with the same adjuster but evaluated independently.

## Agent Guidance

1. At intake, identify the client's own auto insurance UIM coverage limits
2. Compare the at-fault driver's BI limits to estimated damages — if damages likely exceed BI limits, flag for UIM
3. Set up the UIM claim as a separate insurance claim record, even if it's the same carrier as PIP
4. Track the UIM adjuster separately from the PIP adjuster
5. Before accepting any BI settlement offer, obtain consent-to-settle from the UIM carrier
6. Send a courtesy copy of the UIM complaint to the UIM adjuster when filed
7. Track UIM litigation deadlines independently from BI claim deadlines
8. When evaluating the total case value, consider BI limits + UIM limits as the recovery ceiling
9. Budget for additional expenses: UIM litigation may require case evaluation tools (EvenUP, Digital Owl) and additional records requests

## Pitfalls

- Forgetting to check for UIM coverage during intake
- Not obtaining consent-to-settle from the UIM carrier before accepting BI settlement (can waive UIM rights)
- Confusing the PIP adjuster with the UIM adjuster at the same carrier
- Not filing the UIM complaint timely (UIM has its own SOL considerations)
- Not recognizing that UIM litigation is adversarial against the client's own carrier
- Failing to track UIM litigation expenses separately
