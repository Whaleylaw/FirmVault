# Unreachable Client Patterns

> Some PI clients are persistently difficult to reach — their voicemail is full, they don't return calls, and emails go unanswered. This pattern is distinct from client frustration (where the client is actively trying to reach the firm). Unreachable clients create a bottleneck because the firm needs their input to proceed with intake, treatment confirmation, and case management. Persistent multi-channel contact attempts are required, sometimes over weeks.

## Pattern

### Common Unreachability Signals
1. **Voicemail full**: The client's voicemail box is full, preventing the firm from leaving a message
2. **No answer, no callback**: Multiple outbound calls with no answer and no return call
3. **Phone number changes**: Client changes phone numbers without notifying the firm
4. **Email unresponsive**: Welcome emails and information requests go unanswered

### Multi-Channel Escalation
The firm follows a predictable escalation pattern when a client is unreachable:
1. **Day 1**: Outbound call → no answer / voicemail full
2. **Day 1**: Text message sent as fallback
3. **Day 2-3**: Second outbound call attempt + email
4. **Week 2**: Follow-up task created ("Two Week follow up with client")
5. **Week 3-4**: Additional call attempts, emails, texts
6. **Ongoing**: "Still unreachable" logged in case notes
7. **Eventually**: Client responds via a different channel or new phone number

### Impact on Case Timeline
- Intake tasks cannot be completed without client input (providers list, insurance card, SSN)
- File Setup stalls — the case sits in File Setup for months instead of progressing
- Medical records requests are delayed because providers aren't confirmed
- The case may ultimately be declined partly due to the difficulty of working it without client cooperation

### Client Eventually Resurfaces
Unreachable clients often do eventually make contact, sometimes with a new phone number. When they do:
- They expect the case to be progressing despite their absence
- They may provide the requested information belatedly
- The firm must quickly re-engage and catch up on delayed tasks

## Evidence

- In an auto accident case (DOI 3/2024), the client was unreachable from day one of file setup. Multiple calls were made with the note "No answer, vm is full." Text messages were sent as fallback. The client eventually responded via text ("I will call you later, thanks") and then called back from a different number. The voicemail transcription showed they had also been trying to reach the firm: "We have missed each other for the last day or 2." Despite repeated outreach attempts over weeks, the client remained difficult to reach throughout the case. By 2/2025, the note still read "Still unreachable." The client's phone number changed at least once during the case.
- In the same case, even when the firm was trying to close/decline the case, a staff member noted they needed to speak with the client about the case update — and had to use a different phone number than the one on file.
- In a PL case (DOI 9/2023) in active litigation, the client called the attorney repeatedly (20+ missed calls over 7 months), but the calls went unreturned for extended periods. The attorney then made periodic outbound calls. This is a reverse unreachability pattern — the firm is unreachable to the client rather than vice versa, likely due to attorney bandwidth in a litigation-heavy caseload.
- In an elderly MVA case (DOI 1/2022) in litigation, the client's voicemail was explicitly noted as "full" when the firm attempted to call with case updates. The client called back repeatedly but calls were not answered. This voicemail-full pattern prevents both parties from leaving messages, creating a complete communication breakdown.
- In an auto accident case (DOI 4/2023, KY) in active litigation, the firm attempted to call the client multiple times but the phone "just rings and then gives busy signal." The firm logged multiple attempts: "attempted to contact client, just keeps ringing" and "attempted to call client, just rings and gives busy signal." The client was eventually reached for a substantive conversation about the case status and defense counsel's settlement proposal. This is a distinct signal type — not voicemail full, not disconnected, but an indefinite ring/busy pattern suggesting a phone line issue or call blocking.
- In an auto accident case (DOI 1/2024, KY) during a scheduled Zoom deposition, the firm had to text the client urgently: "Dana you good? We lost you" and "They were having some technical problems. Go ahead and log back on." The client was also texted earlier: "Just a reminder, the deposition starts in about an hour" and "if you use zoom before are you good with using that?" This shows a new unreachability pattern: technology-related unreachability during critical legal proceedings (depositions), where the client is willing but technically unable to participate, requiring real-time troubleshooting via text.
- In a trucking MVA case (DOI 2/2024), the client's phone number was "not in service" when the firm tried to reach her during intake. A text was sent and a second number was tried. The client was eventually reached via an alternate number. This was a pattern of phone service disruption rather than willful non-response.
- In an MVA case (DOI 1/2023), the client called the firm repeatedly but no one answered — calls were logged as "missed" to multiple staff members. The client also texted: "Is your office open today?" The firm's unreachability to the client (many missed inbound calls over months) is the reverse pattern, creating friction and frustration.
- In an MVA case (DOI 1/2023), the client was initially unreachable — "call went to VM - left msg." The client then used an alternate phone number for subsequent contact. New phone numbers should always be captured immediately.
- In a treatment-phase MVA case (DOI 8/2024), the client preferred text over phone calls. When staff called, the client was unavailable ("on an errand"). Text became the primary channel. Confirming preferred communication channel at intake prevents unnecessary phone-tag loops.
- In a demand-phase case (DOI 12/2025), the client's voicemail was full when the firm tried to call with a case update. The paralegal texted instead: "your voicemail is full so I couldn't leave a message." Client had called multiple times in the days prior (multiple missed calls logged) but the firm couldn't return calls. This is the classic bidirectional unreachability pattern — both sides trying to reach each other but missing.
- In a file-setup MVA case (DOI 8/2025), the client called multiple times (missed calls logged weeks apart) but received no callbacks. The case was in demand preparation but the client had no updates. The paralegal was uncertain what to tell the client — a pattern where the firm's inability to provide substantive updates compounds the communication gap.
- In an MVA case (DOI 9/2025) with disputed liability, both insurance carriers were denying fault. The paralegal escalated to the attorney multiple times ("Not sure what to tell the guy. Any ideas?") but received no response documented in the logs. The client's inability to get a substantive update — combined with the firm's uncertainty about the case strategy — represents a new pattern: strategy-driven unreachability where the firm avoids contact because it lacks a clear answer.
- In a file-setup MVA case (DOI 9/2024, KY), the initial contact attempt to the client went to voicemail which was full. The paralegal noted "Went straight to vm. VM is full." The client's boyfriend (a companion/passenger in the same accident) had initially called in. Multiple attempts were needed before the client could be reached for a welcome call. The pattern of VM-full on day one of file setup was confirmed in 3 additional cases.
- In an MVA case (DOI mid-2025, KY), the client called the firm's main line (missed calls were logged to the attorney) but the firm did not return calls promptly. The missed call logs showed repeated attempts weeks apart. This reverse unreachability pattern — where the client is available but the firm is not — was confirmed across 4+ cases in this batch, particularly with smaller or early-stage cases that may not receive priority callback attention.
- In a case (DOI mid-2025, KY), the client was described as "difficult" and repeatedly asked to meet with the attorney in person. The paralegal communicated to the attorney: "[Client] is difficult. Nice guy but keeps asking to meet with you." This is a distinct communication pattern — not unreachability per se, but a client whose communication needs exceed the standard phone/text model, requiring in-person attorney engagement.

## Agent Guidance

1. If the first call goes to voicemail and VM is full, immediately send a text message
2. If no response to text, send an email the same day
3. Set a 2-week follow-up task automatically on every case where the client has not responded
4. Log EVERY contact attempt (call, text, email) with outcome (answered, VM full, no answer, etc.)
5. After 3 failed contact attempts, escalate to the attorney with a note about unreachability
6. Always ask the client for a secondary contact number and an emergency contact
7. When the client does respond, immediately gather all needed information in that same conversation — you may not get another chance soon
8. Record the client's updated phone number in the case system immediately when it changes
9. Consider whether unreachability is a pattern that makes the case unworkable — flag for attorney review
10. When reaching out to a new client, provide the paralegal's direct phone number via text so the client can easily call back: "It's [name] from the firm. My direct number is [number]" — confirmed across multiple cases as an effective first-contact strategy

## Pitfalls

- Only trying to call and not using text/email as fallback
- Not logging failed contact attempts (creates the appearance of no effort)
- Waiting weeks between contact attempts instead of escalating channels
- Not asking for a secondary contact number at intake
- Not updating the phone number in the system when the client calls from a new number
- Assuming unreachability means the client is uninterested — they may be dealing with medical/life issues

### Batch 3 Evidence (8 additional cases)

- In 8+ cases from this batch, clients were unreachable during critical negotiation phases. Patterns observed:
  - Phone numbers disconnected or changed without notifying the firm (2 cases)
  - Text messages would not go through (1 case)
  - Multiple VM + text + email attempts over 2+ weeks with no response (3 cases)
  - Client eventually called back after sending a contact letter (1 case)
  - Client's phone number \"was no good\" — required updating contact card when client finally called (1 case)
- In an MVA case (DOI 8/2024, KY), the client was unreachable for 3+ weeks during active negotiation. The firm attempted calls, texts, and emails. The client's phone number was not working and texts would not go through. An email was sent as a last resort. The client eventually responded and accepted the $4,900 offer — but the unreachability prevented counter-negotiation that might have improved the outcome.
- In a case (DOI 1/2024, KY), the attorney personally called the client: \"My office has been trying to reach you. Give me a call or let me know a good time to call so we can talk about your case and get it settled. Put some money in your pocket.\" This attorney-level outreach was used after paralegal-level attempts failed, confirming escalation protocol.
- The standard escalation for unreachable clients in this batch was: (1) call + VM, (2) text message, (3) email, (4) contact letter by mail, (5) attorney personal call. The full escalation took 2-4 weeks before the attorney was involved.

### Batch 6 Evidence (7 unreachable-client closing cases)

- In 7 closing cases from this batch, unreachable clients were the direct cause of case decline. The pattern: multiple outbound call attempts over weeks/months, escalating from paralegal to attorney, with no client response.
- In an MVA case (DOI 2/2024), the paralegal logged "still no luck reaching out to client" across 4+ months (Jun-Aug 2024). Calls were attempted biweekly ("Two Week follow up with client initiated") with no answer. The case was changed to "Client MIA" status, then to "Closing" 3 months later. The full escalation lasted 6+ months from first unreachable attempt to close.
- In an MVA case (DOI 7/2024), the client's phone was "not accepting calls at this time." Texts were sent, emails sent, but the client never responded during the entire case lifecycle. The case was declined from File Setup after 4+ months.
- In an MVA case (DOI 1/2023), the client stopped responding to all outreach in August 2023. The paralegal flagged "@attorney — No Contact with Client since 08/2023" in June 2024. The case was changed to "Client MIA" status. The SOL was approaching (Jan 2025), and the attorney directed "Send decline rep letter and close" in January 2025 — 17 months after last client contact.
- The "Client MIA" intermediate status is now confirmed as a standard Filevine workflow status used across 5+ cases before the final transition to "Closing." It serves as a holding pattern while the firm makes final attempts before closure.

### Batch 6 Evidence (8 unreachable-client closing cases)

- In 8 cases from this batch, client unreachability was either a contributing factor in case decline or was the primary reason for closing.
- In an MVA case (DOI 1/2024, KY), the client's phone number was "not in service" across multiple months. A second contact letter was sent. The paralegal noted: "Client is still unreachable, 2 contact letter was already sent." The case was ultimately declined and closed. This confirms the pattern: persistent phone disconnection + unresponsive to contact letters = case decline.
- In an MVA case (DOI 10/2024, KY), the client's "phone number cannot accept calls at this time" — a distinct signal from VM full or disconnected. The case was closed from Demand in Progress, skipping demand entirely because the client could not be reached.
- In a companion MVA/KAC case (DOI 9/2023, KY), the client "answered but hung up right away" and did not respond to follow-up texts. This is a third distinct unreachability signal: active rejection of contact.
- In a premises liability case (DOI 11/2024, KY), the client texted months after closing asking if the attorney was the one who called, suggesting the client only re-engaged when prompted by a new contact attempt. Clients who are unreachable during the case may resurface when the firm reaches out for file closure.
- In 4 cases, unreachability directly caused the case to stall in File Setup for months before eventual decline — confirming that early-stage unreachability is a strong predictor of eventual case closure.
