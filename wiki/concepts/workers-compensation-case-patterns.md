# Workers' Compensation Case Patterns

> Workers' compensation cases differ fundamentally from personal injury cases in their procedural framework, involved parties, and resolution mechanisms. WC cases are governed by state Department of Workers' Compensation (DWC) rules rather than civil court procedures, involve employer's WC insurance carriers rather than at-fault BI carriers, and resolve through administrative proceedings (Benefit Review Conferences) rather than jury trials.

## Pattern

### Key Differences from PI Cases
1. **Intake**: CaseOpp system may use the wrong contract type (PI vs WC) — must verify and re-send WC-specific intake documents
2. **Insurance Setup**: LOR sent to WC adjuster (not BI/PIP). DWC insurance lookup required to identify the WC carrier
3. **Treatment Monitoring**: Extended treatment periods common (2+ years). Client may undergo surgery, PT, injections, and still be in active treatment. Treatment includes hardware placement (plates, screws)
4. **Administrative Proceedings**: BRC (Benefit Review Conference) hearings are telephonic, scheduled by DWC, with assigned passcodes. BRCs are frequently rescheduled
5. **Forms**: DWC Form 106 required for various proceedings — sent to client via DocuSign for execution per defense counsel request
6. **Defense Counsel**: Employer/carrier represented by defense firm that exchanges correspondence about Form 106, IME scheduling, and case status
7. **IME**: Scheduled through Commonwealth IME (same service as PI), but invoiced and tracked through WC process. IME is paid by the firm

### WC Case Lifecycle
- Intake & File Setup (WC-specific LOR, DWC carrier lookup)
- Active Treatment (potentially years — surgery, PT, injections, MMI assessment)
- DWC Proceedings (BRC hearings, rescheduling)
- IME / Medical Evaluation
- Defense Counsel Negotiations
- Resolution (settlement or hearing)

## Evidence

- In Case F (WC case, DOI 12/2022), client was injured at a commercial restaurant. The case involved:
  - Intake through CaseOpp with wrong contract type (PI instead of WC), requiring re-send of WC intake docs
  - WC carrier identified via DWC lookup (Accident Fund Insurance)
  - LOR sent to WC adjuster via fax on day 1
  - Extensive treatment: ER visit, orthopedic surgery (plate + 10 screws in wrist), physical therapy, shoulder injections, pain management, MRI, dry needling — spanning 2.5+ years
  - DWC Form 106 sent to client via DocuSign multiple times per defense counsel requests
  - BRC hearings scheduled telephonically, rescheduled multiple times (rescheduled from Feb to Mar 2025)
  - Defense counsel (Pohl & Aubrey) actively exchanged correspondence about IME scheduling and Form 106
  - IME scheduled through Commonwealth IME — coordination via email, invoice tracked
  - WC adjuster communications via email about claim status
  - Case timeline: 3+ years from DOI and still active (Jan 2023 to Mar 2026+)
- In a second WC case (estate claim, DOI 12/2020), the worker died and the estate (represented by the surviving spouse) filed the WC claim. Key observations:
  - The initial claim was denied via DC Form 111 on grounds of "no living person named" and "no supporting documents" — requiring a Motion to Substitute Plaintiff (estate for individual) filed via KY DWC LMS
  - DWC scheduling order assigned an Administrative Law Judge, with BRC hearing and formal hearing dates set
  - Defense counsel filed RFPD (Request for Production of Documents) and contested the claim on multiple grounds
  - Forms 104 and 105 were filed along with 45-Day Disclosures and supporting medical documentation
  - Commonwealth IME was used for medical evaluation (3 separate IME invoices totaling $1,750+), confirming this is the standard WC IME vendor
  - BRC hearings were repeatedly rescheduled over a 3+ year period (DOI 2020, proceedings through 2025)
  - The case resolved through ALJ opinion, which was concurred by the WC Board — demonstrating the full WC administrative resolution pathway
  - LMS (Labor Management System) document notifications were the primary court communication channel, sent via no-reply@ky.gov
  - Total case timeline: 5+ years from DOI (12/2020) through Board opinion (2/2025) — significantly longer than the first observed WC case
  - This case confirms that WC estate claims add procedural complexity (substitution of parties, probate requirements) on top of the already complex WC process
- In a third WC case (workplace injury at automotive manufacturing plant, DOI 5/2023), key observations:
  - WC intake packet sent via VineSign; intake took ~2 months for client to return signed documents
  - The client was receiving TTD (Temporary Total Disability) benefits and still treating for an extended period (2+ years)
  - The firm adopted a "monitoring" posture — simply tracking the client's treatment status rather than actively managing the claim ("Per attorney simply monitoring for client - No active participation - LOR has not been sent")
  - Treatment involved multiple providers including hospital (ER), podiatric surgery, orthopedic specialists, physical therapy, and pain management
  - The WC carrier's defense counsel (O'Bryan, Brown & Toner, PLLC) eventually initiated litigation actions: deposition scheduling (in-person at defense office), IME notice, and Request for Production of Documents
  - Defense counsel requested signed medical release/authorizations specifically for their records collection
  - Deposition was scheduled via defense counsel's legal assistant, with court reporter coordination (Kentuckiana Reporters)
  - **Client death during proceedings**: The client passed away before the scheduled deposition. Family member (mother) called to notify the firm via voicemail. The firm then notified defense counsel immediately.
  - This case illustrates a unique WC outcome — client death requiring procedural pivot (potential estate substitution or claim termination)
- In a fourth WC+BI dual-track case (DOI 6/2021, KY), a Tennessee attorney had settled the WC portion separately (lien resolved for ~$17K) but did not pursue the third-party BI claim. The case was referred to the KY firm on the day before SOL expiration. Key observations:
  - The firm filed a BI complaint in Henry Circuit Court the same day, bypassing the normal case workflow entirely
  - The case was immediately placed into Litigation phase, skipping File Setup, Treatment, and Demand
  - The BI carrier (Old Republic Insurance via Sedgwick TPA) was notified via LOR and complaint
  - The WC carrier (AmTrust North America) had a separate lien of $30,926 on the BI recovery
  - An IME was scheduled through Commonwealth IME — confirming this is the standard vendor for both WC and BI evaluations
  - A telephonic settlement conference occurred approximately 2 years into litigation. The BI carrier's top offer was $35K. The judge indicated recovery above $50K was unlikely
  - The BI carrier paid the WC lien directly ($17K), separate from the BI settlement offer
  - Expert deposition was scheduled via Zoom (Dr. Nazar, orthopedic expert) through Esquire Deposition Solutions
  - A motion to exclude expert testimony was filed by the defense in federal court (transferred from state court)
  - The client received VA treatment at a VA Medical Center — adding VA records collection to the mix alongside WC and BI requirements
  - Case timeline: 4+ years from DOI (6/2021) through active litigation (2025+). This is the longest-running WC+BI case observed
  - This case demonstrates the WC/BI dual-track pattern where the WC settlement creates a lien on the BI recovery, the BI claim is litigated separately, and the BI carrier may pay the WC lien directly as part of settlement negotiations

## Agent Guidance

1. Verify the contract type is WC (not PI) during intake — CaseOpp may default to PI
2. Use DWC insurance lookup to identify the WC carrier
3. Send WC-specific LOR (not PI LOR) to the WC adjuster
4. Track DWC Form 106 requests and ensure client executes via DocuSign promptly
5. Monitor BRC scheduling and rescheduling — update all parties on new dates
6. WC treatment timelines are often much longer than PI — set client expectations accordingly
7. Track IME scheduling and invoicing through the WC process
8. Communicate with defense counsel about Form 106 and IME scheduling per DWC rules
9. Bill review and submission goes to WC carrier, not PIP
10. For estate/death claims: file a Motion to Substitute Plaintiff naming the estate as soon as possible — initial claims may be denied if only the deceased individual is named
11. Track ALJ assignments and hearing dates through the LMS system — expect frequent rescheduling

## Pitfalls

- Using PI intake documents instead of WC-specific forms
- Not performing DWC insurance lookup (WC carriers differ from auto/premises carriers)
- Applying PI case timeline expectations to WC cases (WC cases regularly span 3+ years)
- Missing BRC rescheduling notices (these are time-sensitive)
- Not tracking Form 106 execution status
- Confusing WC process milestones with PI phase transitions
- Filing estate claims in the deceased individual's name instead of the estate's name (immediate denial)
- Not filing Forms 104/105 and 45-Day Disclosures promptly after scheduling order
