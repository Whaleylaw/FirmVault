# Workers' Compensation Claim Patterns

> Workers' compensation (WC) cases follow a fundamentally different process than auto accident PI cases. WC claims involve an employer's insurance carrier that must approve treatment, authorize specific providers, and pay Temporary Total Disability (TTD) benefits. Unlike BI claims where the firm collects records and sends a demand, WC cases require ongoing adjuster interaction to authorize each step of treatment.

## Pattern

### WC Intake Differences
1. **Employer notification**: Unlike auto accidents, the client must have notified their employer of the workplace injury
2. **WC carrier identification**: The employer's WC carrier (not the client's personal auto/health insurer) is the primary claim target
3. **LOR sent to WC carrier**: Letter of Representation is sent to the WC adjuster via email and fax
4. **Claim acceptance**: The WC adjuster must formally accept the claim before treatment is authorized

### WC Treatment Authorization
Unlike PI cases where the client simply goes to a provider:
1. The WC adjuster must approve the treating provider
2. The firm may need to call the provider to confirm the approval was received
3. If approval is not received by the provider, the firm must fax the approval directly
4. The client's preferred provider may require formal WC approval before scheduling

### TTD (Temporary Total Disability) Benefits
1. TTD payments are periodic wage-replacement benefits during recovery
2. Payments are based on the Average Weekly Wage (AWW)
3. Payment amounts and timing must be tracked — missed or short payments require immediate follow-up with the adjuster
4. AWW disputes may require filing a formal WC claim to get an accurate wage calculation

### AWW (Average Weekly Wage) Disputes
When the employer or carrier calculates the AWW incorrectly:
1. The firm identifies the discrepancy (e.g., from employer records or client-reported wages)
2. A formal WC claim may need to be filed with the state to correct the AWW
3. This affects the TTD rate retroactively — past payments may need adjustment
4. Resolution requires employer wage records and possibly payroll documentation

### WC Deposition and Litigation
WC cases may involve:
- Defense counsel retained by the WC carrier
- Deposition scheduling directly with defense counsel
- Rescheduling is common when defense counsel needs to coordinate

### Employer-Related Evidence
- Screenshots of conversations between client and employer may be relevant
- Employer confusion about the WC claim process is common
- Employer may not directly discuss return-to-work issues, requiring documentation

## Evidence

- In a workplace injury case (elevator incident, DOI 1/2024), the WC adjuster accepted the claim within days. The firm had to fax the treatment approval to the provider because the provider had not received it. TTD payments were tracked over 12+ months, with at least one instance of a missed/short payment requiring follow-up with the adjuster. A formal WC claim was eventually filed because the firm could not verify the accuracy of the AWW. Defense counsel (a national firm) was retained by the carrier, and deposition scheduling involved multiple reschedules over a 2-month period.
- In a WC case (workplace fall at major logistics employer, DOI 2/2025), the employer directed the client to an occupational clinic (Concentra) which only examined one body part despite multiple injuries, issued minimal work restrictions, and told the client they were "not allowed by law to place off work" due to the employer account. The employer failed to initiate a WC claim despite the client being off work for more than 3 days, forcing the client to use vacation time and PTO. The WC carrier (national insurer) eventually assigned an adjuster and defense counsel, but the case demonstrated significant employer resistance to the WC claim process — a pattern distinct from standard WC intake where the carrier promptly accepts the claim.

## Agent Guidance

1. During intake for WC cases, confirm: employer name, WC carrier name, adjuster contact, claim number, and whether the employer was notified
2. Send LOR to the WC adjuster immediately — include both email and fax
3. After claim acceptance, confirm the adjuster has authorized the client's chosen provider
4. Track TTD payments: set a recurring task to verify each payment was received on time and at the correct amount
5. If a TTD payment is missed or short, contact the adjuster within 24 hours
6. Collect employer wage records early — AWW disputes are easier to resolve with documentation
7. When defense counsel is assigned, add them to the case contacts immediately
8. For deposition scheduling, coordinate with both the client and defense counsel — expect rescheduling

## Pitfalls

- Assuming WC cases follow the same workflow as auto accident PI cases
- Not confirming treatment authorization was received by the provider (approval may be sent to the firm but not the provider)
- Failing to track TTD payments systematically — missed payments cause client financial distress
- Not filing a formal WC claim when AWW is disputed — the carrier may use an incorrect AWW indefinitely
- Not collecting employer records and screenshots of communications as evidence
