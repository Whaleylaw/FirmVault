# Wrongful Death Case Patterns

> Wrongful death cases differ fundamentally from standard PI cases because the injured party is deceased. The case is brought by a representative (typically a surviving family member) on behalf of the estate. These cases involve no medical treatment records (the decedent was pronounced dead at the scene), but require extensive investigation, accident reconstruction, vocational/economic expert evaluations, and often multi-carrier coordination when multiple at-fault parties or vehicles are involved.

## Pattern

### Key Differences from Standard PI Cases
1. **Client is a representative**: The person the firm communicates with is not the injured party but a surviving family member acting on behalf of the estate
2. **No medical treatment phase**: The decedent has no ongoing treatment, so the case skips the treatment phase entirely and moves from file setup into investigation and litigation
3. **Investigation-heavy**: Wrongful death cases require significant investment in investigation (accident reconstruction, witness interviews, scene evidence) — costs can exceed $20K
4. **Expert evaluations focus on lost earnings**: Vocational economics experts evaluate the decedent's earning capacity and life expectancy rather than treatment outcomes
5. **Multi-carrier complexity**: When multiple vehicles or entities are involved, separate BI claims are filed against each carrier, creating parallel negotiation tracks
6. **Higher litigation costs**: Court reporting, investigation, accident reconstruction, and expert fees are substantially higher than typical PI cases
7. **Settlement release coordination**: Release negotiations involve multiple defense counsel firms and can span months of back-and-forth correspondence
8. **Medicare considerations**: Even in death cases, Medicare questions may arise during settlement if the decedent had Medicare coverage

### Wrongful Death Case Lifecycle
- Intake via representative (surviving family member)
- Insurance claim setup (multiple carriers if applicable)
- Investigation and evidence collection (police reports, accident reconstruction)
- Expert retention (vocational economics, accident reconstruction)
- Complaint filing and litigation
- Discovery and depositions (of representative, witnesses)
- Settlement negotiations (potentially with multiple defense counsel)
- Release execution and disbursement

## Evidence

- In a wrongful death MVA case (DOI 8/2023, KY jurisdiction), the decedent was treated at a university hospital and by EMS but subsequently died. A family member served as primary contact. The case involved a BI claim against a risk management services company and a PIP claim. The firm was communicating through family members (daughter-in-law and husband of the decedent). A large health insurance lien ($89K+) through a union benefits trust was present, requiring calls to the trust's main line and escalation when member status was flagged as "not active" — requiring reinstatement coordination. The firm also retained a collision reconstruction expert. The case involved court filings (circuit and district) with associated filing fees. A lien verification service (Zipliens) was used. The case demonstrates that wrongful death cases from MVAs may still involve substantial hospital treatment and bills (unlike instant-death cases), and that health insurance liens through union/trust benefit plans add another layer of complexity with unique contact and escalation patterns.
- In a wrongful death case (DOI 2/2022, KY jurisdiction), the decedent was pronounced dead at the scene of a multi-vehicle crash. A surviving family member served as representative and primary contact. The case involved three separate BI carriers (a personal auto carrier, a commercial trucking company's national liabilities office, and a third personal auto carrier), each requiring independent LOR filing and claim setup. Investigation costs exceeded $20K (focus investigation services). Accident reconstruction ($2K) and vocational economics evaluation ($3.2K) were retained. The case was filed in circuit court (Marshall County 23-CI-00073). Court reporter costs across depositions exceeded $1.1K. Additional litigation expenses included multiple defense law firms ($570, $688, $2K, $2.1K). Settlement release negotiations extended over months with back-and-forth emails between the firm and defense counsel. A Medicare question arose during settlement, requiring coordination. The total case timeline exceeded 3 years from DOI with resolution still pending. Pre-settlement funding ($4K) and workers' compensation lien ($171K+) were also present, adding significant lien complexity. A notary service was used to obtain the representative's signature remotely.

## Agent Guidance

1. During intake, identify the representative and their relationship to the decedent — confirm legal authority to act on behalf of the estate
2. Skip the treatment/records collection phase — focus immediately on investigation and insurance claim setup
3. File LORs with all potentially liable carriers within the first week
4. Budget for substantially higher case expenses: investigation ($10K-20K+), accident reconstruction, vocational economics, court reporting
5. Coordinate with multiple defense counsel firms — each carrier typically retains separate counsel
6. Track release negotiations carefully — multiple defense firms means multiple release documents
7. Check for Medicare/Medicaid liens even in death cases — the decedent may have had coverage
8. Maintain regular contact with the representative — they are grieving and need empathetic communication alongside case updates

## Pitfalls

- Treating a wrongful death case like a standard PI case (no treatment phase, investigation-focused)
- Not identifying all potentially liable parties and their carriers early
- Underbudgeting for investigation and expert costs
- Not confirming the representative's legal authority to act for the estate
- Allowing settlement release negotiations to stall without follow-up
- Ignoring Medicare/lien considerations in death cases
