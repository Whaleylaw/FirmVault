# Client Frustration Drives Premature Settlement or Attorney Attrition

> When clients become frustrated with the pace and communication during the negotiation phase, two negative outcomes are likely: (1) the client pressures for a premature settlement at a lower value than the case warrants, or (2) the client threatens to or actually changes attorneys. Both outcomes are preventable through proactive communication and clear expectation-setting.

## Insight

In the observed auto accident case, the client was told the case was "on settlement phase" but then refused the offer. Over the following 12+ months, the client made dozens of calls (many missed), sent frustrated text messages, and explicitly threatened to "take another route." Staff members were unsure of the case status ("appears was settled but client didn't accept"). The combination of poor communication, premature settlement labeling, and missed calls created a situation where the client-firm relationship was severely strained.

In the premises liability case (6+ years in litigation), the client texted the attorney directly after a court appearance with no follow-up. The attorney's prompt, substantive response prevented escalation — demonstrating that responsive communication, even when there's limited news, maintains the relationship.

In Case M (MVA, multi-plaintiff), the client's frustration was driven by a factor outside the firm's control: another plaintiff's counsel failing to provide medical bills needed for policy limits distribution. The client called repeatedly and was told "we are waiting on the other firm." The frustration escalated to an in-person office visit. This variant shows that inter-counsel coordination delays create the same frustration dynamic as internal delays — and the client cannot distinguish between the two.

In Case N (premises liability estate, 5+ year litigation), the surviving spouse called repeatedly over 18+ months (Jul 2025-Mar 2026) with missed calls and unreturned messages. The emotional stakes are higher in estate cases where the contact is grieving. The long litigation timeline without visible progress compounds the frustration. Despite the firm actively conducting litigation (depositions, discovery), the spouse perceived inaction because he was not being proactively updated.

## Agent Guidance

1. Never label a case as "Settlement" until the client has agreed to accept the offer
2. When an offer is received, schedule a call with the client within 48 hours to discuss
3. Maintain 2-week check-in schedule during active negotiation
4. When a client shows Stage 2 frustration signs, escalate to the attorney for a personal call within 24 hours
5. Ensure all staff members know the current case status so they can answer client questions coherently

### Batch 6 Evidence (5 attrition cases)

- In 5 cases from this batch, client frustration directly led to case closure via client termination (attrition), not premature settlement. The attrition pattern:
  - In an MVA case (DOI 12/2023, KY), the client emailed terminating the firm because her case was "unresolved." The case was in Treatment phase — the client perceived inaction during the records-gathering phase.
  - In an MVA case (DOI 9/2024, KY), the client "called to say she already had someone to take the case previously." The firm had only been retained 6 days earlier — the client had dual-retained and chose the other firm.
  - In an MVA case (DOI 11/2024, KY), the client terminated within 4 days of intake. The firm had not yet completed file setup. The email subject was "Terminate of attorney." Extremely early terminations suggest the client signed up with multiple firms simultaneously.
  - In an MVA case (DOI 7/2024, KY), the firm discovered the client was "being represented by Morgan and Morgan" when calling the carrier for claim info. The client had never told the firm she had hired another attorney. The carrier notified the firm of the competing representation — a novel discovery pathway.
  - In an MVA case (DOI 6/2025, KY), the client terminated after months of non-communication from the firm and retained new counsel. The new firm could not get the file transferred for weeks. This is the most severe attrition outcome observed: the client is harmed by both the lack of service AND the difficulty of transitioning away.
- **Closing pattern**: Unlike premature settlement (where the client gets a lower recovery), attrition cases result in the client getting NO recovery from the first firm and having to restart with new counsel — a worse outcome for the client and a total loss for the firm.
