# Coverage Denial Forces Early Litigation and Alternative Recovery Paths

> When a BI carrier denies coverage, the standard case lifecycle is disrupted at the earliest stage. Instead of proceeding through treatment -> demand -> negotiation, the firm must immediately pursue two parallel paths: (1) litigation to challenge the denial or establish liability, and (2) alternative recovery mechanisms (KAC for PIP benefits, UIM for underinsured/uninsured coverage). This dual-path approach significantly increases case complexity and timeline.

## How the Concepts Interact

### Coverage Denial -> Forced Litigation
- [[coverage-denial-and-litigation]]: When BI coverage is denied, the firm cannot send a demand to the carrier. The only path to BI recovery is litigation — filing a complaint against the driver personally and potentially the carrier.
- [[case-phase-transitions]]: The case skips the demand/negotiation phases entirely and moves from file setup directly to litigation.
- [[statute-of-limitations-monitoring]]: The SOL clock runs regardless of coverage disputes. The firm must file within 2 years even if coverage is still "under investigation."

### Alternative Recovery Paths
- [[kentucky-assigned-claims-process]]: When no BI coverage exists, KAC provides PIP-style benefits through an assigned carrier. This runs as a separate track from the litigation.
- [[uim-claim-management]]: If the client has UIM coverage, a UIM claim provides another recovery path independent of the BI coverage dispute.

### Cascade Effect
In observed cases:
1. BI denial (Day 0) -> Attorney challenge (Weeks 1-8) -> Coverage still under investigation (Month 3-6)
2. KAC claim filed (Month 1) -> Assigned carrier manages PIP benefits (ongoing)
3. Medicaid/health insurance lien grows as treatment continues (ongoing)
4. Complaint filed to preserve SOL (Month 13)
5. Defense counsel extension granted to resolve coverage (Month 15)
6. EUO of defendant (Month 18+)
7. Full litigation (discovery, depositions) while coverage still disputed (Year 2-4+)

The result: cases that should resolve in 12-18 months instead take 4+ years. Client frustration compounds as bills go to collections, Medicaid liens grow, and the case shows no visible progress.

## Evidence

- In Case H, the BI carrier (Allstate) denied coverage due to a stolen vehicle claim. The firm challenged the denial with evidence that the theft report was filed after the accident. Despite this, coverage remained "under investigation" for 6+ months. The firm filed a complaint (22-CI-002878) at 13 months. A KAC claim was set up through Nationwide for PIP benefits. Defense counsel was granted a 60-day extension while attempting to resolve coverage. An EUO of the defendant was conducted. The case remained in active litigation 4+ years later. The Conduent (Medicaid) lien reached $20,669.08.
- In Case G, while not a coverage denial per se, the at-fault driver's BI policy limits were insufficient, requiring a UIM claim against the client's own carrier (Allstate). The UIM complaint was filed in Fayette Circuit Court (24-CI-03580) approximately 14 months after the accident. This demonstrates the alternative-recovery-path pattern: when BI is insufficient (or denied), the firm must pursue the client's own carrier through UIM litigation.

## Agent Guidance

1. When BI coverage is denied, immediately initiate three parallel workstreams:
   - Litigation track (complaint preparation, SOL monitoring)
   - KAC/alternative PIP track (file KAC application, track assigned carrier)
   - UIM evaluation (check client's own UIM coverage limits)
2. Track all three workstreams independently with separate follow-up schedules
3. Set client expectations for a 3-5+ year timeline
4. Monitor Medicaid/health insurance lien growth throughout — these liens compound over time
5. Brief the attorney on the full picture: BI denial + KAC status + UIM potential + lien exposure


- In a file-setup case (DOI 10/2025), coverage was denied for both vehicles after investigation by Liberty Mutual. Unlike the stolen-vehicle case that led to litigation, this case had no treatment and no PIP — making litigation impractical. The coverage denial here leads to case decline rather than forced litigation, representing an alternate outcome path: when coverage denial meets zero treatment, the case simply dies rather than entering forced litigation.
