# Disputed Liability Stalls Demand Phase

> When both insurance carriers deny their insured's fault in an accident, the standard demand workflow stalls. The firm cannot send a demand because neither carrier will accept liability. The paralegal is left without a clear path forward and may avoid client communication because there is no substantive update to provide. This creates a cascading effect: the case sits in demand phase, the client becomes frustrated, and the paralegal repeatedly escalates to the attorney without resolution.

## Pattern

The disputed-liability demand stalemate follows a predictable sequence:

1. **Demand phase entry**: Treatment is complete and records are collected
2. **Carrier denial**: The BI carrier denies fault, claiming their insured had right-of-way or control of the intersection
3. **No arbitration**: The carriers have not initiated inter-carrier arbitration to resolve the liability dispute
4. **Paralegal uncertainty**: The paralegal does not know how to advise the client and escalates to the attorney ("Not sure what to tell the guy. Any ideas?")
5. **Attorney non-response or delay**: The attorney may not immediately respond, leaving the case in limbo
6. **Client frustration**: The client calls for updates but the firm has nothing to report
7. **Strategy-driven avoidance**: The firm may avoid proactive client contact because there is no good news to deliver

The resolution typically requires an attorney decision: file suit to force the liability issue, or attempt alternative dispute resolution.

## Evidence

- In an MVA case (DOI 9/2025), both BI carriers denied fault. The paralegal contacted one carrier and confirmed "no arbitration has been initiated." The paralegal then emailed the attorney: "Not sure what to do here, both sides blaming the other and not accepting any liability. No arbitration has been done etc." The attorney's response was not documented. Two weeks later, the paralegal escalated again: "State Farm still denying liability claiming their insured had control of the intersection. Stating point of impact confirms this." The paralegal then asked the attorney again: "Not sure what to tell the guy. Any ideas?" — indicating the case had been in this stalemate for weeks with no resolution strategy communicated to the paralegal.
- In previously documented cases, disputed liability at 25% acceptance forced immediate litigation. The key distinction in this new pattern is that the case has NOT yet moved to litigation — the stalemate is occurring during the demand phase, before any complaint is filed.

## Agent Guidance

1. When both carriers deny liability, immediately escalate to the attorney with a clear summary of each carrier's position
2. Do not let the case sit in limbo — request an attorney decision within 1 week: file suit, attempt mediation, or pursue inter-carrier arbitration
3. Communicate honestly with the client: "Both insurance companies are disputing who caused the accident. The attorney is evaluating our options, which may include filing a lawsuit."
4. Document the carrier's specific liability position (e.g., "claiming their insured had control of the intersection")
5. Check whether arbitration has been initiated between carriers — if not, ask the attorney whether to request it
6. Track SOL carefully — disputed liability cases can sit in this stalemate until SOL pressure forces action

## Pitfalls

- Allowing the case to sit in demand phase for months without attorney direction
- Avoiding client contact because there is no update — this compounds frustration
- Not documenting the specific liability positions of each carrier
- Assuming the carriers will resolve the dispute themselves without firm intervention
