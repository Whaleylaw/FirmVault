# Filing Litigation as Negotiation Leverage

> This connection documents how [[case-phase-transitions]] from Negotiation to Litigation is not simply a failure of negotiation but a deliberate strategic choice that interacts with [[statute-of-limitations-monitoring]], [[attorney-review-triggers]], and [[multi-carrier-multi-defendant-cases]] to create leverage for better outcomes.

## Connection

Three concepts converge when litigation is used as leverage:

1. **Low Initial Offer** ([[attorney-review-triggers]]): The BI carrier makes an initial offer that values only a fraction of the treatment (e.g., considering only the ER visit while ignoring extensive follow-up care). The attorney evaluates the offer and determines it is inadequate.

2. **Pre-emptive Filing** ([[statute-of-limitations-monitoring]] + [[case-phase-transitions]]): Rather than continuing to negotiate from a weak position, the attorney files suit well before the SOL deadline. This signals to the carrier that the firm is serious and the case will not be resolved cheaply.

3. **Parallel Negotiation** ([[multi-carrier-multi-defendant-cases]]): When multiple carriers are involved, filing suit against one defendant can create leverage with the others. The non-litigating carriers see active litigation and may become more motivated to settle to avoid being drawn in.

## Evidence

- In Case D, West Bend's first BI offer was ~$13K, considering only the initial ER visit despite the client having 20+ providers and extensive treatment for concussion, spine, and cervical injuries. The attorney filed suit in Jefferson Circuit Court approximately 1 year post-accident. The litigation then drove discovery, depositions, and defense IMEs over the next 2 years. Meanwhile, the Progressive adjuster (a different carrier on the same case) began proactively calling to discuss settlement. The adjuster noted in a voicemail that they wanted to "talk this one over" — suggesting the existence of active litigation against a co-defendant may have motivated engagement.

## Insight

Filing suit is not a last resort — it is a strategic tool. In cases with multiple defendants, it can unlock movement on otherwise stalled negotiations. The cost of litigation (court filing fees, court reporters, preparation time) is an investment in leverage. Cases where the initial offer is dramatically below the treatment value are strong candidates for this strategy.
