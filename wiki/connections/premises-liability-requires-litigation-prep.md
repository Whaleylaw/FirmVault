# Premises Liability Cases Require Litigation Preparation from Day One

> Premises liability cases are significantly more likely to proceed to litigation than auto accident cases. This means that from the earliest stages of file setup, the team should prepare for litigation: requesting certified (not standard) medical records, budgeting for expert evaluations, and preserving evidence from the property owner. The failure to prepare for litigation at intake creates costly delays when the case inevitably moves to suit.

## Connection

**[[premises-liability-case-patterns]]** establishes that PL cases have a different trajectory than auto accident cases, with a much higher likelihood of proceeding to litigation against property owners with dedicated defense counsel.

**[[litigation-process-patterns]]** documents the specific requirements of litigation: certified records, expert reports, discovery compliance, and court deadlines.

**[[medical-records-collection]]** shows that litigation requires certified copies of records — requesting standard copies initially and then having to re-request certified copies wastes months.

**[[expert-reports-and-evaluations]]** shows that FCE, IME, and vocational reports are commonly needed in PL litigation, and their scheduling and coordination adds months to the case timeline.

## Insight

In the observed PL case, the case spanned 6+ years from incident to present, involving complaint filing, discovery, depositions, mediation, and multiple expert evaluations. The team had to re-request records as certified copies for litigation and coordinate multiple expert evaluations that were rescheduled repeatedly. Had the team anticipated litigation from the start, certified records could have been requested initially, and expert evaluations could have been scheduled earlier in the process.

In Case N (premises liability estate, fall at hospital, DOI 7/2020), the case confirmed this pattern: the litigation spanned 5+ years with full discovery (interrogatories, RFAs, document production). Records from a home health provider were misidentified at intake (wrong provider name), causing wasted effort. Bills for the home health provider remained outstanding 3+ years into litigation. An expert consultant was retained for case evaluation. The case was even referred to specialist litigation counsel at one point. Had the team prepared for litigation from day one — requesting certified records, correctly identifying all providers, and engaging expert consultants earlier — the 5+ year timeline could potentially have been compressed.

## Agent Guidance

1. For all premises liability cases, request CERTIFIED records from the start (even before litigation is filed)
2. Send preservation letters to property owners at intake (incident reports, surveillance footage, maintenance logs)
3. Budget for expert evaluations (FCE, IME, economist) at file setup, not after litigation is filed
4. Track litigation-specific deadlines from the moment a complaint is filed
5. Coordinate expert scheduling early — availability is limited and rescheduling is common
