# Records Completion Gates Demand Preparation

> This connection documents how delays in [[medical-records-collection]] create the primary bottleneck preventing [[case-phase-transitions]] from Treatment/Demand in Progress to Negotiation. The demand cannot be sent until records are complete, and records delays cascade into client frustration.

## Connection

The dependency chain:

1. **Treatment Completion**: Client finishes treatment (or decides to stop)
2. **Records Requests**: Firm sends records/billing requests to all providers
3. **Wait Period**: Providers take 1-6+ weeks to respond
4. **Completeness Check**: All providers must have returned both records AND bills
5. **Demand Preparation**: Only after completeness can the demand be drafted
6. **Attorney Review**: Attorney reviews and approves demand
7. **Demand Sent**: Case moves to Negotiation

Any delay at step 3 delays everything downstream. And the client experiences the entire wait as "nothing is happening."

## Evidence

- In Case B, treatment was confirmed complete in late October 2025. Records from St. Elizabeth came back same day, but records from Christ Hospital Physicians took significantly longer. The case was still in "Demand in Progress" as of March 2026 — nearly 5 months after treatment ended — partly due to records delays.
- In Case C, the attorney flagged that "we need a record and was sent to get this a month ago with no action" — records delays directly prevented negotiation progress.
- In Case B, the paralegal told the client's husband: "I'm still waiting for the complete records/billing from Christ Hospital Physicians Primary Care. Once I have both of those I can move this case towards resolution." This directly connects records delays to case resolution timeline.

## Insight

Records collection is the single biggest controllable bottleneck in case lifecycle. Reducing the average records turnaround from 4-6 weeks to 2 weeks would compress the overall case timeline by months.

## Agent Guidance

1. Send records requests the same day treatment is confirmed complete (or earlier if possible)
2. Use the fastest channel available for each provider (email for some, fax for others)
3. Set aggressive follow-up intervals: 7 days for the first follow-up, then every 7 days
4. Maintain a per-case tracker showing each provider's records status and bill status
5. When the attorney asks about case status, lead with the records tracker
6. When the client asks about timeline, explain which specific records are outstanding
