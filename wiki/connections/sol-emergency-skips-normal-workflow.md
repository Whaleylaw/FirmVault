# SOL Emergency Filing Skips Normal Case Workflow

> When a case is referred to the firm with the statute of limitations expiring imminently (within 24-48 hours), the normal case workflow (onboarding -> file setup -> treatment -> demand -> negotiation) is entirely bypassed. The firm must draft and file a complaint on the same day as intake, moving the case directly from onboarding to litigation. This creates a case that enters the system "backwards" — litigation is underway before records, providers, or even basic client information has been fully gathered.

## Pattern

### How It Occurs
1. A referring attorney (often out-of-state) contacts the firm about a client whose SOL is about to expire
2. The prior attorney may have handled a related claim (e.g., workers' compensation) but not the third-party BI claim
3. The firm must file suit immediately — often the same day — to preserve the claim
4. A complaint is drafted and e-filed before any records review, demand preparation, or even a complete intake

### Consequences
- The case enters the system in Litigation phase with incomplete client information
- Records collection happens after litigation has begun, not before
- Discovery requests from the defense may arrive before the firm has its own records
- The case requires retroactive file setup — gathering records, bills, and provider information while simultaneously managing litigation deadlines
- Settlement conferences may occur before the firm has fully developed the case value

## Evidence

- In a WC+BI case (DOI 6/2021, KY), the firm received a referral from a Tennessee attorney who had resolved the workers' compensation portion but not the third-party BI claim. The managing attorney emailed: "New client must file suit by tomorrow." A complaint was drafted and e-filed in Henry Circuit Court the same day. The case moved directly to Litigation phase. Records collection began months later. An IME was scheduled over a year after filing. A telephonic settlement conference occurred approximately 2 years into litigation. The case illustrates how emergency filing creates a long retroactive development process — the firm spent years building the case that should have been built before filing.

## Agent Guidance

1. When a SOL-emergency referral arrives, the attorney must immediately verify: (a) the SOL date, (b) the correct jurisdiction, (c) the correct defendant names
2. File the complaint first — everything else can wait
3. After filing, immediately begin the retroactive intake: client contact, insurance claims, medical records requests
4. Flag the case internally as "filed pre-development" so all staff know records/demand were not done pre-filing
5. Prioritize records collection — defense counsel will request them in discovery

## Pitfalls

- Misspelling defendant names in the complaint (no time for research)
- Filing in the wrong jurisdiction
- Assuming the referring attorney's file is complete — it often lacks records relevant to the BI claim
- Not adjusting the case timeline expectations for staff (this case will develop slowly because everything is retroactive)
